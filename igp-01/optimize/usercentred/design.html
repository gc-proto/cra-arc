Create a prototype

What a prototype is

A prototype is a copy of an existing web page(s) that is used to test design solutions (updates and changes), before the final layout and content are determined.

For example, existing web pages on Canada.ca could be duplicated, becoming a prototype of their original version. If content didn’t exist, a mock-up of what the page could look like is also considered a prototype.

The purpose of prototyping

The problems identified during baseline testing (such as content and navigation issues), are addressed in the prototype using design solutions.

An example of a design solution could be moving important content from the bottom to the top of a page, so users can find it easily.

Benefits of prototyping

Provides a visual reference

A prototype provides a visual reference that can be shown to the project team and stakeholders in order to gather their feedback before the web page goes live on Canada.ca. This makes the process of gathering feedback easier because the stakeholders are able to see the proposed design recommendations.

Creates a more efficient approval process

Prototyping makes implementing design changes simpler than if a product is launched and changes are required after the fact. When a prototype is developed, stakeholder feedback and approvals are received after each iteration and throughout the entire design process.

Several iterations or versions of a prototype may be developed throughout the design process.

Considerations when prototyping

Based on evidence gathered from user testing, collected data, and user experience best practices, the project team will have the information required to move forward with a successful re-design.

When creating a prototype, the following are key elements:

Information Architecture/Navigation

  • Prototype new page layouts based on a clear hierarchy of information:
    • the most important information should be at the top
    • add details and tasks in order of importance
    • place supporting and other relevant details toward the end of the text
    • ensure landing pages give a clear overview of the type of information that can be found in that section
  • Organize information into categories or groups, in a way that makes sense to the user
  • Ensure major sections and key information areas of the pages are easy to find and accessible

Content

  • Use plain language, so information is readable for everyone. For example:
    • include terms that users are familiar with
    • avoid large amounts of information and long sentences
  • Reduce the quantity of content to the minimum required to complete whatever goal users have
  • Ensure main content caters to the larger audience of users
  • After the main content is presented, provide access to more specialized content to those with specific needs

Validation usability testing after re-design

Validation testing uses:

  • the newly designed prototype
  • the same testing method as the baseline testing to ensure to the results of both tests can be easily compared

Validation testing is conducted to determine if the design solutions implemented in the prototype were successful in improving the user’s experience.

To determine if KPIs are met, the following data from the baseline test (taken from the UX scorecard) is compared with the data from the validation test:

  • task success rate
  • time on task
  • ease of use
  • System Usability Scale (SUS)

If KPIs are not met, the new or remaining issues are identified by gathering evidence from user testing. A new iteration or version of the prototype is then developed to address the issues and is re-tested until KPIs are met.

Create a highlight reel video

A highlight reel video that compares the baseline test, showcasing pain points of users or key quotes while navigating the pages, with the validation test to present improvements made based on testing.
