  • Line 48400 - Refund

You will get a refund if you paid more income tax than you owe. The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) may give you a refund payment for the amount of tax you overpaid.

On this page:

  • Receiving your refund
  • Check the status of your refund
  • If your refund is not what you expected

Receiving your refund

The method you used to file your tax return determines how long it takes the CRA to process your refund. The timelines apply only to returns that are received on or before the filing due date.

If changes are made to your tax return and you are getting a refund after a reassessment, you should receive it within the processing time.

Processing time

  • Electronic filing

    Processing time: Within 2 weeks

  • Paper filing by mail

    What type of return?

    • T1 income tax and benefit return
    • T1 adjustment request

    Result

    T1 income tax and benefit return – paper

    T1 adjustment requests – paper

    Our service standard is to issue your notice of assessment within 12 weeks of receiving your return and any required supporting documents.

    Our current timeline to issue your notice of reassessment or a letter of explanation is within 13 weeks of receiving your paper or telephone request to change your individual income tax return.

    The Canada Revenue Agency is experiencing delays in processing T1 adjustment requests due to higher than normal intake.

    For faster service you can submit your requests for changes electronically, using Change My Return in My Account or ReFILE.

    The processing time is a targeted time. It applies to straightforward taxpayer-requested adjustments only. It does not apply to complex requests. Complex requests include situations where more information or additional verification is required. Our current timeline to issue your notice of reassessment or a letter of explanation is within 45 weeks of receiving your complex adjustment request. This excludes requests filed for international and non-resident individuals, and emigrants.

    Last updated on: 2026-04-01 | Updated monthly

  • Non-resident or international filers

    Processing time: Within 16 weeks

Certain situations may take longer to process

It may take longer to process a tax refund if:

  • CRA completes a pre-assessment review of your tax return
  • Your tax filing situation includes:
    • Multiple tax returns
    • A bankruptcy return
    • A deceased taxpayer return
    • A request from the CRA to you or your authorized representative for more information or documents

How you will receive your refund

You will receive your tax refund based on your chosen preferences. If you have not received your refund within the processing time, you can check the status.

Direct deposit

We will deposit your refund directly into your bank account.

The CRA will use the banking information we have on file at the time you sent us your return.

If you need to set up or update your direct deposit

If your direct deposit is not set up before filing your tax return, the CRA will send your refund by cheque.

If you do not have direct deposit set up with the CRA, or need to update your banking information for future payments, refer to set up or update your direct deposit.


Cheque

We will send your refund by mail to the address the CRA has on file.

Update your personal information to ensure you get your cheque

If your situation changes, you should notify the CRA to make sure you get your payments.

For example, if you move and do not update the CRA about your address change, your refund cheque may be returned to the CRA. If returned, any future payments will be calculated but will not be issued until your address is updated.

Refer to: Update your personal information

If you need a replacement cheque

You can request a replacement cheque if it has not been cashed and is:

  • Damaged
  • Lost
  • Stolen
  • Missing

Refer to: Uncashed cheques from the CRA

Transferring your refund to an instalment account

If you chose this option, the CRA will transfer the full amount of your refund to your 2025 instalment account. The option to transfer your refund is only available at the time you file your return.

Your refund amount will be applied toward any tax owing and will be considered received on the date your return is assessed. You can confirm if your instalment payment was received.

Check the status of your refund

To find out if you are getting a refund, the CRA must have completed the assessment of your tax return. You can check the status of your tax return either online or by phone.


Online

The fastest way to check the status of your return and refund.

CRA account

Use your CRA Account to check the status of:

  • Your return using the "Progress tracker"
  • Your refund
  • CRA sign in
  • Register

By phone

Call about your refund status

Information you will need before you call

  • Your social insurance number
  • Your full name
  • Your date of birth
  • Your complete address
  • Depending on the which option you choose you with need either:
    • For live agent:
      An assessed return, notice of assessment or reassessment, other tax document, or to be signed in to My Account
    • For automated service:
      Amount from line 15000 of your most recent notice of assessment
Calling on behalf of someone else

You can only call on behalf of someone else in the following 2 situations:

  • The person is present on the call to verify their identity in English or French and can provide verbal authorization for you to speak for them

  • You are the person's legal representative with full legal authority to speak for them

    For details: Authorize a representative

If your refund is not what you expected

If you think your refund amount is incorrect, you can review the tax assessment or reassessment section of your notice of assessment (NOA) or notice of reassessment (NOR).

Refer to: Notices of assessment

Why you may not receive a refund

Generally, you will not receive a refund if your refund amount is $2 or less.

If you have another government debt, the CRA may have kept all or part of your refund to put towards the debt.

Other government debts your refund may be applied to

The CRA may keep all or part of your refund if you have:

  • An amount owing (or pending amount owing)
  • A garnishment order under the Family Orders and Agreements Enforcement Assistance Act
  • Certain other outstanding federal, provincial or territorial government debts, such as student loans, employment insurance (EI) and social assistance benefit overpayments, immigration loans or training allowance overpayments
  • Any outstanding GST/HST returns from a sole proprietorship or partnership
  • Any outstanding payroll returns or remittances from a sole proprietorship or partnership
  • Any outstanding GST/HST or payroll debt from a sole proprietorship or partnership

For more information, refer to how payments are applied to offset debt.

When the CRA pays you interest on your refund

If you overpaid your taxes, the CRA may pay you compound daily interest on your 2024 tax refund.

The calculation will start on the latest of the following 3 dates:

  • May 30, 2025
  • The 30th day after you file your return
  • The day you overpaid your taxes

For more information, refer to prescribed interest rates.
