Fundamental requirements of SR&ED work

Eligible work must meet certain fundamental requirements to be considered scientific research and experimental development.

Your reason for doing the work (why you started it) and the way in which you approach the work (how you carry it out) will help determine if the work is in fact SR&ED.

Eligible SR&ED work must include the 3 following items:

  • A scientific or technological uncertainty that you tried to overcome
  • A systematic investigation or search that you carried out to overcome the uncertainty
  • An advancement that you achieved or tried to achieve

Review the legal definition of SR&ED: SR&ED is defined in the Income Tax Act

The uncertainty

SR&ED work must be based on a scientific or technological uncertainty that you are trying to overcome.

The uncertainty may exist because of a current gap or limitation in the scientific or technological knowledge available at the time. It is the reason for starting the SR&ED work.

You must be able to explain the gap in the existing knowledge when you started the work, the uncertainty, and your attempt to resolve the uncertainty.  

The investigation or [re]search

The work to overcome the uncertainty must be carried out through experiment and analysis. It is not sufficient to simply use known design methods or established techniques.

The work must be a systematic investigation or search.

You must be able to explain the work that was done and describe how you gained the knowledge you needed to overcome the uncertainty.

What a systematic investigation includes

The systematic investigation or search required by the SR&ED tax incentive program is more than simply having a systematic approach to your work. It must include all the following:

A hypothesis
The idea that triggers your investigation to overcome the uncertainty (it may be a possible solution to a problem, a proposed method, or an approach)
An experiment
The experiment or analysis conducted to test your hypothesis, which may change or evolve as a result of the testing
Results obtained
The results or findings of the experiment
Conclusions
The logical conclusions that are based on the results obtained

The advancement

The advancement is the increase in knowledge that your SR&ED work achieved. It’s how the work improved the scientific or technological knowledge base that existed when you started the work.

Keep in mind that it’s the advancement in the underlying science or technology that is important, not how the work advanced your business or business practices.

You do not have to achieve your objective to gain new knowledge.

For example, if your work allowed you to understand that the idea you tested is not a solution for your situation, this can be considered new knowledge.

Eligible research and development work

Work that is eligible for SR&ED tax incentives must be in the fields of science or technology and must be carried out in Canada. Eligible types of research and development work include the following:

Basic research

Basic research is work that aims to increase the scientific or technological knowledge base. It often takes place in laboratories and may be published in scientific journals.

Example of basic research

An example of basic research is the study of a newly discovered virus to better understand its characteristics.

Applied research

Applied research is also work that aims to increase scientific or technological knowledge, but it has a specific practical application in view. Like basic research, the work often takes place in laboratories and may be published in scientific journals.

Example of applied research

An example of applied research would be efforts to develop a vaccine against a new virus by exploiting some identified characteristics of that newly discovered virus.

Experimental development

Experimental development is work that aims to increase scientific or technological knowledge from research and more practical experience. The work attempts to develop new or improved materials, devices, products, or processes.

Example of experimental development

An example of when experimental development could occur might be during the scale up and optimization of processes to enable large scale and rapid production of a vaccine.

Eligible support work

Support work is not SR&ED, but it may be eligible if it corresponds and directly supports the SR&ED work you’re doing.

Types of eligible support work:

  • engineering
  • design
  • operations research
  • mathematical analysis
  • computer programming
  • data collection
  • testing
  • psychological research
Example of support work

For example, if you are developing a vaccine, you may perform tests on the virus to observe its characteristics. This testing would be considered support work of your basic research.

Work that is not eligible

Some work is specifically excluded from being eligible for SR&ED tax incentives. Any work that is not conducted specifically to overcome the uncertainty is not eligible.

Review types of excluded work

The following work is not eligible or considered support work:

  • Market research or sales promotion
  • Quality control or routine testing of materials, devices, products or processes
  • Research in the social sciences or the humanities
  • Prospecting, exploring or drilling for, or producing, minerals, petroleum or natural gas
  • The commercial production of a new or improved material, device or product or the commercial use of a new or improved process
  • Style changes
  • Routine data collection
  • Acquiring available knowledge or know-how (for example, through training)
  • On-the-job learning, hiring expert employees or consultants, or purchasing proprietary knowledge

If you receive other R&D tax credits

Receiving R&D tax credits through your province or territory does not exclude you from claiming SR&ED tax incentives. However, it may reduce the amount you can claim.

You must report any financial assistance you receive in your SR&ED expenditures claim.
