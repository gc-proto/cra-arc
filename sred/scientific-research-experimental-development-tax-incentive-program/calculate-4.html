Use your ITC to reduce income

You may earn an investment tax credit (ITC) on your qualified SR&ED expenditures for the year. This may reduce your income tax payable for the year. If you have excess ITCs in the year, you may also get a refund.

Who can earn an ITC

The following entities may earn a ITC on their qualified SR&ED expenditures:

  • Corporations
  • Individuals
  • Beneficiary of a trust
  • Member of a partnership
Partnerships cannot earn an ITC

Since a partnership is not a taxpayer, it cannot earn an ITC. In general, the ITC is calculated at the partnership level and then allocated to eligible members of the partnership (individuals, corporations or trusts).

If you are submitting a partnership claim for SR&ED, refer to:
SR&ED Claims for Partnerships Policy

Complete the ITC form to calculate your amount

To calculate your ITC amount, you must complete the investment tax credit forms for either corporations or individuals.

For corporations

To calculate a SR&ED ITC for corporations, use:
Form T2SCH31, Investment Tax Credit - Corporations

If you are an associated corporation, you will also need to complete:
Schedule 49, Agreement Among Associated CCPCs to Allocate the Expenditure Limit.

For individuals

To calculate a SR&ED ITC for individuals and beneficiaries of trusts, use:
Form T2038-IND, Investment Tax Credit (Individuals)

Understand ITC rates

Basic rate

The basic ITC rate is 15% on qualified SR&ED expenditures for individuals, beneficiaries of trusts, members of a partnership, or corporations that are not Canadian controlled private corporations (CCPCs).

Enhanced rate

Most Canadian controlled private corporations (CCPCs) may earn an ITC on qualified SR&ED expenditures at the enhanced rate of 35%, up to a maximum threshold of $3 million. This is called the expenditure limit.

Any qualified expenditures beyond the expenditure limit earn an ITC at the basic rate of 15%.

If a CCPC is a qualifying corporation, 40% of the ITC earned at 15% can be refunded.

What qualifying corporation means

A qualifying corporation is a Canadian controlled private corporation (CCPC) whose taxable income (or total taxable income if associated with another corporation) in the previous year was not more than their qualifying income limit for the current year.

For more information, review policy: SR&ED ITC Policy, Qualifying corporation

Determine expenditure limit

Some CCPCs may have a reduced expenditure limit that is lower than $3 million.

The limit starts to decrease when the CCPC’s taxable capital employed in Canada for the previous tax year reaches $10 million. The limit becomes zero starting at $50 million.

Other factors can also reduce the ITC expenditure limit for CCPCs, including short tax years and having multiple tax years within one calendar year.

For more information on the expenditure limits, review the policy: SR&ED ITC Policy, Determining the expenditure limit

Expenditure limit for associated corporations

If your corporation is associated with one or more CCPCs, you must allocate the expenditure limit among the associated CCPCs.

All the associated corporations in the group must file the T2 Schedule 49 that allocates the expenditure limit among them or the expenditure limit is zero.

What an associated corporation means

The CRA considers 2 or more corporations to be associated for SR&ED if:

  • they are associated with the same third corporation
  • the main reason for the separate existence is to reduce income tax payable or increase the amount of refundable ITCs  

For SR&ED, the CRA does not consider a corporation associated if the only reason they are associated is because 2 or more investors have shares in each corporation, provided that: (as long as)

  • The corporations aren’t associated for any other reason
  • There is at least one shareholder of one corporation who is not a shareholder of the other corporation -- and it’s not for the purpose of satisfying these provisions

Refer to: SR&ED ITC Policy, Associated corporations

Some associated CCPCs may also have a reduced expenditure limit.

The limit starts to decrease when the taxable capital employed in Canada of the CCPC and its associated corporations for their last tax years ending in the preceding calendar year reaches $10 million. It becomes zero starting at $50 million.

The allocated ITC for associated corporations can be earned at the enhanced rate of 35%.

If there is anything left above that, it may be allocated to an associated corporation. However, the amount must not exceed the expenditure limit of the associated group for the applicable tax year.

Carry back or carry forward an ITC

If you have an unused ITC beyond the amount needed to reduce your income tax payable to zero, you may carry it back 3 years or carry it forward 20 years. This lets you use the credit to reduce your tax payable for other years.

A refunded ITC will reduce the credit available for carry back or forward.

Review the policy: SR&ED ITC Policy, Carrying back ITCs

Refundable ITC

Claimants may be able to have some or all of their SR&ED ITC refunded. If the ITC isn’t applied in the year to reduce income tax payable and is not carried back, it may be refundable.

Most CCPCs can earn a refundable ITC at the enhanced rate of 35% on qualified expenditures up to their expenditure limit for the tax year. Individuals may earn a refundable ITC at 15%. Generally an ITC can only be refunded in the year that the qualified expenditure occurred.

The refund rate varies depending on if the claimant is a corporation, an individual, a beneficiary of a trust, or a member of a partnership.
