Link eligible work to expenditures

To claim SR&ED tax incentives, you need to calculate how much you spent on your SR&ED project during the tax year. This means linking your eligible work to the expenditures you can claim.
Expenditures you can claim include:

  • salary or wages
  • materials
  • contracts for SR&ED
  • overhead and other expenditures
  • third party payments

You may only claim expenditures for the year in which they occurred.

Select the traditional or proxy method

To calculate your expenditures you may use either the traditional method or the proxy method. Each method has its own advantages and disadvantages. You must decide what works best for your business.

Select one method for the tax year you’re claiming. You cannot change the method  after you submit your claim form.

If you don’t select a method, the CRA will process your claim using the traditional method.

Using the traditional method

The traditional method is the default approach to calculating SR&ED expenditures. It requires y ou to specifically identify all overhead and other expenditures incurred for SR&ED work conducted in the tax year.

Pros and cons of the traditional method

Advantages of the traditional method

  • Overhead expenditures can be included in the pool of deductible SR&ED expenditures
  • May result in actual overhead and other expenditures incurred being greater than the PPA
  • Do not need to calculate the salary base and determine the PPA in Part 5 of form T661
  • Do not need to calculate the overall cap

Disadvantages of the traditional method

  • Must show that the overhead and other expenditures are directly related and incremental to the SR&ED
  • Can be complex in certain situations, such as when SR&ED and non-SR&ED work are done in the same facility (for example, shop-floor SR&ED)
  • Must identify (track) and allocate which overhead and other expenditures are for the SR&ED work
  • Must explain how you determined the amount and provide support for the determination
  • The PPA may be greater than the actual overhead and other expenditures.

Using the proxy method

If you choose to use the proxy method, you do not need to specifically identify and claim SR&ED overhead and other expenditures for the tax year. Instead you calculate a substitute amount called the prescribed proxy amount (PPA).

This affects how you report overhead costs for:

  • salary or wages for support or clerical staff
  • office supplies
  • utilities
Pros and cons of the proxy method

Advantages of the proxy method

  • No need to specifically identify (track) and allocate overhead and other expenditures for SR&ED
  • Investment tax credit is earned on the PPA
  • Easy to determine the PPA once you establish the salary base (PPA is calculated in Part 5 of T661)
  • Actual overhead and other expenditures incurred may be less than the PPA

Disadvantages of the proxy method

  • The PPA or any expenditures it represents cannot be included in the pool of deductible SR&ED expenditures
  • The salary base must be calculated
  • Overall cap on the PPA may need to be calculated
  • the PPA may be less than the actual overhead and other expenditures incurred

Salary or wages

This expenditure includes the portion of salary or wages and taxable benefits that you incurred in the tax year (or within 180 days of the tax year-end) for SR&ED done in Canada.

You may include the salary or wages of employees who are directly engaged in SR&ED and  are primarily employed because of SR&ED.

The portion of salary or wages you include must be based on the time employees spent on SR&ED activities and the tasks they performed, regardless of their job tile.

You may also include the salary or wages of specified employees who are directly engaged in the SRED work. This is an employee who does not deal at arm’s length with the employer.

Review salary or wages of employees directly involved in SR&ED: T4088 - Guide to T661, Salary or wages

For help determining salary or wages for various tasks

Tasks and duties of employees directly involved in SR&ED whose salaries and wages you can include:

  1. Experimentation and analysis
  2. Support work of engineering, design, operations research, mathematical analysis, computer programming, data collection, testing, or psychological research.
  3. Certain tasks performed by non-specialized employees, such as:
    1. operating a machine that is required for an experiment
    2. feeding raw materials into a machine

      (This work must be supervised by staff with scientific or technological qualifications.)

  4. Supervision of employees performing SR&ED and SR&ED contract administration (technical input only)
  5. Technological planning for ongoing SR&ED projects you claimed in the tax year, such as planning for:
    • assignment of technological personnel
    • job priorities
    • development of technological strategies
    • quality of materials used.
  6. Technological feasibility studies relating to SR&ED projects carried out.
  7. Technical documentation for internal use

Salary and wages outside Canada

In limited cases, you may claim a portion of the salary or wages of employees for SR&ED work done outside Canada. This portion is called the permissible salary.

Criteria for claiming work outside Canada

Salary or wages you can claim for SR&ED done outside Canada must meet all the following criteria:

  • The SR&ED work must be done directly by an employee and not a contractor
  • The employee who did the work was a resident of Canada at the time the expense was incurred
  • The work was related to a business of the claimant
  • The work done outside Canada was an integral part and solely in support of the SR&ED project carried out in Canada
  • The salary or wages paid must not be subject to income or profits tax from another country
To determine the permissible salary for work outside Canada

For employees who are not specified employees

Enter the permissible salary for work outside Canada is the lesser of the following 2 amounts:

  • The total of salary or wages for SR&ED done outside Canada
  • 10% of the total of SR&ED salary or wages for SR&ED done in Canada (line 306 of claim)

For specified employees outside Canada

Enter the permissible salary for SR&ED done outside Canada for specified employees (line 307 of claim), however:

  • do not include bonuses or remuneration based on profits
  • the maximum amount of salary or wages is limited to 5 times the year's maximum pensionable earnings (YMPE)

Materials

Materials for SR&ED include all the raw materials, substances, or other items that compose the body of a thing at a given moment in the SR&ED process.

Expenditures may include both the cost of materials consumed or transformed during SR&ED work.

If you purchase materials that you will use later in SR&ED, you may only claim their cost in the year that the materials are consumed or transformed.

SR&ED material expenditures may be:

  • invoice costs
  • custom and excise duties
  • transportation
  • other acquisition costs
  • storage costs
What consumed material means

Materials consumed during SR&ED may be materials that were destroyed or made valueless as a result of the work.

They do not include:

  • cleaning supplies, computer discs, filter papers, lubricants, solvents, acids, and test tubes (these are considered overhead expenditures)
  • measuring appliances
What transformed material means

Materials transformed during SR&ED may be materials that have been incorporated into other materials or products that have some value to you or another party.

Contracts for SR&ED work

You may claim the amount you paid or that was payable to contractors or sub-contractors who did SR&ED work in Canada on your behalf.

Only claim the portion of the contract that is SR&ED and do not include GST in your amount.

You must separate your contract expenditures between arm’s length contractors and non-arm’s length contractors.

Arm’s length contracts can be included in the calculation of qualified SR&ED expenditures for ITC purposes.

Non-arm’s length contract can be included in the calculation of deductible expenditures, but they do not qualify for an ITC.

Review the policy: Contract Expenditures for SR&ED Performed on Behalf of a Claimant Policy

Overhead and other expenditures

This refers to expenses that are not directly related to a SR&ED project but are necessary to support the work.

If you chose to use the proxy method, you do not have to calculate overhead and other expenditures. Instead you calculate a prescribed proxy amount.

If you chose to use the traditional method, you must calculate the overhead and other expenditures you incurred carrying out SR&ED in Canada.

Overhead and other expenditures fall into 2 categories:

Expenditures that are a direct result of doing SR&ED

Expenditures that are a direct result of providing premises, facilities, or equipment for doing SR&ED

A direct result of doing SR&ED

Expenditures that are a direct result of doing SR&ED refers to:

  • Costs that directly relate to doing SR&ED
  • Costs that only occur because of doing SR&ED          

This means the expenditure, or a portion of the expenditure, must be directly attributable to doing SR&ED and would not have occurred otherwise.

Any portion of an expenditure that is indirectly related must be excluded.

If the expenditure does not allow the claimant to engage in SR&ED, for example if it’s for something that occurred after the SRED is complete, it is not directly related to doing SR&ED.

There must be a direct link between the expenditure and specific SR&ED work, staff, or equipment.

Review examples of expenditures that may be directly related: SR&ED Overhead and Other Expenditures Policy, Section 3.1.1

To be directly related to doing SR&ED, the expenditure, or a portion it, must also be an incremental expenditure. This means it was only incurred because SR&ED happened.

An expense that increased because of SR&ED, or would have been less if no SR&ED were carried out, is generally considered incremental.

If you chose the traditional method, you must be able to show that the expense only occurred because of SR&ED.

If an expense is not incremental, it is not a SR&ED overhead and other expenditure -- even if the expense is directly related to doing SR&ED.

Review policy: SR&ED Overheard and Other Expenditures Policy, Determining whether an expense may be incremental

A direct result of providing premises, facilities, or equipment for SR&ED

Expenditures that are a direct result of providing premises, facilities, or equipment for doing SR&ED include:

  • Costs of the maintenance and upkeep of premises, facilities, or equipment for doing SR&ED 
  • Other costs, or portion of costs, directly related to that provision that only incurred because of the SR&ED work

Other costs directly related to providing premises, facilities or equipment for doing SR&ED must be incremental.

Examples of directly related expenditures

Examples of directly related expenditures in the case of a building or equipment owned by the claimant:

  • municipal taxes for a building owned by the claimant;
  • insurance for a building owned by the claimant;
  • insurance for equipment owned by the claimant;
  • expenses for safety and security

Overhead and other expenditures outside Canada

You may be able to claim some overhead and other expenditures for work done outside Canada.

Support work done outside Canada

An expenditure for support work done outside Canada may be deductible as an expenditure for SRED done outside Canada.

But it cannot be part of your pool of deductible expenditures or qualified expenditures even if the work supports a Canadian SRED project. Expenditures for SR&ED done outside Canada do not qualify for an ITC.

Work done outside Canada that is not SR&ED

For work done outside Canada that is not SR&ED but is directly related and incremental to doing SR&ED in Canada, the following expenditures may be deducted as if they were incurred in Canada and earn ITC.

  • the acquisition of equipment used in SR&ED in Canada
  • the acquisition of materials used in SR&ED in Canada
  • training hands-on employees for SR&ED done in Canada
  • visits to foreign customers for SR&ED done in Canada to update them on the SR&ED project's status

Some salary or wages for work done outside Canada may be considered expenditures for SRED that is being done in Canada.

Third-party payments

Third-party payments refer to amounts that you pay for SR&ED work that is not directly done by you or on your behalf.

Third-party payments are different from Contracts work for SR&ED in that the work was not done on your behalf and would have been done regardless of the SR&ED project.

Difference s between third-party payments and contract work

Characteristics of third-party payment vs contract expenditures

Number of funders Usually more than one payer Usually limited to one payer
Type of SR&ED Often basic or applied research Often commercially focused

Review: Third-party Payments Policy, Table 10.1

Accepted third-parties

Third-party payments may be to an approved association, university, college, research institute, or other similar organization. Normally, a payment will earn an ITC in the year in which it is paid.

In some cases a payment to a Canadian corporation in Canada may be considered a third-party payment. In this case, the payment will earn an ITC in the year in which the SR&ED/work is conducted, not the year it is paid.

Review the list of approved entities: Appendix A – List of approved entities

Accepted third-party payments

Third-party payments can be direct financial contributions, funding for students or employees doing the SR&ED, or payments in kind.

You may also claim a property or service you provide if it is at fair market value. However, expenditures for capital property do not qualify.

If you pay agricultural check-off dues

Agricultural producers can earn an ITC from contributions they make to agricultural organizations (also known as check-offs) that fund SR&ED.

To qualify as a third-party contribution:

  • The agricultural producer must be a tax payer
  • The organization must have an agent-principle relationship with the producer

Farm producers must file either a Schedule T2SCH31 or Form T2038(IND) to receive an investment tax credit (ITC) on third-party payments (check-offs) made to agricultural organizations.

They do not need to file Form T661 or Form T1263 if they are only claiming check-offs for the tax year.

If you made a third-party payment, you must complete: Form T1263, Third-Party Payments for SR&ED for each payment and attach it to Form T661.
