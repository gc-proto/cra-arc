Overview

Omit background information and messages that welcome people to your program or site. These kinds of messages get in people's way when they're trying to accomplish a task.

Examples:

  • Write: Present your passport to the border officer.
  • Don't write: According to Canadian legislation, you're required to present your passport to the agent who will welcome you into the country.
  • Write: Submit your completed documents.
  • Don't write: Once completed, you may wish to submit your documents.

Cost savings

Focus on the most important information. Make it action oriented. Consider the following example for a quick comparison:

  • Before edit: The IT Self Service Portal is the one-stop shop for all technological and network help a CRA employee could need.  From requesting software to requesting a physical relocation, the IT Self Service Portal has it all!
    36 words x $.25/word = $9 to translate
  • After edit: IT Self Service Portal provides technological services for employees, their access, computer equipment, mobile devices and software. 
    17 words x $.25/word = $4.25 to translate

Extra words carry dollar signs—especially if your company publishes in multiple languages. If you tighten before you translate, you can save staggering amounts of money [at] 25 cents per word per language (a common translation rate).” […] Even if your words never get translated, and even if you don’t care what it costs to produce and maintain unnecessary words, you care about losing readers. Concise means readable. Concise means comprehensible. Concise means efficient.

Marcia Reifer Johnston

Fear makes people hide their meaning with extra words. It backfires. We know you’re afraid. Adding words makes people less likely to trust you.”
