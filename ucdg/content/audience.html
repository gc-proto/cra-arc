Consider the needs of the audience - CRA user-centred design guide

The user can be anyone, which means you must be aware of various audience needs. This awareness will help you develop empathy for users. This is different than sympathy, which only acknowledges the challenges. Empathy allows you to relate to users, so you can create content that solves problems, supports comprehension, and improves task success.

Literacy levels

Research shows people with literacy challenges:

  • spend a lot of time trying to understand words that contain more than 2 syllables
  • skip over large amounts of information when it contains many multi-syllable words, uncommon terms and long sentences
  • find it difficult to understand content written above grade 8 reading level

To support all users:

  • Check that content is easy to understand
  • Create scannable content
  • Get to the point
  • Start with the most important information
  • Write in plain language
  • Write useful page titles and headings

Numeracy levels

Research shows that people prefer numerals rather than numbers written inwords (for example, 5 rather than five).

Numerals help people quickly make sense of information as they:

  • stand out and are easy to notice
  • stop they eye while scanning
  • take less time to read than words

Many people can find it difficult to use government services because of numeracy challenges.

There is a perception that completing a tax return is confusing, difficult, and challenging, particularly for those who are not adept at math, comfortable with numbers, or who do not have English / French as their mother tongue. [...] Indeed, the perceived complexity of the process and low literacy (both general and financial) were by far the most common challenges faced by people who do not file their income tax return regularly.

Canada Revenue Agency
Barriers associated with tax filing in vulnerable populations

To support all users:

  • Make sure mathematical calculations meet level 2 numeracy

Accessibility

Making content accessible means a wide range of people can use it, including people with physical and cognitive disabilities.

The medical model defines disability as a trait; something permanent and limiting. In contrast, an inclusive design approach is one that perceives disability as a mismatch between our needs and the design features of a product, built environment, system or service.

OCAD University
Disability as Mismatch - The Inclusive Design Guide
Some disabilities are well-known

For example:

  • deafness
  • blindness
  • mobility limitations
Some of disabilities are invisible

For example:

  • reading disorders
  • learning disorders like dyslexia
  • attention deficit disorders
  • memory disorders
  • anxiety
  • migraines
  • chronic pain
Some disabilities are temporary

For example:

  • aging
  • an accident or illness
  • life changes such as pregnancy

Many of these people can experience 1 or more disabilities.

The rates of disability are increasing in part due to ageing populations and an increase in chronic health conditions.

World Health Organization (WHO)
Disability and health - WHO

Even people who do not identify as having a disability will experience some form of situational disability, such as:

  • trying to hear something in a loud location
  • trying to find information during a stressful event

    If I’m in the state of mind that I’ve been laid off and not financially secure, I would not necessarily read everything.

    April 6, 2020 usability study of Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB)

Inclusiveness

Be respectful of human diversity across gender, sexuality, social class, culture, age and ability.

Gender inclusive language

People can have different gender identities, expressions and sexual orientations.

Gender-inclusive writing doesn’t mention gender whenever possible, and is respectful of:

  • individuals whose gender is unknown
  • a diverse group of people (so that no member of the group feels excluded)
  • non-binary individuals (individuals who do not identify as a man or a woman)

Devices, screen size and technology use

Not everyone has access to the same tools and technologies across Canada. What people use and what kind of access they have can depend on many factors including age, income and location.

Depending on context of use, people will view your content on different screen sizes.

Phone and tablet screens display less content than desktop and laptop screens. Readers who use small screens:

  • have to go through more steps to access the same amount of information
  • rely on their short-term memory to recall information that isn't visible on the screen
  • may miss important information if it is not presented closer to the top of the screen
Example: UX confusion on a small device
Context
COVID self-assessment tool page had promotional content about an App presented above the actual self-assessment tool.

The perceived call-to-action buttons seem to be the app download

The real call-to-action is the 'Start' button at the bottom of the page

Insights
Person who arrived on this page, though this was a page to download an app and left the page. They thought they could not do the assessment without downloading the app.
Why this is important
Person missed the self-assessment tool and was not able to complete their task.

There is no one-size-fits all solution. You need to understand who is using your information and under what circumstances.

Specialist audience

A specialist could be an accountant, a lawyer, or other industry-specific, education-specific audience.

Research shows that both specialists and general audiences prefer information written in plain language.

Even people with high literacy levels prefer plain language because:

  • they often have many things to read
  • reading simple texts takes less time than the complicated and technical ones

Good communication has strong business value. Organizations with clear writing styles are perceived to possess greater transparency and credibility than companies that don’t.

Nielsen Norman Group
Plain Language Is for Everyone, Even Experts

If you use terms only specialists are familiar with, consider including a simple explanation for general audiences.

You must always include a summary in plain language for:

  • people who aren't familiar with the subject
  • specialists who might be scanning the content to decide whether it's relevant to their task

To support all users:

  • Write in plain language
  • Avoid FAQs

Equivalent content in English and French

You must publish communications and provide services to the public in both official languages, as part of the Official Languages Act.

You must ensure that government content:

  • is professionally translated
  • reflects Canadian writing conventions in English and French
  • includes fully bilingual images, multimedia files and transcripts, or contains equivalent
  • information in both official languages
  • is reviewed and adapted to reflect unique nuances of the other language

If you have questions about:

  • official languages requirements and exceptions - contact the person responsible for official languages in your institution
  • if it's appropriate to communicate with audiences in other languages, in addition to Canada's official languages - contact your communications team
