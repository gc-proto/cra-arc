Labour requirements for clean economy ITCs

You must make an annual election for the installation tax year to meet the labour requirements in order to claim the higher rate for the clean economy investment tax credit (ITC) you have selected to apply for.

If you do not make an annual election to meet the labour requirements, you can claim the ITC you have selected at a credit rate reduced by 10%.

The 2 main aspects to the labour requirements are prevailing wage requirements and apprenticeship requirements.

Prevailing wage requirements

Generally, the prevailing wage requirement is met by paying covered workers in accordance with a collective agreement they are subject to.

If covered workers are not subject to a collective agreement, the prevailing wage requirement is generally met as long as the covered workers are paid in an amount at least equal to the amount of wages (without taking into account overtime), benefits and pension contributions specified in an eligible collective agreement, that most closely aligns to the worker’s experience level, type of work, and location in which such work is performed.

The claimant must attest that it has:

  • met the prevailing wage requirement for its own employees who are covered workers
  • taken reasonable steps to ensure that any covered workers employed by any other person or partnership at the designated work site are compensated in accordance with the prevailing wage
Eligible collective agreement

An eligible collective agreement may be any of the following:

  • The most recent multi-employer collective bargaining agreement negotiated with a trade union that is an affiliate of Canada’s Building Trades Unions (CBTU) for a given trade in a region of province
  • A project labour agreement that both:
    • covers the work associated with the investments eligible for the specified tax credits, and
    • provides for wages and benefits for covered workers in a given trade that are at least equal to the regular wages (without taking into account overtime) and benefits provided for covered workers in multi employer collective bargaining agreements described in previous paragraph
  • A prescribed agreement

The most recent collective agreements can be accessed through sources such as:

  • CBTU
  • The provincial or territorial government where the work is being preformed
  • Labour organizations in Canada
Agreements for Quebec

For Quebec, an eligible collective agreement means a collective agreement negotiated in accordance with applicable provincial law or a prescribed agreement.

Communicating to employees

An employer must communicate to employees that the designated work site is subject to prevailing wage requirements in relation to covered workers. They also need to provide information regarding how to report failures to pay prevailing wages to the Minister of National Revenue using the CRA informant line Suspected Tax Cheating in Canada.

An employer must communicate this information and what it means for covered workers:

  • Through a poster, notice, or by electronic means
  • In a manner that is readily visible
  • In a manner that is accessible
  • In a plain language format

Factors to consider

There are various factors to consider when determining if labour requirements for prevailing wage have been met. The following list is non-exhaustive, meant only to provide general guidance for potential ITC claimants.

Consider:

  • How the wages were calculated for covered workers
  • The value that was placed on determining the benefits calculated and paid to covered workers (benefits such as vacation, pension, health and welfare)
  • Documentation of the efforts made to obtain eligible collective agreements
  • The eligible collective agreement that was used was not expired
    • If it was expired, the amounts of wages and benefits stipulated in the agreement must be adjusted by the average Consumer Price Index for each calendar year that begins after the expiration
  • The project labour agreement was signed
  • The efforts made to ensure sub contractors were aware of the labour requirements
  • How information was communicated to covered workers

To learn more, review: Subsection 127.46 (3), Prevailing wage requirements

Apprenticeship requirements

Generally, the apprenticeship requirement is met by making reasonable efforts to ensure that at least 10% of the labour performed by workers in the Red Seal trades is performed by registered apprentices at a designated work site.

Red Seal trade means, for a province using the Red Seal Program for a particular trade, the relevant Red Seal trade managed by the Canadian Council of Directors of Apprenticeship and, in any other case, an equivalent provincially registered trade.

This would be subject to the restriction that at no point could there be more apprentices working than are allowed under applicable labour laws or a collective agreement that applies to the work being performed.

The claimant must attest that it has:

  • met the apprenticeship requirements that at least 10% of the total hours that are worked during the installation taxation year are by Red Seal workers
  • ensured that work is at a designated work site of the incentive claimant on the preparation or installation of specified property

Factors to consider

There are various factors to consider when determining if labour requirements for apprenticeship have been met. The following list is non-exhaustive, meant only to provide general guidance for potential ITC claimants.

Consider:

  • The calculation made to confirm at least 10% of the labour performed by Red Seal trades is performed by apprentices
  • The reasonable efforts made to ensure the minimum target of 10% was achieved
  • If projects were under construction prior to the coming into force date, that a review was made to verify requirements were met as of the coming into force date

Reasonable efforts

For the purposes of apprenticeship requirements, an incentive claimant will be deemed to have satisfied the reasonable efforts if certain conditions are met. These include:

  • At least every 4 months:
    • the incentive claimant posts a bona fide job advertisement, seeking sufficient apprentices to perform those hours of labour that includes a commitment to facilitate participation of apprentices in a Red Seal trade program and statement that the job opportunity is open to both existing employees and new hires. This job advertisement should be open and readily accessible on the Job Bank website of the Government of Canada and at least 2 other websites either on a continuous basis throughout the year or for at least 30 days from the time of posting.
    • the incentive claimant communicates with a trade union and at least one secondary school or post-secondary educational institution for the purpose of facilitating the hiring of the apprentice positions described in the job advertisement
    • confirmation in writing from the trade union that the trade union has provided as many apprentices as reasonably possible for the work at the designated work site during the installation year, unless the trade union fails to respond within 5 business days of a request.
  • The incentive claimant reviews and duly considers all applications received in response to the advertisement for apprenticeship opportunities that are offered directly by the incentive claimant and takes reasonable steps to ensure that other applications are reviewed and duly considered
  • The incentive claimant attests in prescribed form that it has complied with the conditions

To learn more, review: Subsection 127.46 (5), Apprenticeship requirements

Consequences of non-compliance

There are consequences for not complying with the labour requirements when the claimant has attested and elected into those requirements, and claimed the higher credit.

These may include:

  • A penalty for prevailing wages not compensated
    • $20 per day for each day in the taxation year, for each covered worker that was not paid the prevailing wage
  • A corrective measure in the form of a top-up amount paid to each covered worker for the amount of the prevailing wage not paid as per notification from the Minister
    • A penalty paid to the Minister of 120% of the top-up amount determined for each worker that was not paid the top-up amount
  • A penalty for apprenticeship hours not met
    • $50 multiplied by the difference between the hours of labour that were required to be performed by apprentices of Red Seal trades, and the hours of labour that were actually performed by apprentices

Where failure to meet any of the labour requirements is done knowingly or in circumstances amounting in gross negligence, the claimant is:

  • disentitled to the regular tax credit rate and is entitled only to the reduced tax credit rate
  • liable to pay a penalty equal to 50% of the difference between the amount of the specified tax credit claimed and the amount that the incentive claimant would have been entitled to under the reduced rate

When the labour requirements apply

Labour Requirements apply in respect to specified property prepared or installed on or after November 28, 2023.

Covered workers

A covered worker is an individual (other than a trust):

  • who is engaged in the preparation or installation of specified property at a designated work site as an employee of an incentive claimant or of another person or partnership
  • whose work or duties in respect of the designated work site are primarily manual or physical in nature

A covered worker is not:

  • an administrative, clerical or executive employee
  • a business visitor to Canada as described in section 187 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulation
