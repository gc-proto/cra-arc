RRSPs

How a Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) is treated at the time of the annuitant’s death depends on whether it is unmatured or matured and if there was a survivor or beneficiary.

You may need to contact the deceased's financial institution or investment broker to determine which type of RRSP the deceased held.

Income received up until the date of death

Found on slip
T4RSP - box 34 (issued to the deceased annuitant)
Report on line
12900 of the deceased annuitant's final income tax return

At the time of the annuitant’s death, we consider them to have received the FMV of:

  • all property held in their RRSP (Unmatured)
  • all remaining annuity payments under their RRSP (Matured)

    This amount and all other amounts the annuitant received from the RRSP in the year have to be reported on the annuitant’s income tax and benefit return for the year of death.

    A beneficiary will not have to pay tax on any amount paid out of the RRSP if it can reasonably be regarded as having been included in the deceased annuitant’s income.

    Unmatured RRSP

    An unmatured RRSP has not yet started to pay the retirement income to the RRSP holder. It is a plan for individuals to contribute to until age 71 when it must be converted to an RRIF or buy an annuity.

    Rollover or transfer the unmatured RRSP

    A survivor or beneficiary will report the FMV as income on their tax return, but will be able to claim a deduction for some or all of this amount.

    At the time of death, you must be either the spouse or common-law partner of the annuitant or a financially dependant child or grandchild of the annuitant.

    Full rollover to a spouse or common-law partner
    Found on slip
    T4RSP - box 18 (issued to the spouse or common-law partner)
    Report on line
    12900 of the spouse or common-law partner's personal income tax return

    2 conditions must be met:

    • spouse or common-law partner is named as sole beneficiary of the RRSP contract, and
    • entire RRSP property is directly transferred, by December 31 of the year following the year of death, into a registered plan (RRSP, PRPP, RRIF) in which the spouse or common-law partner is the annuitant, or to an issuer to buy an eligible annuity
    Transfer to a qualifying survivor (spouse who doesn't qualify for a rollover, or a financially dependent child or grandchild)

    When a qualifying survivor includes a refund of premiums in income, they can defer paying tax on the amount by transferring it to an RRSP, PRPP, RRIF, RDSP or to buy an eligible annuity.

    The transfer must be completed in the year the refund of premiums is received or within 60 days after the end of the year.

    If the qualifying survivor is 71 in the year the refund of premiums is received, the transfer to an RRSP must be completed by December 31 of that year.

    What qualifies as refund of premiums

    Either of the following amounts if paid to a beneficiary:

  • The FMV of the RRSP on the day the annuitant died
  • Income earned in the RRSP from the day after the annuitant died to December 31 of the year after the year of death

      • For more information on refund of premiums, refer to Chart 1 - Amounts paid from a deceased annuitant's RRSP.

      If these amounts are paid to the annuitant's estate, they will qualify as a refund of premiums if both of the following conditions are met:

      • there is a qualifying survivor who is a beneficiary of the annuitant's estate
      • the annuitant's legal representative and the qualifying survivor jointly file Form T2019, Death of an RRSP Annuitant- Refund of Premiums, to designate all or part of the amounts paid to the estate as a refund of premiums received by the qualifying survivor.
      • Transfers that qualifying survivors can chose

        • The issuer who receives the transferred funds will issue a receipt to the qualifying survivor. The qualifying survivor can use the receipt to claim a deduction on their income tax return for the year the refund of premiums was received.

        Rollover to an RDSP
        Found on slip
        T4A - box 131 (issued to the beneficiary)
        Report on line
        12500 of the beneficiary's personal income tax return

        The existing RRSP rollover rules allow a specified RDSP payment from the deceased person's RRSP to the RDSP of an eligible individual.

        Who qualifies as an eligible individual

        A child or grandchild of a deceased annuitant under an RRSP or a RRIF, or of a deceased member of a PRPP, or RDSP into which the eligible proceeds will be paid.

          A specified RDSP payment is a payment that meets all of the following conditions:

          • the payment is made to an RDSP under which the eligible individual is the beneficiary
          • the payment satisfies the contribution conditions described in the Registered Disability Savings Plan
          • effective March 19, 2019, if the eligible individual no longer qualifies for the disability tax credit, the payment is made before the end of the fifth taxation year throughout which the beneficiary is ineligible for the disability tax credit 

          Form RC4625, Rollover to a Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) Under Paragraph 60(m), must be attached to both the deceased annuitant's and the eligible individual's income tax and benefit returns.

          In these situations, you will not have to fill out a Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities. However, you must attach to the income tax and benefit return the receipt indicating the amount of the rollover.

          The amount that can be rolled over to an RDSP cannot exceed the RDSP lifetime limit of $200,000.

          • Guide RC4460, Registered Disability Savings Plan
          Which children qualify as financially dependent

          A child or grandchild of an annuitant, where before the annuitant's death, were living with them and met 1 of the following conditions:

          • net income for the previous year (line 23600 of income tax return) was less than the basic personal amount (line 30000 of income tax return) for that previous year
          • financial independence is due to a mental or physical infirmity and your net income for the previous year was equal to or less than the basic personal amount, plus the disability amount (line 31600 of income tax return) for that previous year
          Claim the deduction for rollover to a spouse or transfer to a qualifying survivor
          • If the property was directly transferred to a beneficiary's RRSP, PRPP or SPP, they can claim a deduction on line 20800 of their income tax return.
            • If the amount was transferred to their RRIF, RDSP or annuity, claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 23200 of their income tax return.

            Refer to the example with Martin and Elaine to determine how to calculate the reduction for rollover to a spouse - Example

              Homebuyer's Plan (HBP) repayments

              If the deceased participated in the HBP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP.

              The amount of any RRSP, PRPP, SPP, or contributions that the deceased made to their individual plans in the year of their death can be designated as a repayment.

              Include on line 12900 the total of all amounts that remained to be repaid at the time of death, unless the legal representative and the surviving spouse or common-law partner jointly elect to have the surviving spouse or common-law partner continue to make the repayments.

              • Deceased participated in the Home Buyers' Plan (HBP).

              Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP) repayments

              If the deceased participated in the LLP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP.

              Treatment of these amounts is the same as with the Home Buyer's Plan, and a similar election is available.

              • Repayments under the Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP).

              Matured RRSP

              A matured RRSP provides retirement income for the annuitant. It is a plan that individuals can no longer contribute to.

              Rollover or transfer the matured RRSP

              If the spouse or common-law partner of the deceased annuitant is entitled to receive amounts under a matured RRSP, the RRSP continues to make annuity payments to the spouse or common-law partner as the successor annuitant.

              Rollover to a successor annuitant
              Found on slip
              T4RSP - box XXXX (issued to the successor annuitant)
              Report on line
              12900 of the success annuitant's income tax return

              If the annuitant's spouse or common-law partner is designated as a beneficiary of the RRSP in the RRSP contract or the will:

              • the remaining RRSP will not need to be reported in the final return of the deceased
              • the spouse or common-law partner will begin receiving the remaining annuity payments from the plan and will report those as part of their income

              Elect to have RRSP payments rolled over to a spouse or common-law partner

              If the surviving spouse or common-law partner is the beneficiary of the estate, that person and the legal representative can jointly elect, in a written letter, to treat the amounts paid to the estate as being paid to the spouse or common-law partner.

              • The written election has to specify that this person is electing to become the annuitant of the RRSP
              • Attach a copy of the written election to the income tax return of the surviving spouse or common-law partner
              • Provide a copy of the written election to the payer of the annuity

              After the election is made, the spouse or common-law partner will be treated as if they were the beneficiary designated in the RRSP contract.

              Claim the deduction for elected split-pension amounts with a spouse or common-law partner

              If the deceased received RRSP annuity payments before the date of death, this income can be treated as pension income for purposes of the deduction for elected split pension amount. This deduction allows this income to be split between the deceased and their spouse or common law partner, which could reduce the total amount of tax payable.

              To make this election, complete Form T1032, Joint Election to Split Pension Income. Enter the full amount of the annuity payments received on line 12900 of the deceased’s tax return, and claim a deduction for the elected split-pension amount on line 21000.

              The spouse or common-law partner of the deceased will need to report their split-pension amount on line 12900 of their tax return.

              • Line 21000 - Deduction for elected split-pension amount

              Income received after the date of death

              Did the value of the registered account increase or decrease between the date of death and when it was paid to a beneficiary?

              Yes - increased in value
              Found on slip
              T4RSP - box 28 (issued to the beneficiary or the estate)
              Report on either line
              12900 of the beneficiary's personal income tax return
              1 of the estate's T3 Trust tax return

              Sometimes there is an increase in the value of an RRSP between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or estate.

              • This amount has to be included in the income of the beneficiary or the estate for the year it is received. A T4RSP slip will be issued for this amount.
              Yes - decreased in value

              Sometimes, the FMV of the property of an unmatured RRSP decreases between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or the estate.

              • If the total of all distributions from the RRSP is less than the FMV of the property that was included in the deceased annuitant's income for the year of death, the deceased's legal representative can request that the difference be deducted on the deceased's final return through a reassessment.

                  • Refer to Capital losses in the T3 can be carried back to the final return

                  • Generally, for the deduction to be allowed, the final distribution must occur by the end of the year that follows the year of death.
                  No - there was no change in value

                  If the value of the RRSP did not change between the date of death and distribution to a beneficiary, there are no further amounts to report.

                  Report income earned after the date of death

                  Income earned in the RRSP after the date of death should be reported with other pension income.
