Tax refunds

If you have an amount on line 48400 of your personal income tax return [notice of assessment?], you will receive a refund.

If your tax return is a complex tax situation

Some tax filing situations are more complex and will require additional verification.

Complex tax filing situations include:

  • Multiple tax returns
  • An international or non-resident, or emigrant return
  • A bankruptcy return
  • A deceased taxpayer return
  • A request from the CRA to you or your authorized representative for more information or documents
  • Processing time: Approximately within 16 weeks

Receive your refund

You can receive your refund:

  • By direct deposit

    If you have direct deposit, we will deposit your refund directly into your bank account.

    If you do not have direct deposit, or need to update your direct deposit information, refer to: Set up or change your direct deposit

  • By cheque

    If you choose not to set up your direct deposit, we will send you your refund by mail to the address the CRA has on file.

    Keep your personal information up-to-date

    If your situation changes, you should let the CRA know to make sure you get your payments.

    For example, if you move and do not update your address with the CRA, your refund cheque is returned to the CRA. A flag is added on your file and any following payments will be calculated but not issued until the address is updated.

    Refer to: Update your personal information

If your refund is not what you expected

If you think your refund amount is incorrect, review the information on your notice of assessment (NOA).

For more information on NOAs, refer to: Notice of assessment

Why you may not receive a refund

Generally, you will not receive a refund if your refund amount is $2 or less.

The CRA may keep all or part of your refund if you have:

  • An amount owing (or pending amount owing)
  • A garnishment order under the Family Orders and Agreements Enforcement Assistance Act
  • Certain other outstanding federal, provincial or territorial government debts, such as student loans, employment insurance (EI) and social assistance benefit overpayments, immigration loans or training allowance overpayments
  • Any outstanding GST/HST returns from a sole proprietorship or partnership

For more information, refer to: How payments are applied to offset debt

Why you may receive interest on your refund

In some situations, the CRA will pay you compound daily interest on your 2023 tax refund.

The calculation will start on the latest of the following 3 dates:

  • May 30, 2024
  • The 30th day after you file your return
  • The day you overpaid your taxes
