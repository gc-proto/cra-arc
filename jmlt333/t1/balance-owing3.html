Personal income tax

Balance owing

You will have a balance owing if you paid less income taxes than you owe. You need to pay the amount that you owe to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA).

If you have paid your balance owing

If your notice was issued before your payment was received by the CRA, your notice may still show a balance owing, even if you have already paid in full.

You can check if the CRA receive your payment: Comfirm your payment to the CRA

How to pay your balance owing

If your balance owing is $10,000 or more, you should make your payment electronically. There may be a penalty if you don't pay your balance owing electronically.

Do not mail cash or include cash with your return. You can pay your balance owing online, by mail, or in person.

If you cannot pay in full

You can have options to manage your balance:

  • Make a partial payment to reduce the interest on unpaid amounts

  • Set up a payment plan to give yourself more time and flexibility

    Refer to: Arrange to pay your debt over time

What happens if you do not pay

There are consequences if you don't pay the taxes you owe, including:

  • You could be charged interest and penalties on unpaid balance owing
  • Applying your tax refunds or other government payments to your debt

Reduce your balance owing for next year

You can reduce the amount of tax you owe to the CRA at the end of the tax year by increasing deductions from your income source.

  • If you receive a paycheque

    Generally, most employers will deduct tax directly from your pay.

    You can choose to have more tax deducted from your pay by completing a TD1 form and providing it to your employer.

    Refer to: Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Returns

  • If you receive CPP payments

    Taxes are not automatically deducted from your CPP payments but you can make the request for it to be.

    Refer to: Deducting federal income tax from your CPP payments
