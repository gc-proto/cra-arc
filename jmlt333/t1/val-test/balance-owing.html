Personal income tax

Balance owing

You may be looking for:

  • Line 48500 - Balance owing

You have a balance owing if you paid less income tax than you owe. You have to pay the amount you owe to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) on or before April 30, 2025 to avoid paying interest and penalties.

Review your balance owing

You can review your balance owing and explanation of changes on your notice of assessment (NOA) or notice of reassessment (NOR). Generally, you do not have to make a payment if your balance owing is $2 or less.

Refer to: Notices of assessment

Check your updated balance

Your CRA account shows the most up-to-date balance owing, including all payments and balances across multiple years.

The amount from your CRA account may be different from your NOA or NOR, if you have:

  • Already paid the amount you owed but your notice was issued before the payment was received
  • An unpaid balance from a previous year

You can check your tax account balance online or by phone. Wait 3 business days for an online payment to process and 10 business days (plus mailing time) for a payment by cheque or money order.

Online

Sign in to your CRA account to view your balance in Accounts and payments. You can register if you do not have an account.

How to pay your balance owing

You can pay your balance owing online, by mail, or in person. Do not mail cash or include cash with your paper return.

If your balance owing is $10,000 or more you must make your payment electronically. There may be a penalty if you do not pay your balance owing electronically.

Find the option that works best for you:

Make a payment

If you cannot pay in full

You have options to pay your balance owing:

  • Make a partial payment to reduce the interest on unpaid amounts
  • Set up a payment arrangement to give yourself more time and flexibility

Refer to: Arrange to pay your debt over time

Interest and penalties on late payments

If you pay your balance owing after the due date, you may be charged interest on the amount until it is fully paid. Also, if you filed your tax return late, you may be charged a late-filing penalty.

Refer to: Interest and penalties on late taxes

If you do not pay

There are consequences if you do not pay your taxes when they are due.

Confirm your payment was received by the CRA

You can confirm the CRA has received your payment for the balance owing on your tax return by checking your updated balance.

You should wait:

  • 3 business days for online payments
  • 10 business days (plus mailing time) for payments by cheque or money order

If you have any issues with your payment, refer to resolve an issue with a payment.

Reduce your balance owing for next year

You can reduce the amount of tax you owe to the CRA at the end of the tax year by increasing the deductions from your income at source.

If you receive a paycheque

Generally, most employers will deduct tax directly from your pay. You can choose to have more tax deducted from your pay by completing Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Returns and providing it to your employer.

If you have more than one job

You must select the box "More than one employer or payer at the same time" on each Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Returns you fill out for your employers.

If you receive pension payments

If you are 60 years or older and receive Canada Pension Plan (CPP) payments or Old Age Security (OAS) payments, taxes are not automatically deducted. If you want taxes deducted from these payments, you can make a request.

For information on how to deduct federal income tax from your pension payments, refer to CPP taxes or OAS payments.

Pay tax by instalments

Tax instalments are payments you make throughout the year to cover the taxes you normally pay in one lump sum on April 30 of the following year.

You may have to pay tax by instalments if your income does not have enough tax withheld or if you are self-employed, have rental or investment income, certain pension payments, or have income from more than one job.

Refer to: Required tax instalments for individuals
