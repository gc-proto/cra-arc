Tax rates and income brackets for individuals

Canadian federal, provincial, and territorial tax rates and income brackets for current and previous tax years.

How tax rates and income brackets work

Tax rates are the percentage of income you pay to federal and provincial or territorial governments when you do your taxes.

Canada has a progressive income tax system, which means you pay more tax as your income increases. The tax rates are split up into brackets, and the taxes you pay are based on which of the tax brackets your income falls into, and where you live in Canada.

A different tax rate applies to each tax bracket but only to the income within that bracket. The higher tax rate does not apply to all your taxable income. The same tax brackets are used for all types of income, such as employment income or occasional earnings.

Provincial and territorial income tax rates apply in addition to federal income tax rates. Use both the federal and provincial or territorial rates to determine the total income tax you may owe.

If you live in one province or territory and work in a different province or territory

Most employers will consider you a resident in the region which you work when deducting provincial or territorial tax from your pay.

You are also required to pay provincial or territorial taxes where you reside.

For example, an Ontario resident working in Quebec will generally have their pay taxed following Quebec’s provincial taxes. They must also pay Ontario provincial taxes when filing their Income Tax and Benefit Return.

Yearly tax rates and income brackets

There are:

  • Federal tax rates and income brackets
  • Provincial and territorial tax rates and income brackets

The Government of Canada sets the federal income tax rates for individuals. Each province and territory determines their own income tax rates.

Each year, income bracket thresholds increase based on inflation. Certain other personal income tax and benefit amounts are also adjusted based on inflation.

For more information, refer to: Indexation adjustment amounts

Reducing your end of year tax amount

You can reduce the amount of tax you may owe at the end of the tax year by claiming deductions and increasing deductions from your payments.

Reduce your taxable income by claiming deductions

The taxes you owe are calculated using your taxable income. You may be eligible to claim various deductions to reduce your taxable income.

Deductions are certain expenses and other amounts that you subtract from your total income or your net income when you fill out your income tax and benefit return.

For more information on all types of deductions, refer to: Claiming deductions, credits, and expenses

Increase income tax deductions from your payments

Increasing your income tax deductions can avoid you having to pay a large amount of tax at the end of the tax year.

Choose to increase deductions from your pay

Generally, most employers will deduct both federal income tax and provincial or territorial income tax directly from your pay. You can choose to have more tax deducted from your pay by completing a TD1 form and providing it to your employer.

Refer to: Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Returns

If you are an employer, refer to: Increasing income tax deducted at source

Request for deductions from your CPP payments

Taxes are not automatically deducted from your CPP payments but you can ask that federal tax be deducted.

For more information, refer to: Deducting federal income tax from your CPP payments

Combined tax rate

Your combined tax rate is your federal income tax rate plus your provincial or territorial income tax rate.

You will calculate your federal tax using the federal income tax rate. You will calculate your provincial or territorial tax using the income tax rate that applies to your situation.

Taxable income

Taxable income is line 26000 of your Income Tax and Benefit Return. It is your income after various deductions have been applied.

If you lived in Quebec on December 31

If you lived in Quebec on December 31 of the tax year, you will calculate your provincial taxes by completing a separate provincial tax return for Revenu Québec.

Refer to: Revenu Québec
