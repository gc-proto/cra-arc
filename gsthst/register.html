Generally, you cannot register for a GST/HST account if you provide only exempt supplies .

You have to register for a GST/HST account if both situations apply:

  • You are not a small supplier
  • You make taxable sales, leases or other supplies  in Canada (unless your only taxable supplies are of real property sold other than in the course of a business)

If you are a selected listed financial institution that is making a reporting entity, consolidated filing or tax adjustment transfer election, refer to: GST/HST Notice 265, GST/HST Registration for Listed Financial Institutions (including Selected Listed Financial Institutions).

Use the following sections to determine if you are a small supplier to find out if you have to register.

Sole proprietor, partnership or corporation

Steps

  1. If you do not exceed the $30,000 small supplier threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters , you are a small supplier.

    Example – Did not exceed the $30,000 threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters

    On February 1, 2023, you started a business that sells candles. You made the following sales:

    Calculation
    Quarter Amount
    First quarter (January 1 to March 31) $2,000
    Second quarter (April 1 to June 30) $10,000
    Third quarter (July 1 to September 30) $12,000
    Fourth quarter (October 1 to December 31) $5,000
    Total $29,000

    You are a small supplier in 2023 because you did not exceed the $30,000 threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters. You are still a small supplier in the first quarter of 2024, unless you exceed the $30,000 threshold in that one single calendar quarter.

    How to calculate your $30,000 small supplier threshold

    • Revenues from worldwide  taxable  supplies, including zero-rated (taxable at 0%)  supplies from all your businesses
    • plus Revenues from worldwide taxable supplies made by all associated  businesses
    • Amounts to exclude
      • Exempt  supplies
        Exception - Independent sales contractor (ISC) of a direct seller
        What is a direct seller

        Direct sellers supply goods to consumers through a network of independent sales contractors (ISCs). They often use non-traditional retail channels, such as door-to-door sales, and party sales.

        What is the alternate collection method (ACM)

        Direct sellers who sell their products to ISCs may apply to the CRA to use the alternate collection method (ACM).

        • The direct seller (or an approved distributor) must receive approval from the CRA to use the ACM.
        • The direct seller must elect with the ISCs to account for the GST/HST on their behalf.

        Once approved by the CRA, they account for the GST/HST on the suggested retail price of exclusive products sold to their ISCs.

        Learn more: GST/HST Info Sheet GI-125, Direct Selling Industry - The Alternate Collection Method for Approved Direct Sellers and Approved Distributors.

        Do not include the following in your $30,000 small supplier threshold calculation if you are an independent sales contractor (ISC) and you have an election with the direct seller to use the alternate method of accounting  (ACM):

        • Your sales of exclusive products
        • Your supplies of sales aids to other ISCs
        • Bonus payments you received from the direct seller

        If you already have a GST/HST account, do not account for the GST/HST on the above supplies.

        Example - Did not exceed the $30,000 threshold

        A self-employed florist sells exclusive products of a direct seller on a part-time basis. They have an election with the direct seller to use the ACM. Over the last 4 consecutive calendar quarters, they earned $28,000 from selling flowers and $35,000 from selling exclusive products of the direct seller.

        The florist is not required to register for a GST/HST account number. The $35,000 revenues from the sale of exclusive products of a direct seller with an approved ACM election are not considered when determining whether he has to register.

        Example - ISCs engaged in other activities or other businesses

        Diane is not registered for the GST/HST. She works from her home selling products for 2 direct selling businesses (Toys Inc and Fashion Inc). Toys Inc has an approved ACM election. As an ISC, Diane purchases toys from Toys Inc and sells them to consumers. Diane also receives bonus payments from Toys Inc and provides training to other ISCs for which she charges a fee to Toys Inc. The ACM rules apply to Diane’s sales of Toys Inc products and to the bonus payments she received from Toys Inc, but not to the training services that she provides.

        Fashion Inc has not provided Diane with an approved election to use the ACM.

        When determining if she has exceeded the $30,000 small supplier threshold calculation, Diane:

        • Includes earnings from

          • Training provided to Toys Inc
          • Sales of Fashion Inc products

        • Does not include earnings from

          • Sales of Toys Inc products
          • Bonus payments from Toys Inc. 

        If she is required to register for the GST/HST because she exceeded the $30,000 small supplier threshold, Diane:

        • Charges the GST/HST on her sales of Fashion Inc products and her invoices for training provided to Toys Inc
        • Does not charge the GST/HST on her sales of Toys Inc products or her bonus payments received from Toys Inc

        Learn more: GST/HST Info Sheet GI-126, Direct Selling Industry - The Alternate Collection Method for Independent Sales Contractors.

        Exception - Commission-based sales representative of a network seller
        What is a network seller

        Network sellers operate networks of independent sales representatives who sell goods or services directly to consumers. In a commission-based model, representatives sell goods on behalf of the network seller and earn a commission.

        What is the network seller method (NSM)

        Network sells who sell their products through commission-based sales representatives may apply to the CRA to use the network seller method (NSM).

        • The network seller must receive approval from the CRA to use the NSM.
        • The network seller must elect with their sales representatives to use the NSM.

        Once approved by the CRA, the network commissions charged by a sales representative to a network seller are not subject to GST/HST.

        Learn more, including how to apply for approval to use the network sellers method: GST/HST Info Sheet GI-052, Direct Selling Industry - The Network Sellers Method for Network Sellers and Sales Representatives.

        Do not include the commissions you received from the network seller in your $30,000 small supplier threshold calculation if all of the following apply:

        • You are a commission-based sales representative of a network seller
        • You have an election with the network seller to use the network sellers method  (NSM)

        If you are have a GST/HST account, do not account for the GST/HST on the commissions.

        Example - Network commissions

        A network seller that uses the NSM sells a variety of food products to consumers in Alberta. Some of the products are taxable while others such as spices and condiments are zero-rated.

        A sales representative receives a commission for each sale of products arranged for the network seller. The sales representative's commissions are not subject to tax regardless of whether the commissions relate to taxable or zero-rated products. Additionally, the sales representative does not include their network commissions in their calculation to determine whether they are small suppliers.

        Example - Sales aids

        A network seller that uses the NSM sells cosmetics and beauty products to consumers. A sales representative receives a commission for each sale arranged for the network seller. The network seller sold the sales representative a demonstration kit that is a sales aid. Later, the sales representative sold the kit to another sales representative.

        The NSM rules apply to sales aids sold by a sales representative to another sales representative. Therefore, the GST/HST does not apply to the sale of the demonstration kit.

        In their calculation to determine whether they are small suppliers, the sales representative does not include either:

        • Their network commissions from the sales of cosmetics and beauty products.
        • Their revenues from their sales of the demonstration kits to other representatives.

        If the sales representative had sold the demonstration kit to a consumer and is registered for the GST/HST, they would charge GST/HST since the NSM rules do not apply to sales of sales aids to consumers.

        Learn more: GST/HST Info Sheet GI-126, Direct Selling Industry - The Alternate Collection Method for Independent Sales Contractors.

      • Supplies of financial services
      • Goodwill  from the sale of a business

    • If the threshold amount does not exceed $30,000 over 4 consecutive calendar quarters, you are a small supplier.

  2. Mandatory registration if you are no longer a small supplier

    • $30,000 threshold
      over 4 consecutive calendar quarters

      You have to register for a GST/HST account number because you exceeded the $30,000 small supplier threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters .

      You are no longer a small supplier at the end of the month following the quarter in which you exceeded the $30,000 small supplier threshold.

      Example – Exceed the $30,000 threshold over 2 consecutive calendar quarters

      On November 1, 2023, you opened a retail store. You made the following taxable sales:

      Calculation
      Quarter Amount
      First quarter (October 1 to December 31) $25,000
      Second quarter (January 1 to March 31) $25,000
      Total $50,000

      In April 2024, your sales totaled $ 8,000.

      Your first sale after April was on May 1, 2024.

      • You stopped being a small supplier on April 30, 2024, because that day is the end of the month after the quarter you exceeded the $30,000 threshold.
      • Your effective date of registration is no later than May 1, 2024. That day is when you made your first supply after you stopped being a small supplier.
      • You have to start charging the GST/HST on all your taxable supplies beginning May 1, 2024 (your effective date of registration).
      • You will have to apply for registration no later than May 30, 2024 (within 29 days of your effective date of registration).
      Example – Exceed the $30,000 threshold within the previous 4 consecutive calendar quarters

      On June 1, 2023, you started a business selling baseball caps. You made the following sales:

      Calculation
      Quarter Amount
      First quarter (April 1 to June 30) $2,000
      Second quarter (July 1 to September 30) $10,000
      Third quarter (October 1 to December 31) $12,000
      Fourth quarter (January 1 to March 31) $8,000
      Total $32,000

      In April 2024, your sales totaled $ 4,500.

      Your first sale after April was on May 2, 2024.

      • You stopped being a small supplier on April 30, 2024, because that day is the end of the month after the quarter you exceeded the $30,000 threshold.
      • Your effective date of registration is no later than May 2, 2024. That day is when you made your first supply after you stopped being a small supplier.
      • You have to start charging the GST/HST all your taxable supplies beginning May 2, 2024 (your effective date of registration).
      • You will have to apply for registration no later than May 31, 2024 (within 29 days of your effective date of registration).

    • $30,000 threshold
      in a single calendar quarter

      You have to register for a GST/HST account number because you exceeded the $30,000 small supplier threshold in a single calendar quarter ..

      You are no longer a small supplier the day of the supply that made you exceed the $30,000 small supplier threshold.

      Example – Exceed the $30,000 threshold in one single calendar quarter

      On January 1, 2024, you started a consulting business. You made the following revenue from your taxable services.

      Calculation
      Quarter Amount
      First quarter (January 1 to March 31) $2,000
      Second quarter (April 1 to June 30) $10,000
      Third quarter (July 1 to September 30) $38,000
      • On September 10, 2024 your sales exceeded $30,000 for the July 1 to September 30 quarter.
      • You stopped being a small supplier on September 10, 2024 (the day you exceed the $30,000 threshold in one calendar quarter).
      • Your effective date of registration is no later than September 10, 2024 (the day of the supply you exceeded the $30,000 threshold in the quarter).
      • You have to start charging the GST/HST on all your taxable supplies beginning September 10, 2024 (your effective date of registration).
      • You will have to apply for registration no later than October 9, 2024 (within 29 days of your effective date of registration).

  3. Registering for the GST/HST

    • Effective date of registration

      Your effective date of registration is no later than the day of the first supply you make after you are no longer a small supplier.

    • Registration application

      You have to apply to register for a GST/HST account number no later than 29 days after your effective date of registration.

    Example

    Steve exceeded the small supplier threshold in a single calendar quarter on November 15, 2024. He is no longer a small supplier as of that date.

    • Effective date of registration

      When Steve applied for registration, he entered November 15, 2024 as his effective date of registration.

    • Registration application

      Steve applied for registration on December 10, 2024.

    Learn more: Register for a GST/HST account number

  4. You have to charge the GST/HST on all your taxable supplies no later than the first supply you make after you are no longer a small supplier.

    Example

    Steve exceeded the small supplier threshold in a single calendar quarter on November 15, 2024.

    He is no longer a small supplier and is required to be registered for the GST/HST as of November 15, 2024.

    Steve applied for registration on December 10, 2024, entering November 15, 2024 as his effective date of registration.

    Even though Steve did not register and receive his GST/HST account number until December 10, 2024, he still has to start charging the GST/HST on his supplies beginning November 15, 2024.

    Learn more: Charge and collect the tax


Sale of real property

Steps

  1. Confirm if the exception to GST/HST registration applies

    If your only commercial activity  is the sale of real property , other than in the course of a business , you do not have to register for a GST/HST account number.

    Example

    A doctor's practice consists of making exempt supplies of health care services. The doctor decides on a one-time basis to buy a commercial building. Several months later, the doctor sells the building for a profit.

    These transactions would be viewed as an adventure in the nature of trade rather than carrying on a business. The doctor's sale of the property is taxable and he must include GST/HST.

    The doctor is not required to register for a GST/HST account number for the purpose of reporting the GST/HST collectible on this sale.

    Learn more: 19-4-1 Commercial Real Property - Sales and Rentals

  2. Registering for the GST/HST

    • If you sell taxable real property in the course of a business, you have to register for a GST/HST account number, unless you are not a small supplier.

      Determine if you are a small supplier

      To determine if you are a small supplier, continue to one of the following depending on your type of business:

      • Sole proprietor, partnership or corporation
      • Charity or public institution
      • Public service body that is not a charity or public institution

    • If your sale of real property is not in the course of a business (a one-time sale), you do not have to register for a GST/HST account.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Charging the GST/HST.

  3. You have to charge the GST/HST on your taxable sale of real property, even if you are not registered for the GST/HST. However, if the purchaser is registered for the GST/HST, the purchaser has to remit the tax you charged on the sale directly to the CRA instead of paying it to you.

    Example

    Continuing with the example in step 1, the doctor sold the commercial property in Ontario for $2,000,000 to a purchaser that is registered for the GST/HST. Since the sale of the commercial real property is taxable, $26,000 HST applies to the sale.

    Because the purchaser is registered for the GST/HST, the doctor does not collect the $26,000 HST that applied to the sale. Instead, the doctor includes the $26,000 HST in the sales agreement and the purchaser is responsible for reporting that HST to the CRA directly.

    If the purchaser was not registered for the GST/HST, the doctor would have to collect the $26,000 HST on the sale and report it to the CRA.

    Learn more: Charge and collect the tax

  4. Claiming input tax credits (ITC)

    If you are not registered for the GST/HST, you are not eligible to claim ITCs.

    If you register voluntarily, you may be eligible to claim ITCs for the GST/HST you had to pay for any costs related to acquiring, improving, leasing or operating the property prior to your sale.

    Learn more: Input tax credits

  5. Filing GST/HST returns

    If you are not registered for the GST/HST and you had to collect the GST/HST on the sale, you have to report the tax collectible on line 103 of Form GST62, Goods and Services Tax/Harmonized Sales Tax (GST/HST) Return (Non‑personalized).

    You have to file this GST/HST return by the end of the month following the month you had to collect the GST/HST on the sale. You also have to provide a letter explaining the real property transaction and a copy of the statement of adjustments (if available).

    Learn more on how to get the GST/HST return: Remittance vouchers and payment forms

  6. Remitting your payment to the CRA

    If you are not registered for the GST/HST and you had to collect the GST/HST on the sale, you have to remit the net GST/HST payable when filing your GST/HST return.

    Learn more: Remit (pay) the tax you collected


Charity or public institution
What is charity or public institution

Steps

  1. Confirm if you are a branch or division

    If you are a branch or a division, you cannot register for a GST/HST account number separately.

    However, if your public service body, including a charity  or public institution  has branches or divisions, you may apply to have each branch or division with $50,000 or less in taxable supplies  designated as a small supplier division.

    A public service body (PSB), including a charity or public institution, you may apply for its branches or divisions to be designated as small supplier.

    Learn more: GST31 Application by a Public Service Body to Have Branches or Divisions Treated As Eligible Small Supplier Divisions

    • If you are a branch or a division, do not continue to next step.

    • If you are not a branch or a division, continue to: Determine if you are a small supplier.

  2. Determine if you are a small supplier

    1. $250,000 gross revenue test

      If you are a charity or public institution in one of the following situation, you are a small supplier under the $250,000 gross revenue test:

      • You are not yet operating
      • You are in your first fiscal year  of operation
      • You are in your second fiscal year of operation and your gross revenue from your first fiscal year is $250,000 or less
      • You are in your third fiscal year of operation and your gross revenue from either or both of your previous 2 fiscal years is $250,000 or less

    2. $50,000 small supplier threshold

      If you are no longer a small supplier under the $250,000 gross revenue test, you need to determine if you are a small supplier under the $50,000 small supplier threshold.

      If you do not exceed the $50,000 small supplier threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters , you are a small supplier.

      Example – Did not exceed the $50,000 threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters

      On January 15, 2023, your charity started making taxable supplies of goods and made the following sales:

      Second quarter (April 1 to June 30) $15,000
      Third quarter (July 1 to September 30) $17,000
      Fourth quarter (October 1 to December 31) $10,000
      Total $49,000

      You are a small supplier in 2023 because you did not exceed the $50,000 threshold over 4 consecutive calendar quarters. You are also a small supplier under this test in the first quarter of 2024, unless you exceed the $50,000 threshold in that one single calendar quarter.

          • Other revenue of any kind whatever during the year, including revenue from both taxable and exempt supplies

Taxi operator or commercial ride-sharing driver
What is a taxi operator or commercial ride-sharing driver

A taxi business means a business carried on in Canada of transporting passengers by taxi for fares that are regulated under the laws of Canada or a province. A driver who supplies taxable commercial ride-sharing services on or after July 1, 2017, is considered to be a taxi business for GST/HST purposes.

You are usually self-employed if you are in one of the following situations:

  • You own your taxicab/motor vehicle
  • You lease a taxicab/motor vehicle from an owner for a flat fee, either on a daily, weekly, or monthly basis
  • You lease the taxicab/motor vehicle from an owner for a percentage of fares

Steps

  1. Determine if you are self-employed

    You can ask the CRA for ruling if you are not sure whether you are self-employed or an employee.

    Learn more: How to get a CPP/EI ruling.

    If you are an employee, do not continue to next step.

  2. Mandatory registration if you are a taxi operator or a commercial ride-sharing driver

    If you are a self-employed taxi operator or a commercial ride-sharing driver, you have to register for a GST/HST account number even if you are not a small supplier.

    Effective date of registration

    You have to register for a GST/HST account number as of the date your business began as a taxi operator or a commercial ride-sharing driver.

    Learn more: Register for a GST/HST account number

  3. Charging the GST/HST

    You have to charge the GST/HST as of the date your business began.

    Learn more: Charge and collect the tax

Non-resident
What is a non-resident business

Non-resident means a person who does not reside in Canada. However, if a non-resident person carries on activities through a permanent establishment in Canada, the person is considered to be resident in Canada in respect of, and only in respect of, the activities of the person carried on through that permanent establishment.

You may be carrying on business  in Canada even if you do not have a permanent establishment  in Canada.

Learn more on how to make an enquiry if you are a non-resident business: Non-resident GST/HST enquiries

Steps

    • You sponsor (host) a convention  in Canada and more than 25% of the attendees are residents of Canada
    • You make taxable  sales, leases, or other supplies (including zero-rated  supplies) of admissions in Canada for a place of amusement, a seminar, an activity, or an event held in Canada

    • If you are a non-resident performer or a supplier of admission, you have to register for a GST/HST account number even if you are not a small supplier.

  2. Determine if you are carrying on a business in Canada

    You are considered to be carrying on a business  in Canada if:

    • You are a non-resident  business and you carry on activities through a permanent establishment  in Canada

      If you are carrying on a business in Canada, you may have to register for a GST/HST account if you are not a small supplier.

      Learn more: GST/HST Policy Statement P-051R2 - Carrying on business in Canada

    • You are a supplier of publications, and all of the following applies:
      • You solicit orders  for, or offers to sell prescribed publications , whether through an employee or agent or by means of advertising directed at the Canadian market
      • The prescribed publication will be sent by mail or courier to the recipient at an address in Canada

Meaning of the term worldwide taxable supplies

The term worldwide taxable supplies refers to:

  • Taxable supplies made in Canada
  • Supplies made outside Canada that would be taxable if they were made in Canada

Meaning of the term taxable supplies

Example of taxable supplies (other than zero-rated) includes:

  • Sales of new housing (certain sales of new housing may be subject to a previous rate of GST/HST)
  • Sales and rentals of commercial real property
  • Sales and leases of automobiles
  • Car repairs
  • Soft drinks, candies, and potato chips
  • Clothing and footwear
  • Advertising (unless provided to a non-resident of Canada who is not registered for the GST/HST)
  • Taxi or commercial ride-sharing services
  • Legal and accounting services
  • Franchises
  • Hotel accommodation
  • Barber and hairstylist services

Meaning of the term zero-rated supplies (taxable at the rate of 0%)

Example of supplies taxable at 0% includes:

  • Basic groceries such as milk, bread, and vegetables
  • Agricultural products such as grain, raw wool, and dried tobacco leaves
  • Most farm livestock
  • Most fishery products such as fish for human consumption
  • Prescription drugs and drug-dispensing services
  • Certain medical devices such as hearing aids and artificial teeth
  • Feminine hygiene products
  • Exports (most goods and services for which you charge and collect the GST/HST in Canada are zero-rated when exported)
  • Many transportation services where the origin or destination is outside Canada

Meaning of the term exempt supplies

Example of exempt supplies includes:

  • A sale of housing that was last used by an individual as a place of residence
  • Long-term rentals of residential accommodation (of one month or more) and residential condominium fees
  • Most health, medical, and dental services performed by licensed physicians or dentists for medical reasons
  • Child care services, where the primary purpose is to provide care and supervision to children 14 years of age or under for periods of less than 24 hours per day
  • Most domestic ferry services
  • Legal aid services
  • Many educational services such as:
    • Courses supplied by a vocational school leading to a certificate or a diploma that certifies the ability of individuals to practice or perform a trade or a vocation
    • Tutoring services made to an individual in a course that follows a curriculum designated by a school authority
  • Music lessons
  • Most services provided by financial institutions such as lending money or operating deposit accounts
  • The issuance of insurance policies by an insurer and the arranging for the issuance of insurance policies by insurance agents
  • Most property and services provided by charities and public institutions
  • Certain property and services provided by governments, non-profit organizations, municipalities, and other public service bodies including municipal transit services and standard residential services such as water distribution

Meaning of the term capital property

Examples of capital property include:

  • Real property such as:
    • A commercial building
    • Land (used in your business)
  • Assets such as:
    • Office furniture, computers, and business tools
    • Manufacturing equipment or machinery
  • Company vehicles such as a delivery truck
  • Intangible capital property such as:
    • Parents
    • Trademarks
    • Copyrights

Meaning of the term goodwill

Example – Financial services revenue

Examples of financial services revenue includes:

  • Interest Income such as:
    • Interest earned on loans or mortgages
    • Interest on bank deposits or investment accounts
  • Dividends received from corporate investments
  • Capital gains such as:
    • Proceeds from the sale of shares, bonds, or other securities
    • Proceeds from the sale of mutual funds or investment portfolios
  • Revenue from leasing financial instruments like securities or bonds
  • Fees for financial services such as:
    • Management fees for investment accounts
    • Bank service fees (for example: account maintenance, wire transfers)
  • Revenue from selling insurance policies (exempt from GST/HST)
  • Revenue from currency exchange activities
  • Royalties from financial instruments or intellectual property

Example – Grants or subsidies received

Examples of grants or subsidies received includes:

  • Government grants or funding such as:
    • Grants for job creation or employee training programs
    • Subsidies for research and development (R&D) activities
    • Funding for community or non-profit projects
  • Industry-specific subsidies:
    • Agricultural subsidies for crop production or farm improvements
    • Renewable energy grants for solar, wind, or other sustainable projects
  • Export or trade subsidies:
    • Funding provided to promote export activities
    • Subsidies to offset costs of international trade shows or marketing
  • Environmental or infrastructure grants:
    • Subsidies for environmental protection or cleanup projects
    • Funding for public infrastructure development, like transportation or utilities
  • Grants to support community services or charitable activities
  • Funding for schools, colleges, or universities to support programs or facilities
GST/HST Treatment
  • Most grants or subsidies are not subject to GST/HST, provided they are not considered payment for a taxable supply
  • If a grant or subsidy is directly tied to the provision of goods or services, it may be taxable under the GST/HST rules
  • Recipients must assess the nature of the funding.
