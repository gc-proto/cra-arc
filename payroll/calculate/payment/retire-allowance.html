Payments of retiring allowances

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may pay a retiring allowance to your employee. Retiring allowances are amounts paid to officers or employees when they retire from an office or employment or after, in recognition of long service or for the loss of office or employment.

Depending whether the recipient is a Canadian resident or a non-resident of Canada, your income tax withholding and reporting will be different.

Steps

  1. Determine if the payment is considered a retiring allowance

    1. If you make one of the following payments, it is considered a retiring allowance:

      • Payments for unused sick-leave credits on termination
      • Amounts received when their office or employment is terminated, even if the amount is for damages for (wrongful dismissal when the employee does not return to work)
      • Payments made after retirement or termination in recognition of service
      What is not considered a retiring allowance

      A retiring allowance does not include:

      • Salary, wages, bonuses, overtime, and legal fees
      • Salary continuance
      • Superannuation or pension benefit
      • Amount an individual receives as a result of an employee's death (these payments may be treated as death benefits)
      • Benefit derived from certain counselling services
      • Payments for accumulated vacation leave not taken before retirement
      • Wages in lieu of termination notice
      • Damages for violations or alleged violations of an employee's applicable human rights awarded under human rights legislation, to the extent these amounts are not taxable

      Learn more: Income Tax Folio S2-F1-C2, Retiring Allowances

      • If the payment is not considered a retiring allowance, do not continue to the next step.

        Refer to: Determine the tax treatment of payments other than regular employment income

      • If the payment is:

        • Damages for loss of employment which were paid with wages in lieu of termination notice, continue to: Step 1b - Review if the full payment would be considered a retiring allowance.
        • A retiring allowance where were not paid with wages in lieu of termination notice, continue to: Step 2 - Determine which deductions you need to withhold.
    2. Review if the full payment would be considered a retiring allowance
      • If you paid both wages in lieu of termination notice and damages for loss of employment, the portion of the payment for:
        • Wages in lieu of termination is considered employment income
        • Damages are generally considered a retiring allowance
      • If you made a lump-sum payment to your employee and the amounts were not separated or were paid together through a court award or settlement, the full amount is generally considered a retiring allowance

      • If the amount paid with the damages for loss of employment is considered wages in lieu of termination notice, do not continue to the next step for this amount.

        Refer to: Payments of wages in lieu of termination notice

      • If the full or a portion of payment is considered a retiring allowance, continue to: Step 2 - Determine if your employee is eligible to transfer directly the retiring allowance.

  2. Determine if your employee is eligible to transfer directly the retiring allowance

    Your income tax withholding and reporting obligations will be different depending wether the payment, or the portion of the payment, is considered an eligible or non-eligible retiring allowance.

    1. Confirm your employee is eligible to transfer all or part of a retiring allowance

      If your employee with years of service before 1996, they may be able to directly transfer all or part of a retiring allowance to:

      • Registered pension plan (RPP)
      • Registered retirement savings plan (RRSP)
      • Specified pension plan (SPP)
      • Pooled registered pension plan (PRPP)

      If you transfer the amount to an RPP, you may have to report a pension adjustment.

      • If the transfer is for an RPP or RRSP for 1996 and later years, your employee is not eligible to transfer a retiring allowance, continue to: Step 3 - Determine which deductions you need to withhold.

      • If the transfer is for an RPP or RRSP before 1996, continue to: Step 2b - Determine the amount eligible to transfer.

    2. Determine the amount eligible to transfer

      Use the following to calculate the maximum amount that is eligible to be transferred to an RRSP or RPP:

      1. Determine the amount eligible to transfer before 1996

      • Each year or part of a year before 1996 your employee or former employee worked for you (or a person related to you)
      • multiply by $2,000
      • equals Amount eligible to transfer before 1996

      2. Determine the amount eligible to transfer before 1989

      • Each year or part of a year before 1989 of that employment in which none of your contributions to a pension plan or deferred profit sharing plan (DPSP) were vested in the employee’s name when you paid the retiring allowance
      • multiply by $1,500
      • equals Amount eligible to transfer before 1989

      3. Determine the amount eligible for transfer

      • Amount eligible to transfer before 1996
      • plus Amount eligible to transfer before 1989
      • equals Total amount eligible for transfer

      4. Determine the amount eligible for transfer paid over one or more years

      • Total amount eligible for transfer (result of 3.)
      • minus The eligible portion transferred in a prior year
      • equals Total amount eligible for transfer paid over one or more years to be included on the T4 slip using code 66

      5. Determine the non-eligible amount

      • Entire amount of retiring allowance
      • minus Total amount eligible for transfer (result of 4.)
      • equals Total non-eligible amount to be included on the T4 slip using code 67
      Example

      In November 2025, the employer pays their previous employee a retiring allowance of $50,000. The employee worked for the employer from 1990 to 2025 (35 years, including part-years of service). The employee did not contribute to a pension plan or DPSP.

      1. Determine the amount eligible to transfer before 1996

      • 10 year or part of a year before 1996 the employee worked for the employer
      • multiply by $2,000
      • equals $20,000 is the amount eligible to transfer before 1996

      2. Determine the amount eligible to transfer before 1989

      • 3 year or part of a year before 1989 the employee worked for the employer
      • multiply by $1,500
      • equals $4,500 is the amount eligible to transfer before 1989

      3. Determine the amount eligible for transfer

      • $20,000 is the amount eligible to transfer before 1996
      • plus $4,500 is the amount eligible to transfer before 1989
      • equals $24,500 is the total amount eligible for transfer to be included on the T4 slip using code 66

      4. Determine the non-eligible amount

      • $50,000 is the entire amount of retiring allowance
      • minus $24,500 is the total amount eligible for transfer (result of 3.)
      • equals $25,500 is the non-eligible amount to be included on the T4 slip using code 67

      • If you paid a non-eligible retiring allowance to your First Nations employee related to their tax-exempt income, the non-eligible amount must be included on the T4 slip using code 69. Do not continue to the next step.

        Refer to: Payments to First Nations workers

      • If the employee is not a First Nations employee, continue to: Step 3 - Determine which deductions you need to withhold.

  3. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    You must withhold the following deductions depending on the nature of the payment:

    • CPP contributions - Do not withhold
    • EI premiums - Do not withhold
    • Income tax - Your income tax withholding and reporting obligations will be different depending wether the retiring allowance is paid out or is transferred to an RPP or RRSP

    • Retiring allowance paid out

      If the retiring allowance is paid out, it is subject to the following lump-sum withholding rates depending on your employee’s residency status:

      • Canadian resident

        You must calculate using the withholding rate that matches your employee’s total amount of retiring allowances paid or expected to be paid in the calendar year:

        • 10% (5% for Quebec), $5,000 or less
        • 20% (10% for Quebec), $5,001 to $15,000
        • 30% (15% for Quebec), $15,001 or more

        Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for using the lump-sum payments method

      • Non-resident of Canada

        You must calculate using the 25% Part XIII tax rate, unless a tax treaty between Canada and that country reduces this rate

        Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for retiring allowances (considered a lump-sum payment) using the Non-resident tax calculator

    • Retiring allowance transferred to an RPP or RRSP

      If the retiring allowance is a direct transfer to an RPP or RRSP, you do not withhold income tax on the amount eligible (step 2b) of the retiring allowance.

      You do not need a letter of authority from the CRA to reduce the tax withheld from the amounts of the payment that were transferred to your employee's RRSP or RPP if they gave you a written statement.

      The amounts that are eligible and transferred into your employee's plan does not impact the deduction limit.

  4. Report the payment on a slip

    You must report payments on a T4 or NR4 slip depending on your employee’s residency status:

    • Canadian resident

      You must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Code 66 - Eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 67 - Non-eligible retiring allowances

      Learn more: T4 slip – Information for employers

    • Non-resident of Canada

      You must report the following amounts on a NR4 slip:

      • Box 16 or 26 - Gross income
      • Box 18 or 28 - Exemption code

      Learn more: NR4 slip – Statement of amounts paid or credited to non-residents of Canada
