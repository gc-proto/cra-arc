Payments of retiring allowances

Content to be covered: Retiring allowances and Transfer of a retiring allowance

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

If you provide a retiring allowance (severance pay) to your employee, you have to report the payment and deductions on a T4 slip. Depending whether the recipient is a Canadian resident or a non-resident of Canada, your income tax withholding and reporting will be different.

Steps

  1. Determine if the payment is considered a retiring allowance

    If you make one of the following payments, it is considered a retiring allowance:

    • Payments for unused sick-leave credits on termination
    • Amounts individuals receive when their office or employment is terminated, even if the amount is for damages (wrongful dismissal when the employee does not return to work)
    Definitions
    Damages

    An amount paid as a result of a judgment or of a settlement in compensation for a loss, or a moral or physical injury. In wrongful dismissal actions, when the worker is not reinstated, the amount allocated is "damages" since under such circumstances, the amount is generally paid to compensate a loss of reputation and income, rather than to put the person back in the same position as if nothing had taken place during the period the person was not working.

    Payment for long service

    An amount paid by the employer to an employee that is related to the time the employee spent in that employment.

    Payment for loss of office or employment

    An amount paid by an employer to an employee for the only reason that the employer has terminated the contract of employment (it is important to note that such a payment would not be made in the case of a temporary lay-off).

    Payment for long service

    An amount paid by the employer to an employee that is related to the time the employee spent in that employment.

    Pay in lieu of notice

    An amount paid by the employer to an employee in cases where an employee is dismissed or laid off without the necessary time notification prescribed by the applicable statute; the amount represents the earnings that the employee would have earned if the employer had given him or her the required notice prior to dismissal or lay-off.

    Retiring allowance

    As defined in subsection 1(1) IECPR, a "retiring allowance" means an amount received by a person.

    • (a) on or after retirement of the person from an office or employment in recognition of the person's long service, or
    • (b) in respect of a loss of an office or employment of the person, whether or not received as, on account or in lieu of payment of, damages or pursuant to an order or judgment of a competent tribunal.
    Severance pay

    This term usually encompasses more than one type of payment made by an employer when the contract of employment of the employee is terminated; for this reason, it is important to try to determine the exact nature of the various payments.

    • If the payment is not one of the above, it is not considered a retiring allowance. Do not continue to next step.

      Refer to:

      • Bonuses, retroactive pay increases or irregular amounts
      • Payments related to pension or superannuation
      • Payments related to the death of your employee
      • Payments of wages in lieu of termination notice
      • Vacation pay and public holiday payments

    • If the payment is one of the above, it is considered a retiring allowance payment.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the lump-sum payment you make.

  2. Determine if the retiring allowance is considered damages

    You need to determine if the retiring allowance is considered damages. This is because damages may be included or excluded from retiring allowances.

    What is considered damages

    Amounts paid on account of damages to an employee or former employee (for example, a payment made under a grievance settlement, arbitration decision or court order) could be considered one or more of the following:

    • Employment income
    • Retiring allowance
    • Non-taxable damages

    Determining the tax treatment of the damages requires a review of all relevant facts. For example, if a human rights tribunal awards an individual an amount for general damages, the amount is normally considered non-taxable damages and is not required to be included in income.

  3. Determine if your employee is eligible to transfer directly the retiring allowance

  4. Determine if you need to report on a slip a direct transfer of a lump-sum payment from an RPP or DPSP

    If you make a lump-sum payment that is transferred directly from an RPP or a DPSP to one of the following plans, you do not have to report the payment on a slip:

    • ALDA
    • DPSP - Only if the transfer is from another DPS
    • PRPP
    • RPP
    • RRIF
    • RRSP
    • SPP

    If the lump-sum payment was transferred directly from an RPP or DPSP to any of the above plans, do not continue to next step. If the lump-sum payment was made to a:

    • Canadian resident

      You can be use one of the following forms to record the direct transfer:

      • Form T2151, Direct Transfer of a Single Amount Under Subsection 147(19) or Section 147.3
      • Form T2157, Direct Transfer from a Registered Plan to Purchase an ALDA

    • Non-resident of Canada

      You must fill out the following form to waive the requirement to withhold non-resident tax:

      • Form NRTA1, Authorization for Non-Resident Tax Exemption

    Learn more: Transfer of funds

    If the direct transfer is not a an eligible direct transfer of a lump-sum payment or exceeds the limit which results in a pension benefit, continue to Step 4 - Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLSP).

  5. Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLSP)

    1. Determine if the payment is a QRLSP

      If you make a lump-sum payment, it is a QRLSP if all of the following conditions apply:

      • Payment is a qualifying amount
        What are qualifying amounts

        Qualifying amounts

        A qualifying amount is the principal portion of a payment or benefit that is eligible for the special tax calculation:

        Eligible source of income are:

        • Income from an office or employment received under one of the following:
          • An order or judgment from a court or other competent tribunal
          • An arbitration award
          • A lawsuit settlement agreement (including damages for loss of office or employment)
        • Benefits from unemployment insurance or employment insurance
        • Benefits from a superannuation or pension plan , including from a foreign pension plan and Old Age Security (other than non-periodic benefits such as lump-sum withdrawals)
        • Spousal, common-law partner, or taxable child support payments
        • Benefits from a wage-loss replacement plan
        • Canadian Forces members' and veterans' income replacement benefits

        Amounts not qualifying

        An amount received under any part of a retroactive lump-sum payment that is eligible for any of the following deductions, is not a qualifying amount:

        • Legal expenses
        • Salary reimbursements
        • Reimbursement of top-up disability payments
        • Repayment of pension or benefits
        • Deduction of social assistance payments, workers' compensation benefits

        An amount under normal collective bargaining, such as negotiated back pay, is not a qualifying amount.

        A different tax treatment may apply if the employee is deceased.

      • Payment has been made after 1994
      • Payment relates to one or more prior eligible tax years
        What is an eligible tax year

        An eligible tax year is a previous tax year that ended after 1977, during which the recipient:

        • Was a resident of Canada
        • Did not become a bankrupt during the calendar year
      • The sum of the payments relating to the preceding years is at least $3,000, not including interest (if a person receives more than one type of QRLSP in a year, the $3,000 threshold applies to the total amount of QRLSP, not to each individually)

      • If the payment does not meet all of the above conditions, it is not a QRLSP. It is a lump-sum payment.

        Continue to: Step 5 - Withhold deductions from the lump-sum payment.

      • If the payment meets all of the above conditions, it is a QRLSP.

        Continue to: Step 4b - Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198 if the payment is a QRLSP.

    2. Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198 if the lump-sum payment is a QRLSP

      If the payment is a QRLSP (step 4a), the recipient can request a special tax calculation be applied to their income tax and benefit return.

      What is the special tax calculation

      Generally, a lump-sum payment is included in income in the year the recipient receives it. This may result in a greater tax liability than if the payment had been received in the year or years to which it related.

      A special tax calculation is available to recipients who receive a QRLSP that relate to any year after 1977. The calculation applies if the total of all principal amounts that relate to prior years (after 1977) from all QRLSPs is $3,000 or more.

      You (as the payer) must provide to the recipient one of the following to help the CRA decide if the special tax calculation is beneficial to them:

      • A completed Form T1198, Statement of Qualifying Retroactive Lump-Sum Payment

      • A letter with the following information:

        • The year in which the lump-sum payment was made to the recipient
        • A complete description of the lump-sum payment and the circumstances that required it to be paid
        • The total amount of the lump-sum payment, including a breakdown between the principal and the interest element, if any, of the payment
        • The principal amount of the lump-sum payment that relates to the current year and each of the preceding years covered by the payment

      It is the recipient's responsibility to send Form T1198 or the letter from the payer to their tax centre and request the special tax calculation be applied to their personal income tax and benefit return.

  6. Withhold deductions from the lump-sum payment

    You must withhold the following deductions from the lump-sum payment depending on the type of payment you made:

    • Lump-sum payment (other than QRLSP)

      • CPP - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • Income tax - Calculate using the lump-sum payments method unless the following situations apply

        Do not withhold income tax if:

        • The recipient's total earnings received or receivable during the calendar year, including the lump-sum payment, are less than the claim amount on their Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return
        • The payment is one of the following:
          • A lump-sum payment (such as a refund of premiums) paid to a deceased annuitant's spouse or common law partner
          • The minimum amount paid out of a RRIF to the original annuitant

    • QRLSP

      • CPP - Calculate using the bonus or irregular payments method
      • EI - Calculate using the any payments (manual calculation) method
      • Income tax - Calculate using the bonus or irregular payments method

  7. How to calculate the tax deduction

    You have to calculate the tax deduction using one of the following tax rate depending on the recipient’s residency status:

    • Canadian resident

      You must calculate using the 15% income tax rate if the patronage allocations are $100 or more.

      Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for patronage allocations (dividents) using the bonus or irregular payments method

    • Non-resident of Canada

      You must calculate using the 25% Part XIII tax rate, unless a tax treaty between Canada and that country reduces this rate

      Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for retiring allowances (considered a lump-sum payment) using the Non-resident tax calculator

  8. Report the payment on a slip

    You must report payments on a T4 or NR4 slip depending on the recipient’s residency status:

    • Canadian resident

      You must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Code 66 - Eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 67 - Non-eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 69 - Indian Act (exempt income) – Non-eligible retiring allowances

      Learn how to report the payment: T4A slip – Information for payers

    • Non-resident of Canada

      You must report the following amounts on a NR4 slip:

      • Box 16 or 26 - Gross income
      • Box 17 or 27 - Non-resident tax withheld
      • Box 18 or 28 - Exemption code

      Learn how to report the payment: NR4 slip – Statement of amounts paid or credited to non-residents of Canada
