Payments of retiring allowances

Content to be covered: Retiring allowances and Transfer of a retiring allowance

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may pay a retiring allowance to your employee or a worker when they retire in recognition of long service or for the loss of office or employment.

Depending whether the recipient is a Canadian resident or a non-resident of Canada, your income tax withholding and reporting will be different.

Steps

  1. Determine if the payment is considered a retiring allowance

    If you make one of the following payments, it is considered a retiring allowance:

    • Payments for unused sick-leave credits on termination
    • Amounts received when their office or employment is terminated, even if the amount is for damages for (wrongful dismissal when the employee does not return to work)
    • Payments made after retirement or termination in recognition of service
    What is not considered a retiring allowance

    A retiring allowance does not include:

    • Salary, wages, bonuses, overtime, and legal fees
    • Salary continuance
    • A superannuation or pension benefit
    • An amount an individual receives as a result of an employee's death (these payments may be treated as death benefits)
    • A benefit derived from certain counselling services
    • Payments for accumulated vacation leave not taken before retirement
    • wages in lieu of termination notice
    • Damages for violations or alleged violations of an employee's applicable human rights awarded under human rights legislation, to the extent these amounts are not taxable

    Learn more: Income Tax Folio S2-F1-C2, Retiring Allowances

    • If the payment is not considered a retiring allowance, do not continue to the next step.

      Refer to: Determine the tax treatment of payments other than regular employment income

    • If the payment is a retiring allowance, continue to: Step 2 - Determine if damages for loss of employment are paid with wages in lieu of termination notice.

  2. Determine if damages for loss of employment are paid with wages in lieu of termination notice

    • If you paid both wages in lieu of termination notice and damages for loss of employment , the portion of the payment for:
      • Wages in lieu of termination is considered employment income
      • Damages are generally considered a retiring allowance
    • If you made a lump-sum payment to your employee and the amounts were not separated or paid together through a court award or settlement, the full amount is generally considered a retiring allowance

    • If the portion of the payment may be considered wages in lieu of termination notice, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more: Payments of wages in lieu of termination notice.

    • If the portion of the payment is considered a retiring allowance, continue to: Step 2 - Determine if the payment is for damages related to loss of employment.

  3. Determine if the payment is for damages related to loss of employment

    You need to determine if you paid your employee for damages related to loss of employment.

    If you are not sure if the damages are related to loss of employment

    Determining the tax treatment of the damages requires a review of all relevant facts. For example, if a human rights tribunal awards an individual an amount for general damages, the amount is normally considered non-taxable damages and is not required to be included in income.

    You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the damages are related to loss of employment.

    Learn more: CPP/EI explained - How to get a CPP/EI ruling

    • If the payment is for damages that is not related to the loss of employment and are awarded under the human rights legislation or for personal injury, it is not considered a retiring allowance. Do not continue to the next step.

      Do not withhold deductions and do not report these amounts on a T4 slip.

    • If the payment is for damages that is related to the loss of employment, it is considered a retiring allowance.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the lump-sum payment you make.

  4. Determine if your employee is eligible to transfer directly the retiring allowance

    Income tax withholdings may vary depending on whether or not the amount is considered an eligible or non-eligible retiring allowance.

    • Eligible retiring allowance - Code 66

      Amounts that can be transferred to an RRSP or RPP without affecting your employee's RRSP contribution room, this includes:

      • $2,000 for each year of service before 1996
      • Additional $1,500 for each year before 1989 if no pension or DPSP benefits were vested

    • Non-eligible retiring allowance - Code 67

      Amounts that cannot be transferred to an RRSP without using contribution room

  5. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    You must withhold the following deductions depending on the nature of the payment:

    • Retiring allowance paid

      • CPP - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • Income tax - You must calculate the income tax deductions depending on your employee’s residency status:

        • Canadian resident

          • Income tax - Calculate using the withholding rates that matches your employee’s total amount of the retiring allowance:

            • 10% (5% for Quebec), $5,000 or less
            • 20% (10% for Quebec), $5,001 to $15,000
            • 30% (15% for Quebec), $15,001 or more

            Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for using the lump-sum payments method

        • Non-resident of Canada

          • Income tax - Calculate using the 25% Part XIII tax rate, unless a tax treaty between Canada and that country reduces this rate

            Learn how to calculate income tax deductions for retiring allowances (considered a lump-sum payment) using the Non-resident tax calculator

    • Transfer of an eligible retiring allowance

      • CPP - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • Income tax on eligible portion - Do not withhold

  6. Report the payment on a slip

    You must report payments on a T4 or NR4 slip depending on your employee’s residency status:

    • Canadian resident

      You must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Code 66 - Eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 67 - Non-eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 69 - Indian Act (exempt income) – Non-eligible retiring allowances

      Learn how to report the payment: T4 slip – Information for employers

    • Non-resident of Canada

      You must report the following amounts on a NR4 slip:

      • Box 16 or 26 - Gross income
      • Box 17 or 27 - Non-resident tax withheld
      • Box 18 or 28 - Exemption code

      Learn how to report the payment: NR4 slip – Statement of amounts paid or credited to non-residents of Canada
