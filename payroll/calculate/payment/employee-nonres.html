Content to be covered: Non-resident employees who carry out services in Canada

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may have non-resident employees who are working in Canada.

The following needs to be covered on this webpage:

  • Exemptions based on a tax treaty agreement
  • Seasonal Agricultural Workers Program

Steps

  1. Determine the residency status of your employee

    Where the income is from employment, you need to consider your employee's residency status.

    If you don't know the residency status 

    Learn more: Determine the province of employment (POE)

    • If your employee is a resident of Canada, do not continue to next step.

    • If your employee is not a resident throughout the year or is a deemed resident or sojourner, continue to: Step 2 - Determine your (as an employer) residency status.

  2. Determine your (as an employer) residency status

    Where the income is from employment, you need to consider your residency status.

    Learn more:

    • Determine the province of employment (POE)
    • Residency of a corporation

    • If you are a non-resident employer, do not continue to next step.

      Learn more: Employees who are working in Canada for a non-resident employer

    • If you are a Canadian employer, continue to: Step 3 - Determine if any exceptions apply to your employee's situation.

  3. Determine if any exceptions apply to your employee's situation

    Depending on the situation, your withholding and reporting obligations may be different. For example:

    • Your employee works at your foreign embassy in Canada
    • Directors' fees paid to non-residents of Canada
    • Your non-resident employee is attending conferences in Canada

  4. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    If you paid employment income to your non-resident employee who is working in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following applies:

    1. Determine if Canada has a reciprocal social security agreement with other countries

      Canada has reciprocal social security agreements with other countries. These agreements ensure that only one plan covers an employe: the CPP or a foreign social security plan.

      If there is a social security agreement between Canada and the country where your employee is working, that agreement must be considered to determine whether the employment is pensionable.

      Each social security agreement sets a limit to the duration of coverage for a detachment.

      Learn more: Canada’s social agreements with other countries.

      • If Canada has a social security agreement with other countries, and the agreement allows for employment outside Canada to be pensionable in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        You must also keep the certificate of coverage to confirm the employment is pensionable in Canada.

        Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If Canada does not have social security agreement with other countries, continue to: Step 3b - Determine if you or your employee can apply for CPP coverage.

    2. Determine if you or your employee can apply for CPP coverage

      Do not deduct CPP if the above conditions are not met.

  6. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you paid employment income to your non-resident employee who is working in Canada, you must deduct income tax as you would for your other employees, unless your non-resident employee:

    • Received a Regulation 102 waiver
    • Claiming a tax deduction on a individual Canadian income tax return due to the tax treaty
    Special situation: Working at foreign embassies in Canada

    Your employee may be working at your foreign embassy in Canada. Depending on your employee situation, your withholding and reporting obligations are different.

    Learn more: Tax information for individuals working at foreign embassies in Canada

  7. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you paid employment income to your non-resident employee who is working in Canada, you must report the following amounts on a T4 slip in the pay period they were received or enjoyed:

    • Box 14 - Employment Income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings

    A slip must always be issued if any tax is withheld. The CRA administrative policy does not apply where tax is withheld, even if the total of all payments is under $500.

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers
