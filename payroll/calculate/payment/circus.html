Payments to workers in connection with a circus, fair, parade, carnival, exposition, exhibition, or other similar activity

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the CRA's treatment of payments to workers in connection with a circus, fair, parade or similar activity.

You may hire a worker in connection with a circus, fair, parade, carnival, exposition, exhibition, or other similar activity . This means that the services performed are related to, connected to, and having to do with the event.

Your withholding obligations will depend on if the employee is regularly employed by you and how long they work for you during the year.

Definitions
Carnival

A carnival is a travelling fair with exhibits, games, rides, and other amusements.

Circus

A circus is a travelling show of performing animals, acrobats, clowns, and other similar shows.

Examples of well-known circuses are the Shrine Circus and the Cirque du Soleil.

Entertainer

For the purposes of employment in connection with circus, fair, parade or similar activity, an entertainer is a professional stage performer or performing artist who uses their creative talent and imagination for form or effect. Examples include:

  • Acrobats
  • Clowns
  • Comedians
  • Contortionists
  • Dancers
  • Illusionists
  • Jugglers
  • Magicians
  • Musicians
  • Singers
  • Stilt-walkers
  • Trapeze artists
Exhibition

An exhibition is a large regional fair, especially with amusements, agricultural exhibits, and craft displays, usually lasting for an extended period, a world’s fair.

Exposition

An exposition is a large public exhibition. For example, a food fair is an exposition with displays relating to various aspects of food.

Fair

A fair is an annual exhibition of produce, livestock, crafts, and other similar exhibitions held especially in rural areas in conjunction with a travelling midway (fall fair); an exhibition, especially to promote particular products (trade fair); a periodical gathering for the sale of goods, often with entertainments.

The Canadian Association of Fairs and Exhibitions website lists many examples of local fairs and exhibitions.

Other similar activity

Other similar activity include pet and horse shows, beauty pageants, flower or garden shows, and music or theatre festivals.

Parade

A parade is a public march or procession that is conducted in a formal, ceremonial, or celebratory manner.

Steps

  1. Determine if the worker is an employee or self-employed

    If you pay the worker for a service in connection with a circus, fair, parade, carnival, exposition, exhibition, or other similar activity , the worker can either be an employee or a self-employed worker.

    • If the worker is a self-employed worker, do not continue to the next step.

      You do not withhold CPP, EI or income tax deductions.

      Generally, if you provide payments to a self-employed worker for their services and you are not considered their employer, you need to report the amounts on a T4A slip.

      Learn more: Payments of fees for services

      If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed for CPP/EI purposes

      You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or is self-employed. You can use the CRA guidance to help you gather the facts based on your situation.

      Learn more about the factors the CRA considers when determining a worker’s employment status: Employee or Self-employed

    • If the worker is an employee, continue to Step 2 - Determine if your employee is considered to be an entertainer.

  2. Determine if your employee is considered to be an entertainer

    • If your employee is considered an entertainer, do not continue to the next step.

      You have to deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees.

      Learn more: How to calculate

    • If your employee is not an entertainer, continue to Step 3 - Determine if the employment is pensionable and insurable.

  3. Determine if the employment is pensionable and insurable

    The employment of your employee in this situation will not be pensionable or insurable if the employment meets both of the following conditions:

    • The employee is not regularly employed by you
    • The employee works for you for less than seven days in the year

    Learn more if you are not sure if the individual is considered a regular employee: CPP/EI explained, Rescue operations-Abating a disaster

    • If the employment meets all conditions above, the employment is pensionable or insurable from the first day of work.

    • If the employment does not meet all conditions above, the employment is not pensionable or insurable.

      Where the employment is not pensionable, your employee can elect to pay CPP contributions on these earnings using Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

  4. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    You must withhold the following deductions on amounts paid to your employee in connection with a circus, fair, parade or similar activity:

    • Pensionable and insurable

      • Income tax
      • CPP contributions
      • EI premiums

    • Not pensionable and insurable

      • Income tax
      • CPP contributions (do not withhold)
      • EI premiums (do not withhold)

    The amounts you withhold must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Learn more: How to calculate

  5. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    You must report the following on amounts paid to your employee in connection with a circus, fair, parade or similar activity on a T4 slip:

    • Pensionable and insurable

      • Box 14 - Employment income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings

    • Not pensionable and insurable

      • Box 14 - Employment income

    Learn more: How to report - T4 slip - Information for employers
