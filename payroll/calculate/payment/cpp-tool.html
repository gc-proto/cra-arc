Full-text to determine the CPP

The following is the alternate format to the above questions or results, and provides the same information in full-text.

Employee who turns 18

You must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after the employee turns 18.

Example

Judith turns 18 on June 27. CPP deductions begin on the first pay period with a pay date of July 1 or later of that same year, even if this pay period includes days worked prior to Judith’s 18th birthday.

CPP contributions are calculated on the entire pay period as long as the pay date falls after the month the employee turns 18 years of age.

Employee at least 18 but under 65 years of age

You must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after the employee turns 18.

CPP contributions must continue until the employee turns 65 years of age.

Employee is between 65 and 69 years old

CPP contributions must continue until the employee turns 70 years of age unless the employee begins receiving CPP/QPP retirement pension and files an application to cease CPP contributions.

You must stop deducting CPP contributions if:

  • Your employee is between 65 and 69 years old
  • Gives you a completed Form CPT30 Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election during the year

Employee who turns 70

You must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee turns 70.

Example

Kamal turned 70 years of age on February 14. As a result, no pay period with a pay date later than February should have CPP contributions withheld.

Employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP

An employee who is considered to be disabled under the CPP does not have to contribute to the CPP.

When to stop deducting if your employee is now considered disabled

You must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee is considered to be disabled according to Service Canada.

How to calculate the prorating

When prorating, you must use the number of months up to and including the month the employee was considered to be disabled.

Example

Joanne is considered disabled under the CPP as of June of this year, even though she will not begin receiving her CPP disability pension until October due to the waiting period. On July 5, her employer will be paying her final hours worked in June before becoming disabled, as well as some sick days and outstanding vacation pay.

No CPP should be deducted for this pay period since it has a pay date of after June. CPP deductions do not restart until the employee is no longer considered disabled under the CPP/QPP.

When to start deducting if your employee is no longer considered disabled

If the employee is no longer considered disabled, you must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after the employee is no longer considered disabled.

How to calculate the prorating

When prorating, you must count the number of months starting with the month after the employee ceased to be disabled.

Deceased employee

You must prorate the maximum CPP contribution deductions when an employee dies in the year.
