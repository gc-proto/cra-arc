Payments related to tenure of office (elected or appointed officials)

You may be looking for:

  • Payments of fees for services

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement or to the CRA administrative policy.

You may pay stipends, director’s or trustee’s fees or remuneration to a person who is in tenure of office. Your withholding and reporting requirements will depend on the type of income paid and whether it is paid alongside other salary and wages.

When are executor, administrator and power of attorney's fees considered fees for services

Executor fees

An executor is an individual or trust institution named in a will and confirmed by a court to settle a deceased's estate. Generally in situations where an individual dies without a will, or the will does not name an executor, a court will appoint an administrator to handle the deceased's estate. Often, executors and administrators are paid a fee for their services.

Power of attorney fees

When someone has been delegated as a power of attorney, they may be paid a fee for their services. The deductions for the fees paid are treated the same as for executor fees.

Deductions and reporting

Generally, fees paid to an executor, administrator, or a person who has a delegated power of attorney are either considered business income or income from employment or tenure of office.

Steps

  1. Determine if the recipient is considered to be in tenure of office

    For CPP and EI purposes, an individual is considered to be in tenure of office where the individual is both:

    • Elected by popular vote or appointed
      Common examples of elected or appointed officials
      • Directors
      • Mayors
      • Municipal councillors
      • School commissioners
      • Chiefs of Indian bands
      • Band councillors
      • Executors
      • Liquidators
      • Administrators of estates
      • Members of a board of referee at the Commission of Canada Employment Insurance
      • Any other position when a person is elected or appointed to that office
    • Entitled to a fixed or ascertainable stipend or remuneration

    • If the payments were not made to someone who is in tenure of office, do not continue to the next step.

      If the payments were made to a worker for acting as an executor or administrator  in the ordinary course of business, this is generally considered business income, refer to: Payments of fees for services.

    • If the payments were made to an individual in tenure of office, continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions.

  2. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    • You must deduct CPP contributions from the following amounts:

      • Honorariums from employment or office
      • A share of profit that an employer paid
      • Incentive payments
      • Directors’ fees, management fees, fees paid to board or committee members, and executor’s, liquidator’s, or administrator’s fees earned to administer an estate (as long as the executor, liquidator, or administrator does not act in this capacity in the regular course of business)
      • Stipends, fees or remuneration paid to elected or appointed officials

    • Do not deduct CPP contributions from the following amounts:

      • Stipends, fees or remuneration paid to a chief of an Indian band or band councillors, unless an application for coverage under CPP has been completed
      • Employment income of an individual in tenure of office in a provincial government department or agency if their employment is listed in Schedule IV of the Canada Pension Plan Regulations

    How to calculate

    When deducting CPP contributions for director’s fees paid alongside salary, the fees are added to the salary for that pay period to calculate the amount to deduct. If the fees are not paid alongside salary, the basic CPP exemption must be prorated over the number of terms you pay the fees during the year when calculating the CPP to deduct.

    Learn more on how to calculate CPP contributions using the: Regular payments at irregular intervals method

  3. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    • You must deduct EI premiums from the following amounts:

      • Employment income of a person holding an office in the private sector where the individual is in a tenure of office in a union or an association of unions
      • Stipends, fees or remuneration paid to elected or appointed officials who hold an office in any federal department or agency listed in Schedules I, II, III, IV or V of the Financial Administration Act
      • The employment income of individuals who hold an office in the provincial government if they are appointed and remunerated under an act governing the public service of a province
      • The employment income of individuals who hold an office for a corporation, a commission, or another body that is an agent to Her Majesty in right of a province, even if they are not appointed and remunerated under an act governing the public service of a province

    • Do not deduct EI premiums from the following amounts:

      • Director’s fees paid to board or committee members of a corporation
      • Director’s fees paid to a corporation or partnership
      • Income you pay to:
        • Mayors
        • Municipal councillors
        • School commissioners
        • Chiefs of Indian bands
        • Band councillors
        • Executors, liquidators, or administrators of an estate
        • Corporation directors
        • Any person elected or appointed to an office in the private sector, other than in a union or association of unions
        • Ministers
        • Members of the House of Commons
        • Members of the Senate
        • The Lieutenant Governor
        • Judges
        • Magistrates
        • Members of a board of referees at the Commission of Canada Employment Insurance
        • Prothonotaries of the Federal Court of Canada
        • Individuals who hold an office in the provincial government when they are not appointed and remunerated under an act governing the public service of a province

    • If all of the above conditions are not met or if the above special situation applies, do not deduct EI premiums.

    • If all of the above conditions are met, you must deduct EI premiums.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  4. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    • You must deduct income tax from the following amounts:

      • Salary, wages, bonuses, taxable stock option benefits or other remuneration
      • Honorariums, share of profits an employer pays, incentive payments, directors’ fees, management fees, fees paid to board or committee members, and executor’s, liquidator’s, or administrator’s fees earned to administer an estate (as long as the executor, liquidator, or administrator does not act in this capacity in the regular course of business)
      • Director’s fees paid to a non-resident corporation or partnership at a rate of 15% unless they have provided you with an approved Regulation 105 waiver
      • Stipends, fees or remuneration paid to elected or appointed officials

    • Do not deduct income tax from the following amounts:

      • Director’s fees paid to a non-resident director for attending a meeting from outside Canada through electronic means (such as a teleconference) as the director is not considered to be employed in Canada
      • The share of the annual fees paid to a non-resident director that were not done in Canada where the services rendered were only partly done in Canada. Example: If you held ten meetings during the year and the non-resident director attended five meetings in Canada, you would deduct income tax at source on one-half of the flat annual amount paid to the non-resident director.
      • Director’s fees where only director’s fees are paid if the total fees paid are estimated to be less than the amount claimed on the director’s Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return (or the basic personal amount if they did not provide you a TD1)
      • Director’s fees paid to a resident corporation or partnership

    Learn more on how to calculate income tax using the: Bonus or irregular payments method

  5. Report the payment on a slip

    You must report payments on a T4 or NR4 slip depending on the recipient’s residency status:

    • Canadian resident

      If the payment is made to a Canadian resident, you must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Box 14 - Employment income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

      Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

    • Non-resident corporation or partnership

      If the payment is made to a non-resident corporation or partnership, you must report the following amounts on a T4A-NR slip:

      • Box 18 - Gross income

      Learn how to report the payment: T4A-NR slip - Payments to non-residents for services provided in Canada
