Starting and Stopping CPP contributions, including information on Form CPT30

This new content has been developed for clarity, completeness, and plain language. No changes were made to the CRA's treatment of CPP withholding.

You may have to start or stop deducting CPP contributions in the year for one of your employees, including when they provide you with a completed Form CPT30, Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election.

You can find information (including examples) on how to calculate both CPP and CPP2 contributions and how to apply proration at Calculate CPP contributions deductions and Calculate second additional CPP contributions (CPP2) deductions.

Situation: Your employee turns 18 in the year

If your employee turns 18 in the year, you must start deducting CPP contributions from the first pay dated in the month after your employee turns 18.

When prorating, use the number of months up to after the month the employee turns 18.

Situation: Your employee turns 70 in the year

If your employee turns 70 in the year, you must continue to deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month when your employee turns 70.

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month the employee turns 70.

Situation: Your employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP

An employee who is considered to be disabled under the CPP does not have to contribute to the CPP. If your employee is considered to be disabled, you must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee becomes or is considered to be disabled according to the letter that Service Canada sent to your employee.

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month your employee was considered to be disabled.

If your employee is no longer considered disabled under the CPP, you must start deducting CPP contributions on the first pay dated in the month after your employee is no longer considered disabled.

When prorating, use the number of months after the month the employee ceased to be disabled.

Situation: Your employee gives you a completed Form CPT30

By filling out Form CPT30, Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election, and giving it to their employer, an employee can either stop or restart their CPP contributions. The employee is responsible for sending an original completed copy of Form CPT30 to the CRA. An employer will generally only receive a copy. If an employee agrees to have an employer send the original to the CRA, the CRA will accept it.

Where a CPT30 election will not apply

The election to stop contributing to the CPP does not affect:

  • the salary or wages of an employee with a province of employment of Quebec
  • an employee who is considered to be disabled under the CPP or QPP
  • the salary and wages of a person who has reached 70 years of age

Do not deduct CPP contributions from the salary and wages you pay these employees.

Stopping CPP contributions

Before you can stop deducting CPP contributions from your employee’s pensionable earnings, you must make sure the employee is eligible to make the election to stop contributing.

An employee is eligible to file an election to stop contributing to the CPP if they meet all of the following conditions:

  • The employee is employed and is receiving pensionable earnings
  • The employee is at least 65 years of age but under 70
  • the employee has not already completed Form CPT30, Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election to revoke an election during the current calendar year
  • The employee does not have a valid CPT30 election currently in effect to stop contributing to the CPP

You should ask your employee to provide all of the following:

  • proof they are receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension (for example, a copy of the award letter)
  • proof of age (your employee must be at least 65 years of age but under 70)
  • a copy of a signed and completed Form CPT30

If your employee meets these conditions, they can make the election to stop contributing to the CPP by giving you a copy of Form CPT30 with parts A, B and C completed. Your employee has not elected if they have not given the completed and signed Form CPT30 to their employer. You have to continue to deduct CPP contributions from your employee’s pensionable earnings until they provide you with a completed Form CPT30 or a copy of a completed form that the employee filed with a previous employer.

The CPT30 election takes effect on the first day of the month after the date the employee gives you a completed and signed Form CPT30. This date should be the first day of the month after the month the employee entered in Part C. You must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month the employee gives you the completed CPT30.

When you prorate, use the number of months up to and including the month before the election becomes effective.

What you should do with a Form CPT30 when the date on it is in the past

Whether you should accept a CPT30 election form that is dated in the past will depend on the circumstances. Review the following scenarios for guidance on when to accept a CPT30 dated in the past:

Example 1

On June 27, 2026, your employee gave you (employer A) a copy of a CPT30 that was signed and dated March 10, 2026.

If your employee says the form is a copy of the election they filed with Employer B in March, but forgot to give you a copy, you should accept the form. Since your employee filed their election with Employer B, you and any other current and future employers should be given a copy of that form. You should also accept the form if your employee had only started working for you in June.

If your employee says they are filing Form CPT30 for the first time (or the first time since revoking a prior election), you should ask your employee to change the date on the election form to the current date (that is, June 27, 2026) before you accept it and they send the original to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). This will ensure you and the CRA are using the same month.

Example 2

On March 28, 2026, your employee turn 65 and decides they want to stop contributing to the CPP. Your employee completes Form CPT30, signs it, and sends a copy that same day to the payroll department through interoffice mail. It arrives in the payroll department on April 5.

As indicated on Form CPT30, your employee should give a copy of the CPT30 to you in the same month that they signs it. An unforeseeable delay that prevents your employee from giving you the election form in the same month should not prevent you from accepting the form, as long as the delay is reasonable. In this situation, you should accept the employee’s election form.

What to do if you receive a post-dated Form CPT30

Generally, you should not accept a post-dated Form CPT30 from an employee. However, there may be times when you need the completed form ahead of time to stop deducting CPP from the employee’s pensionable earnings by the effective date, for example, to meet payroll cut-off dates or system input deadlines. When you receive a post-dated form beyond this, you should return the form to the employee and ask the employee to re-submit it closer to the effective date.

Example

Your employee wants to stop paying CPP contributions in September 2026. On July 25, 2026, they completed a Form CPT30 and gave a copy to you. In Part C of the form your employee post-dated it to August 22, 2026. Even though you received the form in July, you can accept it and then stop deducting CPP from Bernard’s pensionable earnings in the first pay period in September.

Restarting CPP contributions

An employee can choose to restart contributing to CPP if they meet all of the following conditions:

  • The employee filed a Form CPT30 with an employer in a prior year
  • The employee is receiving, or will receive pensionable employment earnings that require CPP contributions

An employee cannot elect to start contributing again to the CPP in the same calendar year in which they made an election to stop contributing to the CPP.

If your employee meets these conditions, your employee can revoke their election by giving you a copy of Form CPT30 with parts A, B, and D completed. The revocation is effective the first day of the month following when you receive the completed form. You will restart deducting CPP contributions from the first pay dated in the month after the employee gives you the form.

When you prorate, use the number of months that includes the months that includes the month the revocation becomes effective.
