Determine if the benefit is considered a shareholder benefit

To determine if the benefit is a shareholder benefit, all of the facts relevant to the situation must be considered to determine if the benefit was received from the corporation in the shareholder’s capacity as a shareholder or as an employee.

This determination must be done based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific case. Each benefit you provide to your shareholder must be reviewed separately of any other benefit that they received. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

Q1. What is the type of benefit?

Types of shareholder benefits

The following are types of shareholder benefits:

  • Personal use of company property: Personal use of company assets like cars, homes, or boats is a taxable benefit.
  • Loans to shareholders: Low or no-interest loans to shareholders can result in taxable benefits.
  • Forgiven or low-interest loans: Loans that are forgiven or not required to be repaid are shareholder benefits.
  • Dividends in lieu of salary: Dividends paid instead of salary to avoid taxes can be a taxable shareholder benefit.
  • Benefits to family members: If benefits are given to family members of shareholders, they are also considered taxable.
  • Personal expenses paid by the company: If the company pays personal expenses on behalf of shareholders, it is considered a benefit.
  • Below-market rent: Rent charged below fair market value is considered a shareholder benefit.
  • Use of corporate tangible property - Example: Real estate, car, horse, parking space
  • Other type of shareholder benefit

Q3. Did the corporation give the benefit to pay off a debt that the corporation owed to the shareholder?

  • Yes - Example: Shareholder provides the business $10,000 for the business to pay their workers and the business paid the shareholder a $1,000 bonus each year in repayment of that debt
  • No - Example: Amount of the debt was not adjusted in the corporate books or shareholder loan account at the time the benefit was conferred

Q3a. Was the value of the benefit given to the taxpayer greater than the amount the corporation owed to the shareholder?

  • Yes - Example: Business owed the shareholder $30,000.00 but paid the shareholder $50,000.00
  • No - Example: Business gave the shareholder a vehicle worth $22,500.00

Q4. Did any customers or clients of the corporation receive the same benefit?

  • Yes - Example: Shareholder received a voucher for a free movie and certain clients of the company also received the same vouchers
  • No - Example: Shareholder received a payment for a loan held with the corporation

Q5. Did the shareholder own 50% or more of the corporation’s outstanding shares?

  • Yes - Example: Shareholder was the sole owner of the corporation and owned 100% of the shares in the corporation
  • No - Example: Shareholder only owned 49% of the shares in the corporation

Q6. Was the benefit given pursuant to a formal, written agreement with the corporation?

  • Yes - Example: Corporation paid rent to the shareholder for the use of the shareholder’s premises in accordance with a rental agreement entered into by both the corporation and the shareholder
  • No

Q7. Was the benefit given to entertain clients or potential clients?

  • Yes -Example: Corporation paid for the shareholder’s ticket to a basketball game for the shareholder to go with clients of the business
  • No

Q8. Was the benefit solely used to maintain or obtain a client, contract, or asset in furtherance of the business?

  • Yes - Example: Corporation paid for the shareholder’s travel expenses for the taxpayer to secure a lucrative contract for the corporation
  • No - Example: Corporation purchased an asset from the shareholder for more than fair market value and did not have a legitimate business objective in paying more than fair market value

Q9. Was the benefit given to reimburse the cost of professional fees incurred by the shareholder?

  • Yes - Example: Shareholder incurred lawyer fees to prevent the corporation from temporarily halting operations and the corporation reimbursed the shareholder for such fees
  • No

Conferral of the benefit

Q10. Was the benefit related to the performance of the shareholder’s duties at the corporation as a worker, director or officer?

  • Yes - Example: Benefit was given to the shareholder to perform their duties in this role
  • No - Example: Shareholder had no role at the corporation other than as a shareholder

Q11. Was the benefit given to the shareholder pursuant to a worker agreement with the corporation?

  • Yes - Example: Benefit was given to the shareholder through their role as an independent contractor at the corporation
  • No - Example: Shareholder worked as an independent contractor at the corporation but their agreement with the corporation did not mention the benefit at issue

  • Yes - Example: At least one worker, officer or director received the same benefit as the shareholder in their capacity as worker, officer or director
  • No - Example: Benefit was only given to the shareholder

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support the benefit was received from the corporation in the shareholder’s capacity as a shareholder and other facts indicate an employee.

To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the relationship of the parties, comparing the intentions of the parties to the facts of the relationship. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you review each question and response, you should be able to decide what weight to assign each indicator based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, that the worker is self-employed.
