Payments from a wage-loss replacement plan for short-term disability (reduced EI premium rate)

Content to be covered: Wage-loss replacement plans, How to reduce the EI premium rate if you provide your employees with a short-term disability plan

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may provide a wage loss replacement plan (WLRP) for short-term disability to your employees. Depending on your situation, you can apply for the EI premium reduction rate through Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC).

Learn more on how and when to apply for a premium reduction through ESDC, including what you have to send with your application:ESDC | EI Premium Reduction Program: For employers

What is a WLRP

Generally, a WLRP is an arrangement between an employer and employees, or an employer and a group or association of employees, where the employees receive benefits on a periodic basis for loss of employment income due to sickness, maternity or accident.

A WLRP may provide short-term disability (STD), long-term disability (LTD) or weekly indemnity (WI) benefits. The benefits may be paid by the employer, or by an insurance company, trustee, board of trustees or other independent organization.

A WLRP must meet all of the following conditions:

  • It is a group plan, in that it covers more than one employee
  • The purpose of the plan is to indemnify employees against a loss of employment income as a result of sickness, accident or maternity
  • Benefits are paid on a periodic basis, not as a lump-sum
  • It follows insurance principles that funds are accumulated, normally in the hands of a trustee or in a trust account, and are calculated to be sufficient to meet anticipated claims

Learn more: Wage loss replacement plans

If the plan is not a group plan (that is it is for a single employee), or if the plan is funded entirely by employee contributions (an employee-pay-all plan), it is a WLRP.

    Opening a separate payroll account

    Once ESDC approves your request for a reduced EI employer premium rate, your existing payroll deduction account will be set at your new reduced EI premium rate.

    You will have to send a request to the CRA to open a separate payroll account  under your existing business number (BN) to make separate remittances at the regular EI premium rate for employees not covered by the wage-loss replacement plan.

    Learn more: Open or manage a payroll account

    Reporting on T4 slips

    You have to report income paid to your employees on separate T4 returns for each payroll account under your BN. For example:

    • RP0001, EI premium reduction rate - For employees covered under an approved plan
    • RP0002, Standard rate of 1.4 times the employee's premiums - For employees who are not covered by the plan

    Where an employee was transferred between both accounts in the same calendar year, you also have to file a separate T4 slip for each account.

    Learn more: How to file

    Situation: You pay an amount to your employee for 5/12 of any savings in the year in which you received the EI premium reduction

    If you pay an amount to your employee for 5/12 of any savings in the year in which you received the EI premium reduction (or within the first 4 months of the following year), the benefit is not taxable if it is considered non-cash or near-cash (exempt from taxation 6(1)(a)).

    For example, the benefit is provided indirectly through increased employer contributions to an employee's health and welfare trust, group sickness or accident insurance plan, private health services plan, or in any other manner.

    Taxable situation

    If you pay an amount to your employee in cash, such as a cash allowance or a cash rebate, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: You pay for a top-up disability payment (as per subsection 6(17))

    Determine if the top-up disability payment you, as an employer, make is to the insurer or directly to the employee.

    Situation: You paid directly the insurer

    Non-taxable situation

    If you make a top-up disability payment, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Insurer is making periodic payments under a disability plan to your employee that became insolvent
    • You make payments to the obligated insurer, either the insolvent insurer or another insurer that has assumed the obligations of the insolvent insurer
    • The obligated insurer continues periodic payments, in whole or in part, to your employee under the policy

    These are payments you makee direclty to the insurer and not to your employee. In this situation, this is not considered to be a benefit received or enjoyed by your employee.

    Taxable situation

    If the payments you make does not meet all of the above conditions, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: You paid directly your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you make a top-up disability payment, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Disability insurance plan was funded only by your employee contributions
    • Insurer is making periodic payments under a disability plan to your employee that became insolvent
    • You make payments directly to you employee
    • The payments replace, in whole or in part, the periodic payments that would have been made under the applicable insurance policy, if not for the insurer's insolvency
    • Under the arrangement, your employee reimburses you if they subsequently receive an amount from an insurer in respect of those same periodic payments

    Generally, these payments are considered an amount payable to your employee based on the relevant disability insurance plan and it is funded only by your employee contributions. In this situation, this is not considered to be a benefit received or enjoyed by your employee.

    Taxable situation

    If the payments you make does not meet all of the above conditions, the benefit is taxable.

  3. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    Depending on the worker’s employment status, you must withhold the following deductions relating to the commission payments:

    • Employee

      • Income tax
      • CPP contributions
      • EI premiums

      If you make commission payments to your employee, you have to report the payments on a T4 slip. Continue to Step 3 - How to calculate deductions.

    • Self-employed worker

      • Income tax (do not withhold)
      • CPP contributions (do not withhold)
      • EI premiums (do not withhold)

      A self-employed worker is responsible for remitting income tax and CPP contributions in the same way as other self-employed individuals.

      If you make commission payments to a self-employed worker, you have to report the payments on a T4A slip. Continue to Step 4 - Report the payment on a slip.

    • Non-resident self-employed worker

      • Income tax at the rate of 15%
      • CPP contributions (do not withhold)
      • EI premiums (do not withhold)

      If you make commission payments to a non-resident self-employed worker, you have to report the payments on a T4A-NR slip. Continue to Step 4 - Report the payment on a slip.

  4. How to calculate EI premiums

    Once approved by ESDC:

    • You will have a reduced EI premium rate that is less than the standard 1.4 times the employee premium (for example, 1.24 times)
    • Your reduced rate will only apply to employees who are covered by the approved plan, including employees serving an eligibility period under the plan of 3 months or less

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  5. Report the payment on a slip

    You must report payments on a T4 or T4A slip depending on the worker’s employment status:

    • Employee - T4 slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Box 14 - Employment income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 42 - Employment commissions

      Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

    • Self-employed worker - T4A slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4A slip:

      • Box 020 - Self-employed commissions

      Learn more: T4A slip – Information for payers

    • Non-resident self-employed worker - T4A-NR slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4A-NR slip:

      • Box 18 - Gross amount

      Learn more: T4A-NR slip - Payments to non-residents for services provided in Canada
