Lump-sum payments, including qualifying retroactive lump-sum payments

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

If you make lump-sum payments to a person, you have to report the payments and deductions on a T4A slip. Depending on the type of lump-sum payments or the situation, you may need to fill out a T4 or NR4 slip instead.

  • Steps

Steps

  1. Determine if the payment is considered a lump-sum payment

    If you make one of the following payments, it is considered a lump-sum payment:

    • Retiring allowances (severance pay)
    • Death benefits
    • Payments from a registered retirement savings plan (RRSP)
    • Payments from a registered pension plan (RPP)
    • Payments from a Deferred Profit Sharing Plan (DPSP)
    • Payments to the original annuitant under a registered retirement income fund (RRIF)
    • Payments from an individual pension plan (IPP)
    • Payments from an amended plan
    • Proceeds from the surrender, cancellation, or redemption of an income-averaging annuity contract (IAAC)

    • If the payment is not one of the above (for example, bonuses and wages in lieu of termination notice), it is not considered a lump-sum payment for income tax purposes. Do not continue to next step.

      Refer to:

      • Bonuses, retroactive pay increases or irregular amounts
      • Payments of wages in lieu of termination notice

    • If the payment is one of the above, it is considered a lump-sum payment.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the lump-sum payment you make.

  2. Determine if any exceptions apply to the lump-sum payment you make

    You may have different withholding or reporting requirements if you make lump-sum payments in one of the following situations:

    • Payments of retiring allowances
    • Payments related to the death of your employee
    • Payments to First Nations workers

    • If the payment is made in one of the above situations, do not continue to next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links.

    • If the payment is not made in one of the above situations, continue to one of the following depending if the lump-sum payment is for:

      • Retroactive lump-sum total payment: Step 3 - Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLSP).
      • Other types of lump-sum payments: Step 6 - Withhold deductions from the lump-sum payment.

  3. Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLSP)

    If you make a lump-sum payment, it is a QRLSP if all of the following conditions apply:

    • Payment is a qualifying amount
      What are qualifying amounts

      Qualifying amounts

      A qualifying amount is the principal portion of a payment or benefit that is eligible for the special tax calculation:

      Eligible source of income are:

      • Income from an office or employment received under one of the following:
        • An order or judgment from a court or other competent tribunal
        • An arbitration award
        • A lawsuit settlement agreement (including damages for loss of office or employment)
      • Benefits from unemployment insurance or employment insurance
      • Benefits from a superannuation or pension plan , including from a foreign pension plan and Old Age Security (other than non-periodic benefits such as lump-sum withdrawals)
      • Spousal, common-law partner, or taxable child support payments
      • Benefits from a wage-loss replacement plan
      • Canadian Forces members' and veterans' income replacement benefits

      Amounts not qualifying

      An amount received under any part of a retroactive lump-sum payment that is eligible for any of the following deductions, is not a qualifying amount:

      • Legal expenses
      • Salary reimbursements
      • Reimbursement of top-up disability payments
      • Repayment of pension or benefits
      • Deduction of social assistance payments, workers' compensation benefits

      An amount under normal collective bargaining, such as negotiated back pay, is not a qualifying amount.

      A different tax treatment may apply if the employee is deceased.

    • Payment has been made after 1994
    • Payment relates to one or more prior eligible tax years
      What is an eligible tax year

      An eligible tax year is a previous tax year that ended after 1977, during which the recipient:

      • Was a resident of Canada
      • Did not become a bankrupt during the calendar year
    • The sum of the payments relating to the preceding years is at least $3,000, not including interest (if a person receives more than one type of QRLSP in a year, the $3,000 threshold applies to the total amount of QRLSP, not to each individually)

    • If the payment does not meet all of the above conditions, it is not a QRLSP. It is a lump-sum payment.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if you have to withhold source deductions from the lump-sum payment.

    • If the payment meets all of the above conditions, it is a QRLSP.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198 if the payment is a QRLSP.

  4. Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198 if the lump-sum payment is a QRLSP

    If the payment is a QRLSP (step 3), the recipient can request a special tax calculation be applied to their income tax and benefit return.

    What is the special tax calculation

    Generally, a lump-sum payment is included in income in the year the recipient receives it. This may result in a greater tax liability than if the payment had been received in the year or years to which it related.

    A special tax calculation is available to recipients who receive a QRLSP that relate to any year after 1977. The calculation applies if the total of all principal amounts that relate to prior years (after 1977) from all QRLSPs is $3,000 or more.

    You (as the payer) must provide to the recipient one of the following to help the CRA decide if the special tax calculation is beneficial to them:

    • A completed Form T1198, Statement of Qualifying Retroactive Lump-Sum Payment

    • A letter with the following information:

      • The year in which the lump-sum payment was made to the recipient
      • A complete description of the lump-sum payment and the circumstances that required it to be paid
      • The total amount of the lump-sum payment, including a breakdown between the principal and the interest element, if any, of the payment
      • The principal amount of the lump-sum payment that relates to the current year and each of the preceding years covered by the payment

    It is the recipient's responsibility to send Form T1198 or the letter from the payer to their tax centre and request the special tax calculation be applied to their personal income tax and benefit return.

  5. Determine which deductions you need withhold

    You must withhold the following deductions relating to the lump-sum payments depending on the recipient’s situation:

    1. Withhold income tax deductions

      If one of the following situations apply, do not withhold income tax from the lump-sum payment :

      • The recipient's total earnings received or receivable during the calendar year, including the lump-sum payment, are less than the claim amount on their Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return
      • The payment is one of the following:
        • A lump-sum payment (such as a refund of premiums) paid to a deceased annuitant's spouse or common law partner
        • The minimum amount paid out of a RRIF to the original annuitant
      • A direct transfer of a lump-sum payment from an RPP or DPSP to any of the following plans:
        • RRIF
        • RRSP
        • RPP
        • Pooled retirement pension plan (PRPP)
        • Specified pension plan (SPP)
        • DPSP - Only if the transfer is from another DPS

      • If one of the above special situation apply, you do not need to withhold income tax deductions on the lump-sum payment.

        Do not continue to next step because you do not need to withhold CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax.

      • If the lump-sum payment is not in one of the above special situation, you must withhold income tax deductions on the lump-sum payment is not a QRLSP.

        Continue to: Step 5b - Withhold CPP contributions and EI premiums.

    2. Withhold CPP contributions and EI premiums

      You must withhold CPP contributions and EI premiums if the lump-sum payment is considered employment income:

      • CPP - Withhold, calculate using the bonus or irregular payments method
      • EI - Withhold, calculate using the any payments (manual calculation) method
      • Income tax - Withhold, calculate using the bonus or irregular payments method

  6. Report the lump-sum payment on a slip

    You need to report the lump-sum payment depending on the type of income you paid:

    • T4 slip - Payments that are employment income

      You must report lump-sum payments that are considered employment income on a T4 slip:

      • Box 14 - Employment income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings

      Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

    • Self-employed worker - T4A slip

      You must report lump-sum payments that are not considered employment income on a T4A slip depending on the type of lump-sum payment:

      • Pension or superannuation
        • Box 016 - Pension or superannuation
        Lump-sum payments
        • Box 018 – Lump-sum payments
        • Code 102 – Lump-sum payments – non-resident services transferred under paragraph 60(j)
        • Code 108 – Lump-sum payments from a registered pension plan (RPP) that you cannot transfer
        • Code 110 – Lump-sum payments accrued to December 31, 1971
        • Code 158 – Lump-sum payments that you cannot transfer that are not reported elsewhere
        • Code 180 – Lump-sum payments from a deferred profit-sharing plan (DPSP) that you cannot transfer
        • Code 190 – Lump-sum payments from an unregistered plan

      Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted tax from the payment.

      Learn more: T4A slip - Information for payers
