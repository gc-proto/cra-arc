Employee who is registered, or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act

Content to be covered: Indian employees

For purposes of the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act, the term Indian is used because it has a legal meaning in the Indian Act. Section 87 of the Indian Act exempts from taxation the personal property of an Indian situated on a reserve. The section 87 exemption:

  • only applies to an Indian as defined by the Indian Act
  • does not apply to all Indigenous Peoples in Canada such as the Inuit or Métis.

Indians who want to determine if their income is taxable should fill out Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian's Employment Income.

You must report all income and deductions on a T4 or T4A slip for status Indian employees regardless of whether their income is taxable or exempt.

Terms used on this page
Employer is resident on a reserve

"Employer is resident on a reserve" means that the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located.

The central management and control of an organization is usually considered to be exercised by the group that performs the function of a board of directors of the organization. However, the real management and control of an organization may be exercised by some other person or group. Generally, management and control is exercised at the principal place of business but it is recognized that this function may be legitimately exercised in a place other than the principal administrative office of the organization. It is a question of fact where the central management and control is exercised.

Indian
"Indian" means an Indian as defined for the purposes of the Indian Act.
Indian lives on the reserve
"Indian lives on the reserve" means the Indian lives on the reserve in a domestic establishment that is his or her principal place of residence and that is the centre of his or her daily routine.
On a reserve
"On a reserve" means on a reserve as defined for purposes of the Indian Act, including any settlements deemed to be reserves for purposes of the Indian Settlements Remission Order, and any other areas given similar treatment under federal legislation (for example, Category I-A lands under the Cree-Naskapi" (of Quèbec) Act).
Social, cultural, educational or economic development
"Social, cultural, educational or economic development" includes the provision of social services such as health care or counselling.

Steps

  1. Determine the status of the employee

    An Indian is defined in the Indian Act as an individual who is listed or is entitled to be listed in the Indian Register. The Indian Register is the official record identifying all Indians who are registered in Canada. Indigenous Services Canada is responsible for the Indian Register and is the only one authorized to issue a "certificate of Indian status" to an individual.

    Status situations

    Situation: Your employee is registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act

    Depending on your situation, the employment income of status Indians may be exempt from tax. It is the income that is exempt from tax and not the person.

    The rules for CPP and EI are different. EI premiums are always required. Income that is exempt from income tax is not pensionable employment although you or the employee may elect into the CPP.

    To determine if the income is exempt from tax in this situation, continue to: Step 2: Determine the residency of the employee or employer.

    Situation: Your employee is a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act

    The employment income is taxable if your employee is a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act.

    Even if your employee has Indian status, you need to deduct CPP, EI and income tax in this situation, same as your regular employees.

    Learn more

    Residents of British Columbia, Newfoundland and Labrador, the Northwest Territories and Yukon are asked to indicate on their tax return whether they:

    • live on Indigenous government lands
    • are citizens or members of these Indigenous governments

    This information is used to ensure that the correct amount of revenue is directed to the appropriate Indigenous government.

    Learn more: Determine whether you live on Indigenous government lands

    Situation: Your employee is not registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act

    The employment income is taxable if your employee is not registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act. The section 87 exemption only applies to an Indian as defined by the Indian Act.

    You need to deduct CPP, EI and income tax for this situation, same as your regular employees.

  2. Determine the residency of the employee or employer

    Residency situations

    Situation: Your employee lives on a reserve and you are a resident on a reserve

    Exempt income situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, the individual's income qualifies for the exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act of the Indian Act if all of the following apply:

    • employee is registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act
    • employee is not a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act
    • employee lives on a reserve (this means it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine)
    • employer is a resident on a reserve (this means the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located)

    You do not deduct CPP, EI and income tax in this situation. This includes employment-related income such as unemployment benefits, retiring allowances, and other pensions.

    Learn more on how to report amounts on a T4 slip when you pay tax-exempt or partly tax-exempt employment income to your employee: How to report income paid for Indian employees - T4 slip - Information for employers

    Examples of where exempt and not exempt

    Examples where exempt

    Mrs. H works for a logging company that is resident on a reserve. Mrs. H lives on a reserve, but performs the duties at lumber camps located off the reserve. The employment income is exempt from income tax because the place where the employee lives and the residence of their employer are factors connecting the income to a reserve.

    Mr. I is a construction worker employed by a construction company that is resident on a reserve, to work on building sites that are not located on a reserve. Mr. I lives on a reserve, except for short periods each year when he lives near the construction sites. When away from the reserve, he retains his residence on the reserve where his family lives. Mr. I is exempt from income tax on his employment income because the place where he lives and the residence of his employer are factors connecting his employment income to a reserve.

    Examples where not exempt

    Ms. J lives on a reserve and works as an accountant at the head office of a bank located off a reserve in a nearby city. The bank maintains a branch on Ms. J's reserve and arranges for her to be paid at that branch. Ms. J's employment income is taxable because the only substantive factor linking her employment income to a reserve is her residence which, without other substantial connecting factors, does not result in exemption. Payment on the reserve is viewed as a connecting factor of relatively little weight.

    Mr. K is a construction worker employed by a corporation situated on a reserve. The corporation is an employment agency that makes employees available to work for clients that are not situated on a reserve, on building sites not located on a reserve. Mr. K lives on a reserve. The employment agency had an office on the reserve, but carried out very few of its business activities on the reserve. The guidelines would not apply in this case, and one must therefore do an analysis of the connecting factors. Mr. K's employment income is taxable because the residence of the employer in this case has little weight as a connecting factor. The only other substantive factor connecting Mr. K's employment income to a reserve is his residence, which is not sufficient to bring the employment income within the exemption.

    Ms. B lives on a reserve and works as director general on that reserve for their corporation (of which they are a shareholder). Their administrative offices are located on that reserve but whose work is carried on mostly off a reserve. Ms. B receives a salary from their corporation. This salary is exempt. Ms. B pays themselves a bonus on top of their salary which bonus is based on the company's performance. The connecting factor of the bonus is not sufficient to bring that income within the exemption.

    Where an employee is a shareholder who controls the business (non-arm's length with the corporation), the source of the business income may be relevant in determining whether the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act applies to the income received in the shareholder's capacity as an employee. Where the source of the business income in this situation is off the reserve, the employment income (salary and bonuses) will not be considered to be connected to the reserve and will not be exempt from tax.

    Exempt income situation

    Under the CRA's temporary administrative policy for the period of Mar 15, 2020 to Dec 31, 2022, the individual's income qualifies for the exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act if all of the following apply:

    • employee is registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act

    • if the employer normally resides on a reserve but the COVID-19 restrictions resulted in the central management and control over the employer organization moving to a location off a reserve
    • if employee normally lives on a reserve but is restricted from returning to their home on a reserve due to COVID-19 (this means it was their principal place of residence and was the centre of their daily routine)

    You do not deduct CPP, EI and income tax in this situation. This includes employment-related income such as unemployment benefits, retiring allowances, and other pensions.

    Situation: Other situations where your employee is required to perform duties on a reserve

    The rules for CPP and EI are different. EI premiums are always required. Income that is exempt from income tax is not pensionable employment although you or the employee may elect into the CPP.

    • Whether an actual "duty of employment" was performed (the employee was required to perform the particular task and was compensated by the employer for performing that particular task)

      Example where exempt or not exempt

      Example where exempt

      Ms. L lives on a reserve and works as a policewoman for an off-reserve employer. She performs more than one-half of her duties on a reserve, and she has an additional connecting factor in that she lives on the reserve. Therefore, she is exempt on the whole of the employment income.

      Example where not exempt

      Ms. M lives off a reserve in a town where she works in a restaurant owned by a corporation resident on a reserve. Once a week, she drives to the reserve to pick up the pay cheques for the restaurant staff. The whole of her employment income is taxable because the only factor connecting the income to a reserve is the residence of the employer and, without other connecting factors, this is not sufficient for the exemption to apply.

      Situation: Your employee performs 50% or less of their employment duties on a reserve

      Partly-exempt income

      Under the CRA's administrative policy, if the employee performs 50% or less of their employment duties on a reserve, the exemption may be prorated and those duties are not merely incidental to duties they performed off a reserve. The exemption will apply only to the portion of the income related to the duties the employee performed on the reserve if all of the following apply:

      • employee is registered as a status Indian under the Indian Act
      • employee is not a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act
      • the employer or the employee is in one of the following situations:
        • employee lives on a reserve (this means it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine)
        • employer is a resident on a reserve (this means the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located)

      Example where exempt or not exempt
      Examples where exempt

      Mr. N works for the education department of a tribal council at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Mr. N lives off a reserve and the tribal council is resident on one of the reserves. Mr. N is exempt from income tax on his employment income because the duties he performs for the tribal council are connected to the reserves served by the council, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Ms. O is a librarian working for a technical training institute operated by five Indian bands for Indians who live on a reserve. The administrative office of the institute is on a reserve. Ms. O lives off a reserve and works at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Ms. O is exempt from income tax on her employment income because the duties she performs for the Indian organization that employs her are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Mr. P works for an Indian organization providing child and family related services to members of a large number of bands with reserves scattered over a large area within a province. Some of these services are provided in the provincial capital, where Mr. P works, and the organization's administrative office is at an off-reserve location central to the bands served. However, the organization's directors, consisting of the band chiefs, meet at each reserve in rotation. Mr. P is exempt from income tax on his employment income because the duties he performs for the Indian organization that employs him are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Ms. Q works for a company that is resident on a reserve and owned by a tribal council. The company exists to provide adequate low-cost housing on reserves and the company's property is for the most part on reserves. Ms. Q performs her duties at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Ms. Q is exempt from income tax on her employment income because the duties she performs for the Indian organization that employs her are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Mr. R works for an Indian organization that manages funds provided by the government to assist in planning for future education needs on reserves. His employer is resident on a reserve, but he works at a location off a reserve. Mr. R is exempt from income tax on his employment income because the duties he performs for the Indian organization that employs him are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Examples where not exempt

      Mr. S works for a commercial building supplies company that is owned by a tribal council and is resident on a reserve. He performs his duties off a reserve and lives off a reserve. Mr. S is taxable on his employment income because, although there is one factor, the residence of the employer, connecting the income to a reserve, this factor by itself is not sufficient to confer the exemption when the employer and the employee are active in the commercial mainstream of society.

      Ms. T works for an Indian organization dedicated to organizing social programs for off-reserve Indians. The organization is located off a reserve. Ms. T is taxable on her employment income because there are no factors connecting that income to a location on a reserve.

  4. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    Exempt income situation

    • Salary, wages and taxable benefits in cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • CPP, only if the employer filed a CPT124 election
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

    • Non-cash and near-cash taxable benefits: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP, only if the employer filed a CPT124 election
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed a CPT124 election - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

    If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, an Indian can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

    Partly-exempt income

    • Salary, wages and taxable benefits in cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP, on the portion of the taxable income or all income if employer filed a CPT124 election
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

    • Non-cash and near-cash taxable benefits: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP, on the portion of the taxable income or all income if employer filed a CPT124 election
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    CPP is only withheld on the portion of the income that is taxable. Where the income is not taxable, CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed a CPT124 election - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

    If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, an Indian can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.
