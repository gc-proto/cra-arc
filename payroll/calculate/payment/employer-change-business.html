Employer restructuring, succession of employers and amalgamations

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You (as the employer) may have changes to the structure of your business, either an employer restructuring, succession of employers or an amalgamation. Depending on the nature of the change, your withholding, remitting and reporting obligations may be different.

Determine your withholding and reporting obligations based on the following changes to your business structure:

  • Employer restructuring or succession of employers
  • Amalgamation
Employer restructuring or succession of employers

Steps

  1. Confirm the nature of the change to your business is a restructuring or succession of employer

    An employer restructuring or succession of employer occurs where an employer, with the agreement of the former employer or by operation of law, immediately succeeds another employer as a result of any of the following:

    • Formation of a corporation
    • Dissolution of a corporation
    • Acquisition of all or part of a business
    What if you are not sure whether a restructuring or a succession of employers applies to you

    If you are not sure whether the conditions for an employer restructuring or succession of employers have been met, you can ask the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) for a ruling.

    Learn more: How to get a CPP/EI ruling

    Learn more: Employer restructuring/Succession of employers - CPP/EI explained

  2. Determine how to calculate the CPP contributions and EI premiums deductions

    If all of the conditions are met for change to be considered an employer restructuring or a succession of employers, the successor employer has the option to consider the employment of the employee to be continuous for the purpose of CPP and EI deductions.

    Continuous employment

    If you choose to consider the employment of the employees to be continuous, the successor employer continues to deduct CPP and EI as a continuation of the previous employer’s deductions, taking into account what had been deducted by the previous employer when determining the annual maximum for each.

    If any employee reached the CPP and EI maximums with the previous employer, the successor employer is not required to make any further deductions.

    Not continuous employment

    If you do not choose to consider the employment of the employees to be continuous, the successor employer has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if the employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

    Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. As an employer, you are not entitled to a refund for the employer's share of the contributions.

    • If all of the conditions are met and the successor employer chooses to consider deductions made by the previous employer, the CPP and EI deductions made by the previous employer are treated as having been made by the successor employer.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Calculate the CPP and EI withholdings when the succession of employer applies.

    • If all of the conditions are not met or the successor employer chooses not to consider the previous employer’s deductions, the employer restructuring has no impact on CPP and EI deductions.

      The employer must withhold CPP contributions and EI premiums until their employees reaches the maximum without taking into account deductions made by previous employer or any another employer.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Report on a T4 slip .

  3. Determine how to calculate the income tax deductions

    Whether or not there was an employer restructuring, succession of employers or a restructuring, the previous employer, the successor employer, the predecessor corporation and the successor corporation must deduct income tax from the remuneration or other income they have paid to their employees regardless of whether the employees have other employers in the year. The successor employer may need to ask employees for a TD1 form if they do not have the records of the previous employer. If the employee has already claimed personal tax credit amounts in the year on Form TD1 with another employer, they cannot claim them again when starting employment with their employer.

  4. Calculate the CPP and EI withholdings when the succession of employer applies

    If the successor employer chooses to consider the employment of the employee to be continuous, the CPP and EI deductions made by the previous employer are treated as having been made by the successor employer when determining the annual maximum for each deduction. The successor employer may need to ask employees for a TD1 form if they do not have the records of the previous employer.

    The successor employer continues to deduct CPP and EI as a continuation of the previous employer’s contributions. If any employee had reached the CPP and EI maximums with the previous employer, the successor employer is not required to make any further deductions.

    What if the previous employer withheld deductions but did not remit

    The trustee (or previous employer) may be legally required to hold amounts in trust and remit. If a trustee continues to operate the employer’s business, they must get a new BN and continue to deduct and remit.

    Learn more: How to calculate

  5. Remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

    The previous and successor employers must remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions for the actual CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax amounts they deducted.

    The previous employer must report a final remittance within 7 calendar days of the end of the restructuring or succession of employers.

    Learn more on which method to use to remit based on the situation: When to remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

  6. Report on a T4 slip

    The previous and successor employers must report on the T4 slips the actual CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax amounts they deducted.

    • Previous employer

      The previous employer is required to file T4 slips up to the day before the change

    • Successor employer

      The successor employer is required to file T4 slips from the date of the change to year-end

      The successor employer will only enter the insurable income using box 24 and pensionable income using box 26 remaining up to the annual maximum to make sure the calculations of the CPP and EI deducted match the pensionable and insurable income amounts.

      If the employee has reached their CPP and EI maximums with the previous employer, the successor employer must enter $0 in boxes 16, 16A, 17, 17A, 18, 24, and 26.

    Example

    A succession of employers occurs between employer A and employer B in the 2026 calendar year. Their employee, whose province of employment was Ontario, earned $50,000 with the previous employer and $40,000 with the successor employer. The employer received a CPP/EI Rulings decision confirming that a valid succession of employers had occurred.

Amalgamation

Steps

  1. Confirm the nature of the change to your business is an amalgamation

    An amalgamation occurs when two or more corporations, known as predecessor corporations, combine their businesses to form a new successor corporation.

    • Predecessor corporation is a business that existed independently prior to the date of amalgamation.
    • Successor corporation is a business that is formed as a result of an amalgamation.
      • The successor corporation is considered a continuation of all the predecessor corporations.
      • The successor corporation acquires all of the assets and liabilities of the predecessor corporations.
    What if you are not sure whether an amalgamation applies to you

    If you are not sure whether the conditions for an amalgamation have been met, you can ask the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) for a ruling.

    Learn more: How to get a CPP/EI ruling

    Learn more: Employer restructuring/Succession of employers - CPP/EI explained

  2. Determine how to calculate the CPP contributions and EI premiums deductions

    When an amalgamation occurs, the new corporation is not considered a new employer for CPP or EI purposes.

    The successor corporation continues to deduct CPP and EI as a continuation of the predecessor corporation’s deductions in the normal manner, taking into account what has already been deducted by the predecessor corporation when determining the annual maximum for each.

    Reduced EI premium rate

    If the predecessor corporation provided a wage-loss replacement plan (WLRP) for short-term disability to their employees, the successor corporation must re-apply for the reduced EI premium rate with Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC).

    ESDC will inform them if it is approved and confirm the reduced EI premium rate to use. If the rate is different than the predecessor's EI premium rate, it is effective from the date of amalgamation.

    Until ESDC completes their review, the reduced EI premium rate RP account will maintain its existing remittance rates.

    Learn more on how and when to apply for a EI premium reduction through ESDC, including what you have to send with your application: ESDC | EI Premium Reduction Program: For employers

  3. Determine how to calculate the income tax deductions

    Whether or not there was an employer restructuring, succession of employers or a restructuring, the previous employer, the successor employer, the predecessor corporation and the successor corporation must deduct income tax from the remuneration or other income they have paid to their employees regardless of whether the employees have other employers in the year. The successor employer may need to ask employees for a TD1 form if they do not have the records of the previous employer. If the employee has already claimed personal tax credit amounts in the year on Form TD1 with another employer, they cannot claim them again when starting employment with their employer.

  4. Remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

    When an amalgamation occurs, the successor employer:

    • Can keep the business number (BN) of one of the corporations (unless one of the corporation is a non-resident).


      When to remit

      Continue to remit normally under the payroll account of the successor BN based on the remitter type.

    • Can apply for a new BN.

      A copy of the new constituting act must be attached with the application to make sure that the funds from the account to be closed can be transferred to the new corporation's account.

      When to remit

      Must report a final remittance within 7 calendar days of the amalgamation.

    Learn more on which method to use to remit based on the situation: When to remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

  5. Report on a T4 slip
