Employees working outside Canada

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

Before you start

  1. Confirm your (employer) residency status

    You (as the employer) may be uncertain of your residency status.

    The residency status is determined on a case-by-case basis and an employer's whole situation. The conditions that determine residency depend on the type of legal entity.

    Corporations

    If your business is a corporation, whether it is a resident or non-resident is based on common-law principles.

    Learn more: Residency of a corporation

    Partnerships

    If your business is a partnership:

    • If all partners are Canadian residents, it is considered a Canadian partnership
    • If the partnership has at least one non-resident partner, it is considered a non-resident partnership

    Sole proprietorship

    If your business is a sole proprietorship, you must confirm the residency status of the owner of the business. Continue to: Confirm the residency status of your employee.

  2. Confirm the worker status: Employee or is self-employed

    If you or an individual working for you is not sure of the worker's employment status (employee or self-employed), either one of you can request a CPP/EI ruling to determine the status and whether the employment or the earnings are pensionable, insurable or both.

    Learn more:

    • How to get a CPP/EI ruling
    • CPP/EI Explained
  3. Confirm the residency status of your employee

    The employee may be uncertain of their residency status.

    The residency status is determined on a case-by-case basis and an employee's whole situation. If your employee wants the CRA's opinion on their residency status, they can fill out one of the following:

    • Form NR74, Determination of Residency Status (Entering Canada)
    • Form NR73, Determination of Residency Status (Leaving Canada)

    Learn more: Individuals – Leaving or entering Canada and non-residents

Situations

Determine your withholding and reporting obligations based on the following situation:

  • Canadian resident employee working outside Canada for a Canadian resident employer
  • Canadian resident employee working outside Canada for a non-resident employer
  • Non-resident employee working outside Canada for a Canadian resident employer
Canadian resident employee - Canadian resident employer

You, as a Canadian resident employer, may have Canadian resident employees who are working outside Canada.

  If you have employees whose province of employment is ordinarily Quebec, refer to: Employee Who Reports for Work Only at One of Your Establishments Located Outside Canada or Who Is Paid From One of Your Establishments Located Outside Canada | Revenu Québec

Steps

  1. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    1. If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following applies:

      • Your employee usually reports for work at one of your establishments in Canada
      • Your employee's salary is paid at or from one of your establishment in Canada

      • If one of the above conditions apply, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If the above conditions do not apply, international social security agreements may require the employment to be pensionable.

        Continue to: Step 1b - Determine if your employee is covered by the CPP under an international social security agreement.

    2. Determine if your employee is covered by the CPP under an international social security agreement

      If the employment does not meet one of the conditions in step 1a, international social security agreements may require the employment to be pensionable.

      Canada has international social security agreements with more than 50 countries that offer pension programs that are comparable to the CPP.

      How to confirm if the country has an agreement with Canada

      If the country is on the list of countries that have an agreement with Canada (except Australia and New Zealand), this means that the agreement allows for employment in that country to be pensionable. The list provides the names of the countries that have agreements with Canada, their effective dates, the related CPT form numbers to apply for a certificate of coverage, and the maximum periods of initial detachment.

      Certificate of coverage

      If the country has an agreement with Canada, fill out the CPT form for that country to apply for a certificate of coverage for your employee and send it to the CPP/EI Rulings Division. Once approved, the certificate of coverage will make sure your employee contributes only to the CPP and be exempt from contributing to the pension plan of the other country.

      You must keep the certificate of coverage to confirm the employment is pensionable in Canada and is not subject to social insurance contributions in the other country.

      Learn more: How to apply for a certificate of coverage

      • If the country has an agreement with Canada and a certificate of coverage was issued by the CRA, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If the country does not have an agreement with Canada or you do not have a certificate of coverage issued by the CRA, the employment may still be pensionable.

        Continue to: Step 1c - Determine if your employee’s employment is pensionable if no social security agreement exists between the country in which they are working and Canada.

    3. Determine if your employee’s employment is pensionable if no social security agreement exists between the country in which they are working and Canada

      If the country does not have an agreement with Canada or you do not have a certificate of coverage issued by the CRA, the employment is pensionable if all of the following applies:

      • The employment outside Canada would be pensionable if it were in Canada
      • Your employee meets one of the following conditions:
        • Your employee performs services under an international development assistance program (subject to certain conditions)
        • The individual is the spouse or common-law partner of your employee in steps 1a or 1b (subject to some conditions)
        • Your employee of Her Majesty in right of Canada who is locally engaged (subject to certain conditions)

      • If one of the above conditions is met, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If the above conditions are not met, you can apply for CPP coverage for your employee if all conditions are met.

        Continue to: Step 1d - Determine if you can apply for CPP coverage.

    4. Determine if you can apply for CPP coverage

      If the conditions in steps 1a, 1b and 1c are not met for the employment is to be pensionable, you have the option to extend CPP coverage for your employee if all of the following conditions apply:

      • You (as the employer) are operating in Canada and the employment in question would be pensionable if it were in Canada
      • Your employee was resident and present in Canada at the time they were hired and you (the employer) have undertaken to pay both the employee’s and the employer’s contributions for all of your employees that have the same employment in that country
      • The country does not have an agreement with Canada where your employee is working

      • If all of the above conditions are met, you can extend CPP coverage to your employee working outside Canada by filing out Form CPT8, Application and Undertaking to Cover Employment Outside Canada under the Canada Pension Plan.

        Once the CRA approved your application, you must deduct CPP contributions.

      • If you do not choose to cover CPP for your employee working outside Canada, do not deduct CPP contributions.

        Your employee can elect to pay CPP on their earnings outside Canada if all conditions are met by filling out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      5. Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

  2. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must deduct EI premiums if all of the following apply:

    • You, as the employer, reside in Canada or have a place of business in Canada
    • Your employee usually resides in Canada
    • The employment would be insurable employment if it were in Canada
    Special situation: Employment on a ship outside or partly outside Canada

    Even if the above conditions are met for the employment to be insurable, the employment outside Canada is not insurable when all of the following conditions apply:

    • The ship regularly travels between ports outside Canada
    • The ship has been chartered (leased) to someone who lives outside Canada

    • If all of the above conditions are not met or if the above special situation applies, do not deduct EI premiums.

    • If all of the above conditions are met, you must deduct EI premiums.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you generally have to deduct income tax.

    Employee wants to reduce income tax deductions

    Your employee may be entitled to a foreign tax credit in Canada for income taxes paid in the foreign country by requesting a letter of authority to reduce their deductions of Canadian income tax.

    Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source

    Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  4. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 10 - Province of employment using abbreviations US or ZZ in a country other than Canada
    • Box 29 - Employment code 16 if your employee is considered detached
    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

Canadian resident employee - Non-resident employer

You, as a non-resident employer, may have Canadian resident employees who are working outside Canada.

Steps

  1. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, do not deduct CPP contributions.

    Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

  2. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must deduct EI premiums if all of the following apply:

    • You, as the non-resident employer, have a place of business in Canada
    • Your employee usually resides in Canada
    • The employment would be insurable employment if it were in Canada

    • If all of the above conditions are not met or if the above special situation applies, do not deduct EI premiums.

    • If all of the above conditions are met, you must deduct EI premiums.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must deduct income tax.

    Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  4. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you outside Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 10 - Province of employment using abbreviations US or ZZ in a country other than Canada
    • Box 29 - Employment code 16 if your employee is considered detached
    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

Non-resident employee - Canadian resident employer

You, as a Canadian resident employer, may have non-resident employees who are working outside Canada.

Steps

  1. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have non-resident employees who are working outside Canada, you must withhold the following deductions:

    • CPP contributions - Do not withhold
    • EI premiums - Do not withhold
    • Income tax - Do not withhold. You should contact the tax authority for the country in which the work is performed regarding your tax obligations in that country.

  2. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and your have non-resident employees who are working outside Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 10 - Province of employment using abbreviations US or ZZ in a country other than Canada
    • Box 29 - Employment code 16 if your employee is considered detached
    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers
