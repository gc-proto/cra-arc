What if you do not know if a worker is an employee or is self-employed

If you or an individual working for you is not sure of the worker's employment status (employee or self-employed), either one of you can request a CPP/EI ruling to determine the status and whether the employment or the earnings are pensionable, insurable or both.

Non-resident employer - Non-resident employee working in Canada

You, as a non-resident employer, may have employees working in Canada. Generally, you must report the payment as employment income on your employee’s T4 slip. Depending on the worker’s employment status, you may need to fill out a T4A, T4A-NR or NR4 slip instead.

Steps

  1. Determine if you have an establishment in Canada for income tax, CPP and EI purposes

    Where the income is from employment, you need to determine if you have an establishment in Canada for income tax, CPP and EI purposes.

    • If you have an establishment in Canada, do not continue to the next step.

      Refer to: ???

    • If you do not have an establishment in Canada, continue to: Step 2 - Determine the residency status of your employee.

  2. Determine the residency status of the employee

    Where the income is from employment, you need to consider the employee's residency status.

    Ordinarily resident for CPP/EI purposes

    If the employee is considered a factual resident of Canada, they will generally be considered for CPP/EI purposes to be ordinarily resident in Canada.

    Confirm if your employee is a factual resident

    You may need to ask your employee to confirm if they are considered a factual resident.

    Learn more: Factual residents – Temporarily outside of Canada

    • If your employee is a ordinarily resident of Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax.

      You also have to report on a T4 slip if they are your employees or a T4A slip if the worker is self-employed.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Report the payment on a slip.

    • If your employee is not ordinarily a resident of Canada or is a deemed resident or sojourner, you need to determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax.

      You must report the employment income on a NR4 slip if they are your employee or on a T4A-NR slip if they are self-employed.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the payment made to your employees.

  3. Determine if any exceptions apply to the payment made to your employees

    Depending on the situation, your withholding and reporting obligations may be different. For example:

    • Your non-resident employee is attending conferences in Canada
    • Your employee works at your foreign embassy in Canada

    • If the payment is made in one of the above situations, do not continue to next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links.

    • If the payment is not made in one of the above situations, continue to: Step 4 - Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions.

  4. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    1. If you are a non-resident employer and if you have employees working in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following applies:

      • If one of the above conditions apply, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

        Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If the above conditions do not apply, international social security agreements may require the employment to be pensionable.

        Continue to: Step 4b - Determine if Canada has an international social security agreement .

    2. Determine if Canada has an international social security agreement

      Canada has international social security agreements with more than 50 countries that offer pension programs that are comparable to the CPP.

      Confirm if the country has an agreement with Canada

      If the country is on the list of countries that have an agreement with Canada (except Australia and New Zealand), this means that the agreement allows for employment in that country to be pensionable. The list provides the names of the countries that Canada has agreements with, their effective dates, the related CPT form numbers to apply for a certificate of coverage, and the maximum periods of initial detachment.

      Certificate of coverage

      If the country has an agreement with Canada, fill out the CPT form for that country to apply for a certificate of coverage for your employee and send it to the CPP/EI Rulings Division. Once approved, the certificate of coverage will make sure your employee contributes only to the CPP and be exempt from contributing to the pension plan of the other country.

      You must keep the certificate of coverage to confirm the employment is pensionable in Canada and is not subject to social insurance contributions in the other country.

      Learn more: How to apply for a certificate of coverage

      • If the country is on the list of countries that have an agreement with Canada (except Australia and New Zealand), you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

        Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If Canada does not have an international social security agreement with the other country, continue to: Step 4c - Determine if you or your employee can apply for CPP coverage.

    3. Determine if you or your employee can apply for CPP coverage

      • If all of the conditions to be pensionable are met and you have elected to extend CPP coverage to your employee, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

      • If the conditions to be pensionable are not met or you have not elected to extend CPP coverage to your employee, do not deduct CPP contributions.

  5. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you have employees working in Canada, you must deduct EI premiums if all of the following apply:

    • If all of the conditions to be insurable are met, you must deduct EI premiums.

      Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

    • If the conditions to be insurable are not met, do not deduct EI premiums.

  6. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    1. If you are a non-resident employer and you have employees working in Canada, you must deduct income tax if your employee is a Canadian resident and is paid from your place of business in Canada.

      • If all of the above conditions apply, you must deduct income tax.

        Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

        Continue to: Step 7 - Report the payment on a slip.

      • If the above conditions do not apply, you may not have to deduct income tax on the employment income paid to a non-resident employee.

        Continue to: Step 6b - Determine if your country and your employee's country a tax treaty with Canada.

    2. Determine if your country and your employee's country a tax treaty with Canada

      Canada has tax treaties with more than 90 countries. These treaties help prevent double taxation and determine how employment income, business profits, pensions, and other types of income are taxed when someone has ties to both Canada and another country.

      Confirm if the country has a tax treaty with Canada

      If the country is on the list of countries that have a tax treaty in force with Canada, this means that the treaty allows for employment in that country to be taxable. The list provides the names of the countries that Canada has treaties with, the dates it was signed, and more.

      • If Canada does not have a tax treaty in force with Canada, you must deduct income tax.

        Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

        Continue to: Step 7 - Report the payment on a slip.

      • If the country is on the list of countries that have a tax treaty in force with Canada, continue to: Step 6c - Determine if you have a valid non-resident employer certification.

    3. Determine if you have a valid non-resident employer certification

      The non-resident employer certification is a voluntary program that allows eligible non-resident employers to be exempt from withholding Canadian income tax on salary paid to non-resident employees if all conditions are met. This reduces administrative burden for your businesses with cross-border employees.

      You must be certified by the CRA before the employment period begins.

      Conditions to qualify

      • Qualifying non-resident employer
        • You are resident in a country which Canada has a tax treaty
        • You do not have a permanent establishment in Canada during the period your employee works in Canada
        • You are certified by the CRA before the employment period begins
      • Qualifying non-resident employee
        • Your employee is resident in a country which Canada has a tax treaty in force
        • Your employee must:
          • Be present in Canada for less than 45 days in the calendar year that includes the time of payment
          • Your employee must be present in Canada for less than 90 days in a 12-month period that includes the time of the payment
        • Your employee must be exempt from Canadian tax under the tax treaty

      How to apply

      Learn more: Where to send international waiver and non-resident employer certification applications

      • If you are a qualifying non-resident employer, you do not withhold income tax on the employment income paid to a qualifying non-resident employees.

        A non-resident employer certification is valid for up to 2 calendar years.

        Continue to: Step 7 - Report the payment on a slip.

      • If you do not have a valid non-resident employer certification from the CRA or the employee is no longer a qualifying non-resident employee, continue to: Step 6c - Determine if your employee is exempt from Canadian tax under a tax treaty.

    4. Determine if you can apply for a R102 waiver certification

      If after reasonable enquiry, employers find that their employee no longer meets the definition of a qualifying non-resident employee, they should immediately notify the CRA by sending a letter.

      Conditions to qualify

      • Qualifying non-resident employer
        • You are resident in a country which Canada has a tax treaty in force
        • You do not have a permanent establishment in Canada during the period your employee works in Canada
        • You are certified by the CRA before the employment period begins
      • Qualifying non-resident employee
        • Your employee is resident in a country which Canada has a tax treaty in force
        • Your employee must be present in Canada for less than 183 days in a 12-month period that includes the time of employment
        • Your employee must be exempt from Canadian tax under the tax treaty

      The letter should fully disclose the employee's circumstances, including why the employee no longer qualifies.

      If the employee is still treaty-exempt, the employer should send the CRA a completed Form RC102 Regulation 102 Waiver Application.

      Learn more: Tax treaties

      Learn more: Where to send international waiver and non-resident employer certification applications

      • If all of the conditions to be pensionable are met and you have elected to extend CPP coverage to your employee, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

      • If the conditions to be pensionable are not met or you have not elected to extend CPP coverage to your employee, do not deduct CPP contributions.

  7. Report the payment on a slip

    A qualifying non-resident employer is required to file a T4 slip if they either:

    • Made tax exempt payments to a qualifying non-resident employee of more than $10,000 in the calendar year
    • Deducted CPP or EI on behalf of a qualifying non-resident employee

    You must report payments on a slip depending on the worker’s employment status:

    • Canadian employee - T4 slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

      • Box 14 - Employment income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings

      Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

    • Canadian self-employed worker - T4A slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4A slip:

      • Code 028 - Other income

      A slip must always be issued if any tax is withheld. The CRA administrative policy does not apply where tax is withheld, even if the total of all payments is under $500.

      Learn more: T4A slip – Information for payers

    • Non-resident self-employed worker - T4A-NR slip

      You must report the following amounts on a T4A-NR slip:

      • Box 18 - Gross amount

      Learn more: T4A-NR slip - Payments to non-residents for services provided in Canada

    • Non-resident employee - NR4 slip

      • Annuity payments made to a non-resident.

        • Box 16 - Gross income

      Learn more: NR4 slip
