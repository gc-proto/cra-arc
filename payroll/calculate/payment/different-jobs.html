Employees with more than one employer

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may have employees who have another employer at the same time as they work for you or had another employer earlier in the year. In general, this does not change your withholding or reporting obligations.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Confirm if your business was part of an employer restructuring, succession of employers or an amalgamation

    • If you (as the employer) have changes to the structure of your business, either an employer restructuring, succession of employers or an amalgamation, do not continue to the next step.

      Refer to: Employer restructuring, succession of employers and amalgamations

    • If you do not have changes to your business, continue to: Determine how to calculate the CPP contributions and EI premiums deductions.

  2. Determine how to calculate the CPP contributions and EI premiums deductions

    You must deduct CPP contributions and EI premiums without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if the employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

    The annual maximum amount apply to each job your employee holds with different employers (different business numbers).

    If your employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if your employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

    Example

    Example - CPP

    Your employee notifies you that they have reached the maximum CPP contribution for the year. They tell you that they had $1,700 in CPP contributions deducted by a previous employer in the year and that when combined with the CPP you have deducted from their pay, they have reached the maximum.

    Do not stop deducting CPP contributions from their pay. You are required to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pay until they reach the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you. Do not factor in CPP contributions made through other employers.

    Example - EI

    Your employee notifies you that they have reached the maximum EI premiums for the year. They tell you that they had $900 in EI premiums deducted by a previous employer in the year and that when combined with the EI you have deducted from their pay, they have reached the maximum.

    Do not stop deducting EI premiums from their pay. You are required to deduct EI premiums from your employee's pay until they reach the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you. Do not factor in EI premiums made through other employers.

  3. Determine how to calculate the income tax deductions

    The current and the other employers must deduct income tax from the remuneration or other income they have paid to their employees regardless of whether the employees have other employers in the year.

    If the employee has already claimed personal tax credit amounts in the year on Form TD1 with another employer, they cannot claim them again when starting employment with their employer.

  4. Remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

    The current and the other employers must remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions for the actual CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax amounts they deducted.

    Learn more: Remit (pay) payroll deductions and contributions

  5. Report on a slip

    The current and the other employers must report on the T4 slips the actual income paid and the actual CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax amounts they deducted.

    Learn more: Fill out the slips and summaries
