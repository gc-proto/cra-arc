Employees working inside Canada - Non-resident employer or non-resident employee

You may be looking for:

  • Tax information for individuals working at foreign embassies in Canada
  • Payments to agriculture and horticulture workers, including foreign workers employed in Canada under the Seasonal Agricultural Workers Program

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

Determine your withholding and reporting obligations based on the following situation:

  • Canadian resident employee working inside Canada for a non-resident employer
  • Non-resident employee working inside Canada for a Canadian resident employer
  • Non-resident employee working inside Canada for a non-resident employer
Canadian resident employee - Non-resident employer

You, as a non-resident employer, may have Canadian resident employees who are working in Canada.

Steps

  1. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    1. If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions if you have an establishment in Canada.

      For CPP purposes, an establishment in Canada means any of the following:

      • Any office
      • Bar
      • Club
      • College
      • Factory
      • Farm
      • Hotel
      • Mine
      • Motel
      • Oil or gas well
      • Pier
      • School
      • Residence
      • Restaurant
      • Tavern
      • Timber land
      • Warehouse
      • Wharf
      • Workshop
      • Any other place or premises in Canada that is owned, leased, or licensed by the employer and where the employer or its employees work, report for work, or where the employer pays its employees

      • If you have an establishment in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions.

        Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If you do not have an establishment in Canada, you can apply for CPP coverage for your employee.

        Continue to: Step 1b - Determine if you can apply for CPP coverage.

    2. Determine if you can apply for CPP coverage

      If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you in Canada, you have the option to extend CPP coverage for your employee by filing out Form CPT13, Application for an Employer Resident Outside Canada to Cover Employment in Canada Under the Canada Pension Plan.

      • Once the CRA has approved your application, you must deduct CPP contributions.

      • If you do not choose to cover CPP for your employee working in Canada, do not deduct CPP contributions.

        Your employee can elect to pay CPP on their earnings in Canada if all conditions are met by filling out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

    Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

  2. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct EI premiums.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct income tax.

    Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  4. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have Canadian resident employees working for you in Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

Non-resident employee - Canadian resident employer

You, as a Canadian resident employer, may have non-resident employees who are working in Canada.

Steps

  1. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions unless Canada has a signed international social security agreement with your employee’s home country.

    Canada has international social security agreements with more than 50 countries that offer pension programs that are comparable to the CPP.

    How to confirm if the country has an agreement with Canada

    If the country is on the list of countries that have an agreement with Canada (except Australia and New Zealand), this means that the agreement allows for employment in Canada to only be covered by that country’s pension plan. The list provides the names of the countries that have agreements with Canada, their effective dates and the maximum periods of initial detachment.

    • Do not deduct CPP contributions if both of the following apply:
      • The country has an agreement with Canada
      • Your employee provides you with proof of coverage from that country’s social security program
    • You must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following apply:
      • The country does not have an agreement with Canada
      • The country has an agreement with Canada, but your employee does not provide you with proof of coverage from that country’s social security program

    Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

  2. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct EI premiums unless the unemployment insurance laws of the home country require premiums to be paid for that employment.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct income tax, unless your employee provided you with a regulation 102 waiver.

    Learn more about how your employee can obtain a Regulation 102 waiver: Treaty based waivers involving Regulation 102 withholding

    • If your employee provided you with a regulation 102 waiver, do not deduct income tax.

    • If your employee did not provide you with a regulation 102 waiver, you must deduct income tax.

      Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  4. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a Canadian resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

Non-resident employee - Non-resident employer

You, as a non-resident employer, may have non-resident employees who are working in Canada.

Steps

  1. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    1. If you are a non-resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, do not deduct CPP contributions if you do not have an establishment in Canada.

      • If you do not have an establishment in Canada, do not deduct CPP contributions.

        Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums.

      • If you have an establishment in Canada, you may have to deduct CPP contributions if Canada has a signed international social security agreement with your employee’s home country.

        Continue to: Step 1b - Determine if your employee’s home country has an international social security agreement with Canada.

    2. Determine if your employee’s home country has an international social security agreement with Canada

      If you have an establishment in Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions unless Canada has a signed international social security agreement with your employee’s home country.

      • Do not deduct CPP contributions if both of the following apply:
        • The country has an agreement with Canada
        • Your employee provides you with proof of coverage from that country’s social security program
      • You must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following apply:
        • The country does not have an agreement with Canada
        • The country has an agreement with Canada, but your employee does not provide you with proof of coverage from that country’s social security program

    Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

  2. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct EI premiums unless the unemployment insurance laws of the home country require premiums to be paid for that employment.

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct income tax unless one of the following situations apply:

    • Situation 1: Your employee provided you with a Regulation 102 waiver

      If you are a non-resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must deduct income tax, unless your employee provided you with a regulation 102 waiver.

      • If your employee provided you with a regulation 102 waiver, do not deduct income tax.

      • If your employee did not provide you with a regulation 102 waiver, you must deduct income tax unless situations 2 or 3 apply.

    • Situation 2: You are a qualifying non-resident employer and your employee is a qualifying non-resident employee

      If you are a qualifying non-resident employer, you do not have to deduct income tax for employment income you pay during the period you are certified if your employee meets all of the following conditions to be considered a qualifying non-resident employee:

      • Your employee is resident in a country that Canada has a tax treaty with at the time of the payment
      • Your employee must:
        • Work in Canada for less than 45 days in the calendar year that includes the time of payment
        • Be present in Canada for less than 90 days in any 12-month period that includes the time of the payment
      • Your employee must be exempt from Canadian because of a tax treaty

      Learn more about how and where to apply: Non-resident employer certification

      • If all of the above conditions are met, do not deduct income tax.

        A non-resident employer certification is valid for up to 2 calendar years.

      • If all of the above conditions are not met, you must deduct income tax unless situations 1 or 3 apply.

    • Situation 3: You sent your non-resident employee to attend a conference in Canada

      Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you are a non-resident employer and you sent non-resident employees to attend a conference (a formal meeting attended by a minimum of 30 participants for professional purposes) in Canada, you do not have to deduct income tax if all of the following conditions apply:

      • You, as the employer, will not otherwise be carrying on business in Canada

      • Your employee will only be present in Canada to participate at the conference and will spend 10 days or less in Canada, including days spent travelling

      • Your employee is a resident of a country that Canada has a tax treaty with

      • Your employee is not providing any services for which their employer is receiving a fee from another person

      • Your employee is not:

        • Soliciting business on behalf of you as their employer

        • Providing services to a parent, subsidiary, or partnership related to you as their employer

        • Marketing your (their employer’s) services or products

        • Meeting with clients regarding your (their employer’s) business

      If the CRA considers the situation abusive, the CRA administrative policy does not apply. For example, when an employee participates in numerous conferences in Canada during the year, the policy does not apply.

      • If your employee meets all of the above conditions under the CRA administrative policy, do not deduct income tax.

      • If your employee does not meet all of the above conditions under the CRA administrative policy, you must deduct income tax unless situations 1 or 2 apply.

    Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  4. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    If you are a non-resident employer and you have non-resident employees working for you in Canada, you must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits
    Situation: You are a qualifying non-resident employer

    A qualifying non-resident employer is required to file a T4 slip if they:

    • Made payments to an employee other than a qualifying non-resident employee
    • Deducted income tax, CPP contributions or EI premiums from the payments
    • Made payments to a qualifying non-resident employee and after review had reason to believe that the employee's total taxable income earned in Canada (including any tax-exempt amounts under a tax treaty) in the year were more than $10,000
    Situation: You sent your non-resident employee to attend a conference in Canada

    If you have sent your non-resident employee to attend a conference in Canada and they met all of the conditions in situation 3 of step 3, you do not have to report the employment income earned by the employee for the period spent at the conference on a T4 slip unless:

    • You deducted income tax
    • Your employee will be providing services in Canada in addition to attending the conference

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers
