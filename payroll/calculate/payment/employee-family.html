Employee who is hired as a family member or a related person

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may have employees who are family members or are considered related to you.

Depending on the facts of the working relationship, your employee’s employment may not be insurable and you may not have to deduct EI premiums. However, the fact that your employee is a family member does not automatically make your employee’s employment not insurable.

Steps

  1. Determine if your employee is considered related to you

    You (as the employer) may have employees who are considered related to you if one of the following situations apply:

    • Confirm if individuals are considered related to you

      Blood relationship

      The following individuals are related by blood:

      • A parent and a child (or another descendant, such as a grandchild or a great-grandchild)
      • A brother and a sister

      Blood relatives do not include more distant relatives such as aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews or cousins.

      Marriage

      Individuals are related if they are legally married to each other. They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their spouse.

      If the marriage is dissolved because of divorce or death of a spouse, the individual ceases to be connected by marriage.

      Common-law partnership

      Individuals are related if they are in a common-law partnership with each other. The CRA considers that 2 individuals are in a common-law partnership in one of the following situations:

      • They live together in a conjugal relationship and have done so for a continuous period of at least 12 months
      • They live together in a conjugal relationship and have a child together (this includes through adoption), in which case the 12-month period does not apply

      They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their common-law partner.

      If there is a breakdown in the relationship, they are considered to continue to live in a common-law partnership until they have lived separate and apart for 90 consecutive days.

      Adoption

      Individuals are related through adoption if one individual is the adopted child of the other.

      In addition to their parents, the child is also related to their siblings, grandparents and great-grandparents.

      Adoption includes legal adoption and de facto adoption.

      The following factors are considered when determining whether a de facto adoption exists:

      • Actual control and custody
      • An exercise of parental care and responsibility on a continuing basis
      • Dependency
      • Proximity to each other

    • Confirm if persons are considered related to a corporation

      A person (or related group of persons) and a corporation

    • Confirm if a person and a partnership are considered related

      A person and a partnership

      The following partners who control a partnership  are related if one of the following applies:

      • A partner controls a partnership

        Example
        Description

        Dylan, Leonard, Donovan and Sarah form a partnership. They are not related and each has an equal 25% share in the profits.

        Under the partnership agreement, Sarah manages the business and has full decision making authority. Since Sarah controls the partnership, she does not deal at arm's length with the partnership.

        Result

        For income tax purposes, Dylan, Leonard and Donovan deal at arm's length with the partnership based on control, though they may not deal at arm's length based on other factors.

      • A related group of partners controls a partnership

        Example
        Description

        Dylan, Leonard, Donovan and Sarah form a partnership. Leonard, Donovan and Sarah are siblings (related). Dylan is their cousin which they means they are not related. Each has an equal 25% share in the profits.

        Result

        Since they are a related group that controls the partnership, Leonard, Donovan and Sarah do not deal at arm's length with the partnership.

        For income tax purposes, Dylan deals at arm's length with the partnership based on control, though he may not deal at arm's length based on other factors.

    • If your employee is not considered related to you, do not continue to the next step.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees.

      Learn more: How to calculate

    • If your employee is considered related to you, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions.

  2. Determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions

    If you (as the employer) and your employee are related, you must deduct CPP contributions as you would for your other employees.

    Your obligation to deduct CPP contributions applies regardless if you are related.

    Learn more: Calculate CPP contributions deductions

  3. Determine if you need to deduct EI premiums

    If you (as the employer) and your employee are related, your employee’s employment may not be insurable since they are considered to be dealing at arm’s length .

    Your employee’s employment can be insurable if, after reviewing all the circumstances of the employment, it is reasonable to conclude that you would have negotiated a similar contract with a person with whom you deal at arm’s length.

    Factors to determine if you would have negotiated a similar contract

    All the facts about the working relationship must be reviewed to see if other employees would have been hired under a similar contract of employment.

    Generally, the CRA reviews a combination of the following factors to determine whether the parties are dealing with each other at arm’s length (not just a single factor):

    • Remuneration paid
    • Terms and conditions of employment, such as the hours of work
    • Duration of work performed
    • Nature and importance of the work performed by your employee

    Learn more: Not dealing at arm's length for purposes of the Employment Insurance Act (EIA)

    • If your employee’s employment is not insurable, do not deduct EI premiums.

    • If your employee’s employment is insurable, you must deduct EI premiums.

      Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

  4. Determine if you need to deduct income tax

    If you (as the employer) and your employee are related, you must deduct income tax as you would for your other employees.

    Your obligation to deduct income tax applies regardless if you are related.

    Learn more: Calculate income tax deductions

  5. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    You must report the following on a T4 slip:

    • Box 14 - Employment income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings (if the employment is insurable)
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • Any applicable codes for taxable benefits

    Learn more: T4 slip - Information for employers

What is considered a related group

  • Related group

    Means that each member in a group of persons is related to every other member of the group

  • Unrelated group

    Means a group of persons that is not a related group

Person or related group of person controls a partnership

For income tax purposes, partners are not normally related to a partnership and partnerships are not related to other persons.

Control determines if partners or partnerships deal at arm's length with each other or another person.

A partner can control a partnership if one of the following apply:

  • Ownership of a controlling interest, by having more than a 50% share in the profits of the partnership
  • A mandate vested in that partner by the other partners, by being granted decision making powers in the partnership agreement
