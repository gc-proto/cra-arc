Travel expenses

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide travel allowances or reimbursements to your employees, this include food, beverage, lodging, and transportation expenses, but not motor vehicle expenses.

Steps

  1. Determine if the traveller is considered in a special situation

    • Do not continue to next step if the traveller is in one of the following special situation:
      • Benefits provided to an employee in prescribed zones
      • Benefits provided to an employee who has an severe or prolonged impairment
    • If no special situation apply, continue to Step 2 - Determine regular place of employment

  2. Determine the regular place of employment

    Generally, you will have a written working arrangement with your employees on or before they start, this arrangement may include the following:

    • Address of the employer
    • Address of the expected place of work, including all the locations (if more than one)
    • Details of any work in a special work site, remote location or outside Canada for a long period (for example, more than 1 month)

    The location can become important during the employment. For example, when determining the regular place of employment.

  3. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, travel allowances or reimbursements you provide to your employees are taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations - You provide an allowance or you reimburse for travel expenses

    Situation: Your employee is working part-time

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your part-time employee for travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee incurs the expenses from travelling to and from the part-time job
    • You and your employee are at arm's length
    • Your employee:
      • is a teacher or professor who works part-time in a designated educational institution in Canada and provides service to the employer as a teacher or professor
      • has additional employment or carries on a business outside of their employment with you
    • The location of employment is 80 kilometres or more from your employee's home (or from the other employment or business)

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, continue to Situation: Your employee is not part of the above situation.

    Situation: Your employee is a salesperson, an agent selling property or negotiating contracts

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee who is a salesperson, an agent selling property or negotiating contracts for travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Expense related to the performance of duties of the office or employment
    • Travel allowance is reasonable, for meal expenses this is generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable

    Learn more: Determine if the real estate agent is an employee or self employed – CPP-EI explained

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the full amount is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is a member of the clergy

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee who is a member of the clergy for travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Expense related to the performance of duties of the office or employment
    • Travel allowance is reasonable, for meal expenses this is generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the full amount is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is a member of the Canadian Forces and it is for separation allowances under service regulations

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance or you reimburse your employee who is a member of the Canadian Forces for travel expenses and separation allowances under service regulations, the benefit is not taxable.

    Learn more from the Department of National Defence: Chapter 209 - Transportation and Travelling Expenses

    Situation: Your employee's main duty of employment is transporting goods

    To determine if the benefit is taxable and if any exemptions apply, see: Travel expenses - Provided to an employee who is transporting goods part of their employment duties.

    Situation: Your employee or their employee's spouse (or common-law partner) spends more than 50% of the time on business-related activities

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee or their employee's spouse (or their common-law partner) for business-related travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • You require their presence on the business trip to accomplish the business objectives of the trip
    • Employee or their employee's spouse (or common-law partner) spends more than 50% of the time on business-related activities
    • Travel allowance is reasonable, for meal expenses this is generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)

    The benefit is considered from the employee's travelling expenses necessary to accomplish the business objectives of the trip.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

      For example, the travel is taxable in one of the following situation:
    • Free holiday or vacation you pai to your employee, the employee's family, or both
    • You pay a third party for a trip or vacation for your employee or their family
    • Vacation property that you own and allow your employee to use for other than your business purpose
    • Business trip extended by your employee, only the extension of the trip is taxable

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is a host of an incentive award trip for at least 90% of the day

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • You awards employees or customers with an incentive award trip
    • The trip was arranged for your employees or your customers
    • Your employee acts as host for the incentive trip
    • Your employee works directly in business activities at least 90% of each day (a substantial part)

    Your employee's travelling expenses are considered necessary to accomplish the business objectives of the trip.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is working at a special work site

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a special work site and a remote work location. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    What is considered a special work site

    A special work site is an area where temporary duties are performed by an employee who keeps a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE) at another location as their principal place of residence. Because of the distance between the two areas, the employee is not expected to return daily from the work site to his or her principal place of residence. A SCDE is a dwelling-house, apartment, or other similar place of residence in which place a person as a general rule sleeps and eats. A room in a hotel, dormitory, boarding house, or bunkhouse is not ordinarily considered to be a SCDE.

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The free or subsidized transportation, or the allowance, was for transportation between the special work site and your employee's principal place of residence
    • Your employee's duties require them to be away from their principal place of residence or at a special work site for a period of more than 36 hours
    • You (or a third party) provided board and lodging, or a reasonable allowance for board and lodging, to your employee for that period
    • Form TD4 is completed

    You and your employee must fill out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site, to exclude board, lodging and transportation from your employee's income. You must keep a copy. The TD4 requires the exact location of the work site and the distance to your employee's principal place of residence.

    Taxable situation

    If the travel expense you provide to your employee is:

    • For both business and personal purposes, the personal portion is taxable
    • Does not meet all of the conditions above, it is taxable

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is working at a remote location

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a special work site and a remote work location. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Remote work location which is located 80 kilometres or more from the nearest established community with a population of at least 1,000 people and one the following apply:
      • Canada and the transportation or travel took place between the remote work location and any location in Canada
      • Another country and the transportation or travel took place between the remote work location and any location in the same country as the remote work location
      • Another country and the transportation or travel took place between the remote work location and any location in Canada
    • Your employee's duties require them to be away from their principal place of residence or to be at the remote work location for a period of more than 36 hours
    • You (or a third party) provided board and lodging, or a reasonable allowance for board and lodging, to your employee for that period

    Taxable situation

    If the travel expense you provide to your employee is:

    • For both business and personal purposes, the personal portion is taxable
    • Does not meet all of the conditions above, it is taxable

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    Situation: Your employee is not part of the above situation

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide a travel allowance (other than an allowance related to the use of a vehicle), the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

      • The travel is:
      • for business purposes outside the municipality and metropolitan area where your establishment is located and where your employee ordinarily works or reports
      • within a municipality or a metropolitan area in order to perform their duties in a more efficient way during a work shift
    • Travel allowance is reasonable, for meal expenses this is generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable
    • You are the primary beneficiary of your employee's travel
    • Travel allowance is not a form of additional remuneration and is not a form of tax avoidance

    To determine what is reasonable, you can use the guide for the public service employee travel allowances: Rates and allowances on the National Joint Council website.

    Examples
    Examples
    Examples Result

    Antoine installs and repairs power lines for a hydroelectric company. He works in an area of the municipality located over an hour from his office, where he generally has lunch. Instead of Antoine driving to the office during his normal meal break and returning to his workplace afterward, the employer pays him a reasonable meal allowance to allow him to eat at a restaurant closer to where he performs his duties. The employer is the primary beneficiary of this arrangement and the allowance would not be included in Antoine's salary.

    The benefit is not taxable.

    Jeannine works for DEF Corporation and must occasionally use her vehicle to travel into the municipality for supplies as part of her duties. This generally takes up the better part of her day. DEF gives Jeannine a daily allowance of $50 to cover vehicle expenses and all food and drink expenses that she may incur while performing her duties outside her normal workplace. Because this policy does not include a motor vehicle use allowance, we would consider unreasonable the $50 allowance that would cover her meals and her motor vehicle expenses.

    The allowance is a taxable benefit for Jeannine except if DEF Corporation can prove that the portion related to meals is reasonable under the circumstances.

    An employer gives its employees a travel allowance that covers food and drink when they have to go outside their normal workplace to perform duties. One of the employees regularly returns to the office before noon and stays there until having to go to an appointment that afternoon. It would be to the employee's advantage to return to the office to have his meal.

    The allowance is taxable because it takes the form of another type of compensation. However, if it happens that the employee is legitimately outside the office during his normal meal break, the allowance for those days may be excluded from his income.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the full amount is a taxable benefit.

      For example, the travel is taxable in one of the following situation:
    • Free holiday or vacation you pai to your employee, the employee's family, or both
    • You pay a third party for a trip or vacation for your employee or their family
    • Vacation property that you own and allow your employee to use for other than your business purpose
    • Business trip extended by your employee, only the extension of the trip is taxable

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

  4. Calculate the value of the benefit

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to:

    • Total value of the travel allowances or reimbursements that are taxable
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip
    • multiply by GST/HST calculation (if applicable)
    • equals Amount of GST/HST to remit
    Example 1 - Calculations

    An employee is sent to Toronto in Ontario for 9 days to meet with key partners. Presence is required on all days with free time to tour Toronto at own expense. The airfaire for the employee is $1,300. The hotel cost is $300 per night for 9 nights for a total of $2,700.

    • $4,000 is the total value of the travel allowances
    • minus $4,000 is the amount of the travel allowance that is not taxable
    • equals $0 because the benefit is not taxable

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 2 - Calculations

    Employee sent to Niagara Falls in Ontario for 9 days to meet with key partners. Presence is required on all days with free time to tour Toronto at own expense. The airfaire for the employee is $1,300. The hotel cost is $300 per night for 13 nights for a total of $3,900.

    • $5,200 is the total value of the travel allowances
    • minus $4,000 ($2,700 for 9 nights + $1,300 airfaire) is the amount of the travel allowance that is not taxable
    • equals $1,200 (4 nights) is the value of the taxable benefit
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $1,200 is the value of the benefit to be included on employee's T4 slip
    • multiply by 12/112 is the HST calculation in Ontario
    • equals $138.06 is the amount of HST to remit

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 3 - Calculations

    An employee is sent to Niagara Falls in Ontario for 9 days to meet with key partners. Presence is required on all days with free time to tour Toronto at own expense. Employee brings spouse and stays for an extra 4 nights. The airfaire for the employee is $1,300 and for the spouse is $1,300. The hotel cost is $300 per night for 9 nights for a total of $2,700.

    • $5,300 is the total value of the travel allowances
    • minus $4,000 ($2,700 for 9 nights + $1,300 airfaire for employee) is the amount of the travel allowance that is not taxable
    • equals $1,300 (airfaire for the spouse) is the value of the taxable benefit
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $1,300 is the value of the benefit to be included on the employee's T4 slip
    • multiply by 12/112 is the HST calculation in Ontario
    • equals $149.56 is the amount of HST to remit

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

  5. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

    If the benefit is taxable, you must withhold the following deductions. The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    • Cash: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

  6. Report the benefit on a slip

    If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following on the T4 slip.

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information

    • Cash: Option 2

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information
