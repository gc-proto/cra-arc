Automobile provided by the employer

You may be looking for:

  • Automobile or motor vehicle benefits – Allowances or reimbursements provided to an employee for the use of their own vehicle

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an automobile to your employee or a shareholder to use in performing their duties of office or employment. Generally, the personal use of the employer provided automobile is a taxable benefit.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder

Steps

  1. Determine if the motor vehicle is an automobile

    The Income Tax Act defines automobiles and motor vehicles differently for tax purposes.

    Answer a few questions to determine if the vehicle is an automobile or a motor vehicle.

    Launch vehicle validator

    Vehicle validator

    Automobile and motor vehicle definitions
    • If the vehicle is a motor vehicle, do not continue to next step. Refer to Motor vehicle provided by the employer.
    • If the vehicle is an automobile, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you made the automobile available to the employee.

  2. Determine if you made the automobile available to the employee

    • If you did not make the automobile available to your employee, this is not a taxable benefit. Do not continue to next step.
    • If you made the automobile available to your employee, continue to Step 3 - Determine if there was personal driving of the automobile.

  3. Determine if there was personal driving of the automobile

    • If there is no personal driving, the use of the employer-provided automobile is not a taxable benefit. Do not continue to next step.
    • If there is personal driving, the use of the employer-provided automobile may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 4 - Determine if special considerations apply to the benefit.

  4. Determine if special considerations apply to the automobile benefit

    • If the use of the employer-provided automobile is in one of the above special situation, do not continue to next step.
    • If the use of the employer-provided automobile is not in one of the above special situation, the use of the automobile may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 5 - Determine if the automobile benefit is taxable.

  5. Determine if the automobile benefit is taxable

    • Automobile standby charge benefit related to the availability and personal use
    • Automobile operating expense  benefit when you pay the operating costs of the automobile subject to a standby charge
    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to Step 4 - Review the required information (logbook) your employee must have kept.

  6. Review the required information (logbook) your employee must have kept


    Get the required records from your employee

    To meet condition 1 of 4 for the reduced standby charge, you must get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the vehicle benefit. Based on these records, you need to determine if you can use the reduced standby charge calculation.

    • If no record is kept, the employee does not benefit from the reduction to the standby charge. Continue to Step 10 - Calculate the standby charge benefit.

    • If the employee kept the required detailed records and you received a copy, the benefit meets the condition 1 of 4 for the reduced standby charge. Continue to Step 7 - Determine if the employee is required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment.

  7. Determine if the employee is required to use the automobile in connection with or in the course of an office or employment

    To meet condition 2 of 4 for the reduced standby charge, you need to determine if the employee was required to use the automobile in connection with or in the course of an office or employment.

    • If the employer did not require the employee to use the automobile as part of the duties of an office or employment, the employee does not benefit from the reduction to the standby charge. Continue to Step 10 - Calculate the standby charge benefit.

    • If the employer required the employee to use the automobile as part of the duties of an office or employment, the benefit meets condition 2 of 4 for the reduced standby charge. Continue to Step 8 - Calculate if the distance travelled is 50% or more (primarily) business driving.

  8. Calculate if the distance travelled is 50% or more (primarily) business purposes

  9. Determine if the number of kilometres of personal driving is within the limit for the reduced standby charge

    To meet condition 4 of 4 for the reduced standby charge, use the following to calculate the personal-use quotient. The number of kilometres travelled of personal driving must be less than 1,667 km per 30 day period or 20,004 km in the year.

    1. Determine the actual number of kilometres travelled for personal driving

      Based on the required information (logbook)  you received from your employee, determine the actual number of kilometres travelled for personal driving.

      If no record is kept, the employee does not benefit from the reduction to the standby charge.

    2. Calculate the limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use

      The number of kilometres travelled for personal purposes cannot exceed 1,667 kilometres per 30-day period or 20,004 kilometres in the year to meet one of the conditions for the reduced standby charge.

      Use the following to calculate the limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use:

      • Total number of days when the automobile was made available to the employee
      • divide by 30 days
      • equals Result (rounded to the nearest whole number)
      • multiply by 1,667 km
      • equals Limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use

      Judith can use an automobile that was made available by her employer during 150 days of the year. Judith was required to use the automobile in connection with her employment. She used the automobile for business purposes 80% of the time. In reviewing her travel logbook, you found that she drove 5,500 km for personal use (1,100 km per 30-day period) out of a total of 27,525 km.

      • divide by 30 days
      • equals 5 is the result

    3. Calculate the kilometre personal-use quotient for the reduced standby charge

      Use the following to calculate the kilometre personal use quotient:

      • Actual number of kilometres travelled for personal use (step 9a)
      • divide by Limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use (step 9b)
      • equals Personal-use quotient must be less than 1 for the reduced standby charge

      Judith can use an automobile that was made available by her employer during 150 days of the year. Judith was required to use the automobile in connection with her employment.       • divide by 8,335 is Judith's limit that applies to the number of kilometres travelled for personal use (step 9b)

    • If the personal-use quotient is more than 1, the employee will not benefit from the reduction to the standby charge. Continue to Step 10 - Calculate the standby charge benefit.

    • If the personal-use quotient is less than 1 (calculations from step 9a, 9b and 9C), the benefit meets the condition 4 of 4 for the reduced standby charge.

  10. Calculate the standby charge benefit

    Reminder: You can use Automobile Benefits Online Calculator that will calculate the benefit for you.

    If you make an automobile available  to your employee, you must calculate the value of the standby charge related to the personal use  and the availability of the automobile.

    How to calculate the value using estimates during the year

    During the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit using estimates. To obtain the amount you must add to the employee’s salary for a given pay period, you must divide the value of the benefit you have estimated by the number of pay periods in the year.

    At the end of the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit related to an automobile using the actual number of kilometres travelled during the year.

    Situations

    Situation: You own the automobile and you want to calculate using the simplified calculation

    You can use simplified calculation if all the following conditions are met:

    • You own the automobile provided to the employee
    • The employee uses the same automobile throughout the year
    • The employee's duties do not consist primarily in selling or leasing automobiles
    • The employee is not eligible for a reduction of the value of the standby charge
    What is included in the cost of the automobile

    The cost of the automobile includes all of the following:

    • Cost of the automobile when it was bought, including options and accessories, and the GST/HST and PST, but not including any reduction for a trade-in
    • Cost of additions, including the GST/HST and PST, made to the automobile after it was bought

    Where the automobile was purchased from a non-arm's length person, the cost is generally equal to the fair market value when it was bought, including options and GST/HST or PST.

    How to calculate the cost of the automobile when used part of an automobile fleet

    During the year, when your employee uses at least one automobile that is part of an automobile fleet that you operate, the guidelines below apply for calculating the cost of the automobile.

    • If an automobile is assigned to your employee from a fleet or pool on a long-term or exclusive basis, you can calculate the standby charge based solely on the cost of the automobile assigned to the employee.
    • If your employee uses more than one automobile and your fleet is mostly made up of similar automobiles, or if it can be grouped into a few similar groups, you can calculate the cost of the automobile based on the average cost of the group from which the automobile was provided to your employee.

      You and your employee must both approve this calculation method.

    Situation: You lease the automobile and you want to calculate using the detailed calculation

    Use this calculation if you lease the automobile.

      Cost of automobile

      What is included in the cost of the lease

      The cost of leasing the automobile includes the following:

      • Rental cost for the automobile
      • Any associated costs, such as maintenance contracts, excess mileage charges, terminal charges less terminal credits
      • GST/HST and PST paid to the lessor under the leasing contract

      The cost of leasing the automobile does not include liability and collision insurance costs.

      What are lump-sum lease payments

      Some lease contracts may contain clauses that reduce the monthly lease costs by requiring a substantial lump-sum payment at the start or end of the lease. Care must be taken to determine if it is actually a lease payment, not a payment aimed at purchasing the vehicle, which could change the calculation of standby charge for the vehicle. Payments made at the start of a lease contract are lease payments for the year and apply in full to the calculation of the benefit. The CRA will give the employer the option of proportionally attributing such payments over the term of the lease and calculating the benefit accordingly.

      A variation of this approach is seen when an employer provides a substantial refundable deposit at the start of a lease contract, after which the monthly lease costs are reduced and the deposit is credited to the purchase of the vehicle at the end of the lease contract or refunded if the employer does not purchase the vehicle. The value of the lease, when calculating the benefit received by the employee from the use of the vehicle, is the fair market value of the lease, equivalent to what the lease payments would have been if the deposit did not exist.

      Terminal credit - Lump sum payment you received at the end of a lease

      A lump sum payment you receive from the lessor at the end of a lease is considered to be a terminal credit. This means the standby charge for the automobile has been overstated because the lease costs should have been lower. In this situation, use one of the following methods:

      • Deduct the terminal credit from the lease costs in the year you end the lease
      • Amend the T4 or T4A slip of the employee who used the automobile and provide a letter explaining the reduction, as long as the employee agrees and can still ask for an income tax adjustment for the years in question

      Your employee can send a request to the CRA to adjust their T1 income tax and benefit returns for those years.

      Terminal charge - Lump sum payment you made at the end of a lease

      If you make a lump sum payment at the end of a lease, the CRA considers the payment to be a terminal charge. This means your lease costs should have been higher and the standby charge for the automobile has been understated. In this situation, use one of the following methods:

      • Add the terminal charge to the lease costs in the year you end the lease
      • Prorate the payment over the term of the lease and amend the T4 or T4A slip of the employee who used the automobile, as long as they agree and can still ask for an income tax adjustment for the years in question
