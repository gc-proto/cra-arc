Automobile - Standby charges and operating expense benefits

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may require your employee, a shareholder, or a partner to use an automobile to perform their duties of office or employment.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder
  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if the motor vehicle is an automobile

    The CRA defines automobiles and motor vehicles differently for tax purposes.

    Answer a few questions to determine if the vehicle is an automobile or a motor vehicle.

    Launch vehicle validator

    Vehicle validator

    Automobile and motor vehicle definitions

    The difference between an automobile or a motor vehicle depends on:

    • Seating capacity
    • Type of vehicle
    • Use in the course of employment

    As per the Income Tax Act:

    • An automobile is defined as a motor vehicle that is designed or adapted mainly to carry individuals and their baggage on highways and streets, and has a seating capacity of not more than the driver and eight passengers. Vans and family cars also belong to this category. Motor vehicles used for emergency response and mainly to earn income are excluded from the definition of the term automobile; for example, ambulances, taxis and hearses.
    • A motor vehicle is defined as an automotive vehicle designed or adapted to be used on highways and streets but does not include trolley buses (tramway or streetcar) and any vehicle that operates exclusively on rails.
    More than 8 passengers, plus a driver

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle.

    Less than 8 passengers, plus a driver

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if one of the following apply:

    • Primarily (50% or more) of the distance driven as a taxi
    • Primarily (50% or more) of the distance driven as a business of transporting passengers
    • Bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental, or leasing business
    • An ambulance
    • Clearly marked as a police or fire emergency response vehicle
    • Clearly marked as an emergency medical response vehicle that you use to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics
    • Van, pick-up truck or similar type vehicle (function and design similar to pick-up truck or van) that can be used for the transportation of goods, equipment or passengers to earn or produce income, and one of the following apply:
      • Back part of the vehicle adapted so that the vehicle is unable to carry passengers
      • Used 50% to 89% or more at a remote work location (at least 30 kilometres away from any community having a population of at least 40,000)
      • Used 90% or more (in the year it was bought or leased) to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • A hearse or other vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers

    A clearly marked emergency response vehicle means that the vehicle has decals and visible signs of being an emergency vehicle, including clearly marked police or fire vehicles and medical response vehicles, used to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics.

    If the vehicle is not one of the above, it is considered an automobile.

    • If the vehicle is a motor vehicle, do not continue to next step. Refer to Motor vehicle - Operating costs, allowances and reimbursements.
    • If the vehicle is an automobile, continue to Step 2 - Determine the regular place of employment

  2. Determine the regular place of employment

    Generally, you will have a written working arrangement with your employees on or before they start, this arrangement may include the following:

    • Address of the employer
    • Address of the expected place of work, including all the locations (if more than one)
    • Details of any work in a special work site, remote location or outside Canada for a long period (for example, more than 1 month)

    The location can become important during the employment. For example, when determining the regular place of employment.

  3. Determine the required information (logbook) your employee must keep if you make an automobile available

    If you make an automobile available to your employee, the employee must keep a logbook of the trips made with the automobile. They must give you a copy.

    When is an automibile considered made available to the employee

    An automobile is available to your employee if they have access to or control over the vehicle. It includes any part of a day, weekends and holidays during the calendar year.

    If your employee does not use your automobile for any personal driving, there is no taxable benefit, even if the automobile is available to your employee for the entire year. This applies as long as the kilometres driven by your employee were in the course of their employment duties and the vehicle is returned to your premises at the end of their work day.

    Examples - Automobile made available

    Examples of automobile made available
    Examples Result

    Employee is a technician who works for a local telephone company. The company permanently provides the employee with a service vehicle, unit 333. At the beginning of each work day, the employee uses unit 333 to conduct the service visits assigned to them. At the end of each day, the employee returns unit 333 to the company's parking lot and returns the keys to the employer.

    The benefit is not taxable to the employee because:

    • The automobile is made available during working hours
    • The automobile is only used for business purposes

    Employee is a regional manager for the same telephone company. Unit 222 is permanently assigned to the employee. One of the requirements of the employee's position requires them to travel abroad on a regular basis. As a result, the employee keeps the keys to the vehicle throughout the year. When travelling abroad, the employee leaves unit 222 at the airport. The employee also uses the vehicle to travel between their home and their workplace.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee because:

    • The automobile is made available throughout the year
    • The automobile is used for business and personal purposes

    As a work incentive, the employer makes an automobile available to an employee's spouse, to be driven for personal use.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee (not the spouse) because:

    • The automobile is made available throughout the year
    • The automobile is used for personal purposes to an employee's spouse

    The employee must record the following information:

    • Total number of days that the automobile was made available them during the year
    • Total number of kilometres travelled (on a daily, weekly or monthly basis) during the number of days that the automobile was made available.

    Logbook - Additional required information

    • Business use  information

      The employee must record, on a daily basis, for each trip made to perform their duties, the following information:

      • Place of departure and the place of destination
      • Number of kilometres travelled between those two places
      • Any other information to establish that the trip is made by the employee to perform their duties
    • Personal use  information

      If the automobile is used only for personal purposes, the employee must record the following information:

      • Total number of days that the automobile is made available to the employee (or to a person related to the employee) during the year
      • Kilometres registered on the odometer at the beginning and end of the year or of each period during which the automobile is made available, on a continuous basis, to the employee
    What is considered business use and personal use

    The personal use is when the employee's use of an automobile provided by the employer or a person related to the employer, for purposes other than the employer's business.

    • Business use

      • Travel from office workplace to client workplace (and vice versa)
      • Travel from office workplace for errands for business purposes (and vice versa)
      • Travel from home to client workplace (and vice versa)

    • Personal use

      • Travel from office workplace to home (and vice versa), even if the employer insist the employee to drive the vehicle home, for example:
        • Employee is on 24 hour call
        • Employer feels the security of the vehicle and its contents is better served at the employee's residence
        • More convenient or economical to have the vehicle driven to the employee's residence
      • Vacation trips

  4. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, if your employee uses one of your automobile to perform their duties of office or employment, the benefit is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you make an automobile available   to your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Automobile is for business use only  
    • Your employee was required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment
    • You tell your employee in writing that they cannot make any personal use of the vehicle, other than travelling between work and home

    Learn more about the factors to consider if you don't know:

    • Who is the primary beneficiary: employer or employee 
    • Worker classification: employee or self-employed 

    Taxable situation

    If you make an automobile available   to your employee and they use the automobile for both business and personal purposes  , the personal use is taxable. You need to calculate the value of the following:

    • Automobile standby charge benefit related to the availability and personal use
    • Automobile operating expense  benefit when you pay the operating costs of the automobile subject to a standby charge
    Examples of operating costs
    • Includes
      • Fuel
      • Oil
      • Maintenance costs
      • All repair costs, minus insurance payments
      • Licenses
      • Insurance costs, etc.
    • Does not include
      • Interest
      • Capital cost allowance (CCA) or leasing costs
      • Parking expenses

    Continue to Step 5 - Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the automobile benefit.

  5. Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the automobile benefit

    Reminder: You can use Automobile Benefits Online Calculator that will calculate the benefit for you.

    How to calculate the value using estimates during the year

    During the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit using estimates. To obtain the amount you must add to the employee’s salary for a given pay period, you must divide the value of the benefit you have estimated by the number of pay periods in the year.

    At the end of the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit related to an automobile using the actual number of kilometres travelled during the year.

    You must get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the automobile benefit. Based on these records, you need to determine if you can use the reduced standby charge calculation.

    • If the employee kept the required detailed records and you received a copy, the benefit meets the condition 1 of 4 for the reduced standby charge.

    • If no record is kept, the employee does not benefit from the reduction.

  6. Determine if the employee is required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment

    You need to determine if the employee was required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment.

    Learn more about the factors to consider if you don't know who is the primary beneficiary: employee or employer .

    • If the employer required the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the benefit meets condition 2 of 4 for the reduced standby charge.

    • If the employer did not require the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the employee does not benefit from the reduction.

  9. Calculate the standby charge benefit

    If you make an automobile available   to your employee, you must calculate the value of the standby charge related to the personal use  and the availability of the automobile.

    Situations

    You own the automobile - Calculate using the simplified calculation

    You can use simplified calculation if all the following conditions are met:

    • You own the automobile provided to the employee
    • The employee uses the same automobile throughout the year
    • The employee's duties do not consist primarily in selling or leasing automobiles
    • The employee is not entitled to a reduction of the value of the standby charge

    Calculations

    What is included in the cost of the automobile

    The cost of the automobile includes all of the following:

    • Cost of the automobile when it was bought, including options and accessories, and the GST/HST and PST, but not including any reduction for a trade-in
    • Cost of additions, including the GST/HST and PST, made to the automobile after it was bought

    Where the automobile was purchased from a non-arm's length person, the cost is generally equal to the fair market value when it was bought, including options and GST/HST or PST.

    How to calculate the cost of the automobile when used part of an automobile fleet

    During the year, when your employee uses at least one automobile that is part of an automobile fleet that you operate, the guidelines below apply for calculating the cost of the automobile.

    You and your employee must both approve this calculation method.

    • If an automobile is assigned to your employee from a fleet or pool on a long-term or exclusive basis, you can calculate the standby charge based solely on the cost of the automobile assigned to the employee
    • f your employee uses more than one automobile and your fleet is mostly made up of similar automobiles, or if it can be grouped into a few similar groups, you can calculate the cost of the automobile based on the average cost of the group from which the automobile was provided to your employee.

    You lease the automobile - Calculate using the detailed calculation

    Use this calculation if you lease the automobile.

      Cost of automobile

      What is included in the cost of the lease

      The cost of leasing the automobile includes the following:

      • Rental cost for the automobile
      • Any associated costs, such as maintenance contracts, excess mileage charges, terminal charges less terminal credits
      • GST/HST and PST paid to the lessor under the leasing contract

      The cost of leasing the automobile does not include liability and collision insurance costs.

      What are lump-sum lease payments

      Some lease contracts may contain clauses that reduce the monthly lease costs by requiring a substantial lump-sum payment at the start or end of the lease. Care must be taken to determine if it is actually a lease payment, not a payment aimed at purchasing the vehicle, which could change the calculation of standby charge for the vehicle. Payments made at the start of a lease contract are lease payments for the year and apply in full to the calculation of the benefit. The CRA will give the employer the option of proportionally attributing such payments over the term of the lease and calculating the benefit accordingly.

      A variation of this approach is seen when an employer provides a substantial refundable deposit at the start of a lease contract, after which the monthly lease costs are reduced and the deposit is credited to the purchase of the vehicle at the end of the lease contract or refunded if the employer does not purchase the vehicle. The value of the lease, when calculating the benefit received by the employee from the use of the vehicle, is the fair market value of the lease, equivalent to what the lease payments would have been if the deposit did not exist.

      Lump-sum payments paid or received at the end of the lease are considered to be terminal charges or credits.

  12. Determine if you need to remit the GST/HST for the benefit

    Generally, you have to remit GST/HST on the benefit related to automobile standby charges and operating expenses.

    GST/HST considered collected or not collected

    Situation: GST/HST not considered collected

    Under the Excise Tax Act, you are not considered to have collected the GST/HST if you make an election by filling out Form GST30, Election for Passenger Vehicles or Aircraft to be Deemed to be Used Exclusively in Non-Commercial Activities (or state in writing the information required on the form) and one of the following conditions apply:

    • You are an individual or a partnership and the vehicle that you have bought is used less than 90% in the commercial activities of the business
    • You are not an individual, a partnership, or a financial institution, and the vehicle that you bought is used 50% or less in the commercial activities of the business
    • You are a financial institution and choose to treat the vehicle you lease or have bought as being used only in non-commercial activities of the business
    • You are not a financial institution and you lease the vehicle which you use 50% or more in non-commercial activities of the business, and you choose to treat it as being used 90% or more in such non-commercial activities

    You do not have to send the Form GST30 to the CRA. You must keep this form in your records in case you are asked for it.

    If all conditions are met, you do not need to do other steps or calculations.

    Continue to Step 15 - Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

    Situation 2: GST/HST considered collected

    If the conditions above are not met, the GST/HST is considered to have been collected and needs to be remitted. Continue to Step 13 - Determine which GST/HST rate to apply.
