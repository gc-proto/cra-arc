Automobile or motor vehicle benefits – Allowances or reimbursements provided to an employee for the use of their own vehicle

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an allowance, an advance or reimburse your employee to compensate for use of their automobile or motor vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if the employee is required to use their vehicle in the course of their employment duties

    You need to determine if the employee was required to use their own automobile or motor vehicle in the course of their employment duties.

    • If you did not require your employee to use their own vehicle in the course of their employment duties, the benefit from the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to the employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If you required your employee to use their own vehicle in the course of their employment duties, the benefit from the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to the employee may not be taxable if they meet all conditions.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if there was business driving of the automobile

  2. Determine if there was business driving of the automobile

    Business driving is any driving done by an employee between work locations for purposes related to their employment duties. This includes regular places of employment and a point of call.

    Personal driving is any driving by an employee of a vehicle for purposes not related to their employment.

    • Business driving

      • Travel between office workplace and a client workplace
      • Travel between office workplace for errands for business purposes
      • Travel between home and a client workplace
      • Travel between home and a point of call, such as:
        • Salesperson visiting customers
        • Going to a client’s premises for a meeting
        • Making a repair call

    • Personal driving

      • Personal driving is any driving by an employee of a vehicle for purposes not related to their employment
      • Driving to conduct personal activities
      • Travel between home and a regular place of employment other than a point of call
      • Travel between home and a regular place of employment even if you insist the employee drives the vehicle home, such as:
        • When the employee is on call
        • When the employer believes the vehicle and its contents are more secure at the employee’s residence
        • Where it is more convenient or economical to have the vehicle driven to the employee’s residence

    • If there is no business driving, the benefit from the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to the employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If there is business driving, the benefit from the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to the employee may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Get the required records from your employee to confirm the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving

  3. Get the required records from your employee to confirm the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving

    Your employee should keep records of the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving made with their own vehicle. For example:

    • Relevant receipts
    • A log of the total number of kilometres driven all year as part of their employment duties
    • A log of each work trip taken that includes the date, destination and purpose of the trip

    You must get records from your employee, such as expense claims or a copy of the records mentioned above, to justify that the allowance, advance or reimbursement that you paid was based only on the number of kilometres for business driving.

    • If you do not receive the required records, the benefit is taxable because it does not meet the requirements of the Income Tax Act for the benefit to be only based on business kilometres driven.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If your employee kept the required detailed records and you received a copy, the benefit from the allowance, advance or reimbursement may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if special considerations apply to the benefit

  4. Determine if special considerations apply to the benefit

    The benefit may not be taxable if the benefit paid to your employee for the use of their vehicle in the course of their employment duties is done in one of the following special situations:

    • Refer to: Your employee is working in a prescribed zone
    • Refer to: Your employee has a severe or prolonged impairment
    • Refer to: Your employee works in a remote location
    • Refer to: Your employee works at a special work site
    • If the use of the vehicle is in one of the above special situations, do not continue to next step.

    • If the use of the vehicle is not in one of the above special situations, the benefit paid for the use of their in the course of their employment duties may be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if the rate per-kilometre is considered reasonable

  5. Determine if the rate per-kilometre is considered reasonable

    Generally, the CRA allows you to use the per-kilometre rates prescribed in section 7306 of the Income Tax Regulations (maximum amount deductible as a business expense) to determine if the rate used for amounts paid to your employee is reasonable (step 5a). 

    In exceptional situations, a rate other than the prescribed rate may be considered reasonable (step 5b).

    Example of rates per-kilometre not considered reasonable
    Example of rates per-kilometre not considered reasonable
    Examples Result

    You are a controller at a retirement home. Currently, employees receive a rate of $0.35 per kilometre for automobile and motor vehicle allowances when they have to travel for work to help the residents with their daily tasks. This rate was negotiated in the last collective agreement and the collective agreement is signed for four years. You understand that the $0.35 rate is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee because:

    • The rate per kilometre is not considered reasonable because it is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate
    • Even when the rates are part of a collective agreement, if the rates are lower or higher than the prescribed rate, the rate is not considered reasonable.
    • You may need to fill out Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, if your employee is eligible to deduct part of the cost

    1. Determine using the prescribed rates (in section 7306 of the Income Tax Regulations)

    2. Exceptional situation: Determine using a lower or a higher than the prescribed rate

      In exceptional situations, you may determine that a reasonable rate would be lower or higher than the prescribed rate. For example, factors such as the type of vehicle and the driving conditions may be situations where the rates may be different.

      However, when the rates are part of a collective agreement, if the rates are lower or higher, this is not considered a reasonable rate. 

      To make sure you are using a rate that the CRA would consider reasonable in this situation, you can ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA.

      Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    • If the rate per-kilometre is not considered reasonable (either higher or lower), the benefit is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If the rate per-kilometre is considered reasonable, the benefit may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Determine if the benefit is taxable

  6. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Depending on your situation, there may not be a taxable benefit for certain amounts you pay to your employees for the use of a vehicle they own and use in the course of their employment duties under the Income Tax Act or the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: You provide an allowance to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you paid an allowance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The allowance is calculated only on the actual number of kilometres for business driving , (in the course of their employment duties) (step 1)
    • The reasonable rate per-kilometre  is used to calculate (if the rate is lower or higher , you must be able to justify your position) (step 4)
    • If you reimburse your employee for other expenses related to the same use of the vehicle, the reimbursement is only for supplementary business insurance, or tolls and ferry charges not paid as part of the allowance

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance provided to your employee for automobile or motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    For example, the benefit is taxable in one of the following situations:

    • Allowances paid at per-kilometre rates that are not considered reasonable because they are either too high or too low
    • Flat-rate allowances paid to employees where the amount paid is not related to the number of kilometres driven
    • Allowances that combine flat-rate and reasonable per-kilometre allowances for the same use of the vehicle
    • Allowances received where the employee is also reimbursed for the same use of the vehicle (except where the reimbursement is in respect of supplementary business insurance or toll or ferry charges and the amount of the allowance was determined without reference to those reimbursed expenses)
    Examples of rates per-kilometre not considered reasonable
    Examples of rates per-kilometre not considered reasonable
    Examples Result

    You own an office supply company where you employ workers on the road as salespersons. Each salesperson has a specific territory to cover and they use their own vehicle. They are paid a monthly flat rate allowance of $600 no matter where they go or how far they drive. This was agreed on when they were hired and is cited in their respective employment contracts.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee because:

    • The flat rate allowance is not considered reasonable because it is not based on the number of business kilometres driven

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat per-diem rate to offset the employee's fixed expenses for each day the vehicle is required. You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for each kilometre driven to offset the operating expenses.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee because:

    • The flat per-diem rate compensates the employee for some of the same use on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based. That is, the fixed expenses incurred by the employee to operate the vehicle.
    • The combined amount is considered one allowance and it is not based only on the number of kilometres the vehicle is used for employment purposes.

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat-rate per month for travel inside the employment district. You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for employment-related travel outside the employment district.

    The benefit is partly taxable to the employee because:

    • The flat-rate allowance does not cover any of the same use of the vehicle on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based, the allowances are considered separately.
    • The reasonable per-kilometre allowance paid for travel outside the district is not taxable.
    • The amount based on a flat-rate paid for travel inside the district is taxable, since it is not based only on the number of kilometres for which the vehicle is used in connection with the employment.
    Situation: You reimburse your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you reimburse your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The reimbursement is only for the use of your employee's vehicle for business driving , (in the course of their employment duties) (step 1)
    • You received a copy of the required records to confirm the amounts reimbursed (step 2)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount you reimburse being reasonable (step 3)

    Taxable situation

    If the reimbursement you provide to your employee for automobile or motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Example - Taxable

    An employee uses their vehicle approximately 5 times per month to do deliveries for their employer. In exchange for this service, the employer provides the employee with a company credit card to pay for gas for their automobile.

    Based on gas, insurance and wear and tear, the employee estimates that the cost of using their automobile for work totals $250 per year. The company's credit card statements show that the employee spent $1,500 in gas and insurance in the year.

    • $1,500 is value in gas and insurance in the year
    • minus $250 is the cost of using their automobile for work
    • equals $1,250 is the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip
    Situation: You provide an accountable advance to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you provide an accountable advance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The advance is only for the use of your employee's vehicle for business driving , (in the course of their employment duties) (step 1)
    • You received the required records (vouchers or expense claims for their kilometres travelled) to confirm the amounts paid as an accountable advance (step 2)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount of the accountable advance being reasonable (step 3)
    • You can justify your position regarding a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount (step 5)
    • You document in your employee’s record the method for establishing and keeping track of the accountable advance
    • No other provision of the Income Tax Act requires you to include the advances in the employee’s income
    • If your employee’s expenses were under the amount of the advance, the employee was required to return the difference by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • If your employee’s expenses were over the advance amount, you topped up the advance by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest

    You are not allowed to take a flat-rate or lump-sum allowance not based on the amount of kilometres driven and average it at the end of the year to determine a reasonable per-kilometre rate and then be excluded from the employee’s income.

    Examples of rates per-kilometre considered reasonable
    Examples of rates per-kilometre not considered reasonable
    Examples Result

    You own a sporting goods store. There is an out of town sales conference your business is participating in and you have scheduled one salesperson to attend. Two weeks prior to travel, you pay an accountable advance to your salesperson to cover expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicle to travel to and from the conference. This amount is based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate. The salesperson accounted for their expenses.

    The benefit is not taxable to the employee because:

    • The rate per kilometre is considered reasonable
    • The employee accounted for their expenses by producing vouchers and returned any amount they did not spend

    You are a manager for an insurance company. Your employees travel frequently to meet new and existing clients as part of their duties. Given the frequency of travel of representatives, you use the averaging allowances method to calculate the accountable advance your employees receive each pay to cover eligible expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicles. The amounts are based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate, and are recorded in the employees' files.

    At the end of the year, the employees provide you with a log book that lists the amount of business kilometres they have travelled and any advances they have received. You review the advances paid to your employees and their eligible expenses, at which time the company will pay any amounts owing to the employee or the employee will reimburse any amounts received that exceed their eligible expenses.

    The benefit is not taxable to the employee because:

    • There is a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount
    • The rate and the advance are reasonable under the circumstances
    • The method is documented in the employee's file

    Taxable situation

    If the accountable advance you provide to your employee for automobile or motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: Self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership owns or leases a motor vehicle

    The information on this webpage does not apply to a self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership who personally owns or leases a motor vehicle to earn income from the business.

    Learn more:

    • IT521R – Motor Vehicle Expenses Claimed by Self-Employed Individuals
    • Business and Professional Income (T4002)
    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to: Step 7 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

  7. Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    If the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to the employee for the use of their own vehicle is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to the value of the benefit paid to the employee in the year. The amount must be included on the T4 slip of the employee.

  8. Determine if you need to fill out Form T2200 if the benefit is taxable

    You may need to fill out Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, if your employee is eligible to deduct part of the cost.

    If you include an allowance as income for your employee and the allowance is considered to low, you must fill out and sign Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment and your employee can deduct:

    • An amount up to the amount of the allowance (if the employee did not keep a record or does not wish to claim employment expenses)
    • Expenses paid in the year (if a record was kept)
    • No expense if the allowance is not added to his or her income

    By signing the form, you are only certifying that the employee met the conditions of employment and had to pay for the expenses under their employment contract.

    It is the employee's responsibility to claim the expenses on their income tax and benefits return and to keep records to support the claim.

    Examples

    Example 1

    You allow your employees to use their personal motor vehicle for business and pay them a monthly motor vehicle allowance to pay for the operating expenses and you include the allowance in the employees' employment income as a taxable benefit.

    Example 2

    You have a formal telework arrangement with your employees that allows the employees to work at home. Your employees pays for the expenses of their work space on their own.
