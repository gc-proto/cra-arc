Start, stop or restart CPP deductions

For current employees or pensioners, you need determine if you need to stop deducting CPP because the employee reached the maximum contribution for the year.

This applies to each job the employee holds with different employers (different business numbers). If an employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if the employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

Employee who turn 18

You must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after the employee turns 18.

Employee who turn 70

You must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee turns 70.

Employee between the age of 65 and 70 who submit Form CPT30

Employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP

An employee who is considered to be disabled under the CPP does not have to contribute to the CPP.

You must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee is considered to be disabled according to Service Canada.

When prorating, you must use the number of months up to and including the month the employee was considered to be disabled.

What happens if the employee is no longer considered disabled

If the employee is no longer considered disabled, you must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after the employee is no longer considered disabled.

When prorating, you must count the number of months starting with the month after the employee ceased to be disabled.

Deceased employee

You must prorate the maximum CPP contribution deductions when an employee dies in the year.
