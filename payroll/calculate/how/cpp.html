Calculate CPP contributions deductions

How to use the CPP contributions tables and how to calculate manually the amount to withhold.

If the employee's province of employment is in Quebec, you are required to deduct the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) contributions and not the CPP contributions, refer to: Québec Pension Plan Contributions | Revenu Québec

Calculation methods

Reminder: There are online calculators that will calculate the CPP deductions for you.

Calculate CPP deductions based on the type of payment:

  • Regular payments (CPP tables)
  • Bonuses, retroactive or irregular payments
  • Any payments (manual calculation)
  • Year-end verification or multiple pay periods

Regular payments (CPP tables)

CPP contribution tables calculate the required CPP contribution for you on given ranges of income for a specified pay period. The share of the annual exemption for that pay period has already been factored into these calculations.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use CPP contribution tables

    CPP contribution tables can be used in most common situations.

    When to use or not to use the tax tables

    Use when

    • The payment is for regular employment income or was made with regular pay for one of the common or uncommon pay periods for which tables are provided
    • If you received Form TD1-IN (in addition to the TD1) from your employee, only use these tables on the portion of their income which is taxable

    Do not use when

    • The payment (for example, bonuses, commissions, retroactive pay) is made separately from regular pay
    • Your pay period does not have a table provided in the common or uncommon pay periods per year
    • You are doing a year-end verification of your CPP contributions
    • The employee already reached their CPP maximum contributions for the year

  2. Determine if the employee reached the maximum contribution

    Use the calculation depending if your employee is pensionable for the full year or if your employee is pensionable only for part of the year (prorate) .

    Calculation - Employee pensionable for full year
    • Maximum annual employee CPP contributions  in their employment with you
    • minus Employee's contributions to date for the year in their employment with you
    • equals This amount is the maximum CPP contribution that you can deduct from your employee's pay for the rest of the year
      • Calculation example
      • $3,499.80 is the maximum 2022 annual CPP contribution
      • minus $300 is the amount Joseph contributed for the year to date (in Joseph's employment with you)
      • equals $3,199.80 is the maximum CPP amount you can deduct
    Calculation - Employee pensionable only for part of the year (prorate)
    • Maximum annual pensionable earnings 
    • minus Basic exemption amount ($3,500)
    • equals This amount is the maximum CPP contributory earnings
    • multiply by Number of months the employee is pensionable
    • equals Result
    • divide by 12 months
    • equals Maximum prorated pensionable earnings
    • multiply by CPP rate 
    • equals This amount is the maximum prorated CPP contribution in their employment with you
    • minus Employee's contributions to date for the year in their employment with you
    • equals This amount is the maximum CPP contribution that you can deduct from your employee's pay for the rest of the year
    Calculation example

    Joseph turns 18 on October 24, 2022 and will be pensionable for two months.

    • $64,900 is the maximum annual pensionable earnings in their employment with you
    • minus $3,500 for the basic exemption amount
    • equals $61,400 is the maximum CPP contributory earnings
    • multiply by 2 months that Joseph is pensionable
    • equals $122,800
    • divide by 12 months
    • equals $10,233 is the maximum prorated pensionable earnings
    • multiply by 5.7% is the CPP rate
    • equals $583.30 is the maximum prorated CPP contribution in their employment with you
    • minus $300.00 is the employee's contributions to date for the year in Joseph's employment with you
    • equals $283.30 is the maximum CPP contribution that you can deduct from your employee's pay for the rest of the year
    • Do not deduct CPP contributions if they have reached the maximum.
    • Continue to next step if they have not reached the maximum.

  4. Determine the pay range

    Find the range that includes your employee's gross remuneration (including any taxable benefits) in the "Pay" column

  5. Get the amount of CPP contributions to deduct

    Find the amount in the "CPP" column which is next to the the column "Pay"

  6. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to withhold

    Use the amount that applies to your situation to calculate step 7:

    • If the amount in step 5 is less than step 2, withhold the amount from step 5
    • If the amount in step 5 is greater than step 2, withhold the amount from step 2

  7. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to remit

    • CPP contributions you have to withhold from your employee (step 6)
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals This is the total amount you have to remit: your employee's share and your share of the CPP contribution
    Calculation example
    • Employee's contribution is $240.40
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals Total CPP contributions to remit is $480.80: your employee's share and your share of the CPP contribution
Bonuses, retroactive or irregular payments

In addition to regular types of payments, you must deduct CPP contributions from bonuses, retroactive payments or other types of irregular payments.

Steps

  1. Determine if you can use the bonus method

    • If the payment is made with regular pay, do not use the following steps to calculate. Use one of the following:

      • Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC)
      • Calculations of CPP, EI and income tax deductions using the tax tables

    • If the payment is made separately from regular pay, continue to next step (because no basic exemption is included)

  2. Determine if the employee reached the maximum contribution

  3. Determine the amount of CPP contributions to deduct

    • Bonus, retroactive or irregular payment
    • multiply by CPP rate 
    • equals This amount is the CPP contribution to deduct
    Calculation example
    • Joseph receives a retroactive pay increase of $450.00 on June 29 2022
    • multiply by CPP rate is 5.70%
    • equals $25.65 is the amount of CPP contribution to deduct

  4. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to withhold

    Use the amount that applies to your situation to calculate step 5:

    • If the amount in step 3 is less than step 2, withhold the amount from step 3
    • If the amount in step 3 is greater than step 2, withhold the amount from step 2

  5. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to remit

    • CPP contributions you have to withhold from your employee (step 4)
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals This is the total amount you have to remit: your employee'share and your share of the CPP contribution
    Calculation example
    • Employee's contribution is $240.40
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals Total CPP contributions to remit is $480.80: your employee's share and your share of the CPP contribution

Any payments (manual calculation)

Manual calculation can also be used for all payments made to employees to calculate the CPP contribution that must be withheld for the pay period.

Steps

  1. Determine if the employee reached the maximum contribution

  2. Calculate the basic pay-period exemption

    Use the basic exemption amount  that matches your pay period.

  3. Calculate the total pensionable income

    • Employee's gross pay for the period
    • plus Employee's taxable benefits and allowances for the period
    • minus Total earnings that do not require CPP contributions
    • equals This is the total pensionable income
    Calculation example

    Joseph receives a weekly salary of $500 and $50 in taxable benefits.

    • $500 is Joseph's gross pay for the period
    • plus $50 is Joseph's taxable benefits for the period
    • minus $0 because all of Joseph's earnings require CPP contributions
    • equals $550 is Joseph's total pensionable income for the period

  4. Determine the amount of CPP contributions to deduct

    • Total pensionable income (step 3)
    • minus Basic pay-period exemption (step 2)
    • multiply by CPP rate 
    • equals This amount is the CPP contribution to deduct
    Calculation example

    Joseph's total pensionable income for the period is $550

    • $550 is Joseph's total pensionable income
    • minus $67.30 is Joseph's basic pay-period exemption
    • multiply by 5.70% is the 2022 CPP rate
    • equals $27.51 is the amount of CPP contribution to deduct

  5. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to withhold

    Use the amount that applies to your situation to calculate step 6:

    • If the amount in step 4 is less than step 1, withhold the amount from step 4
    • If the amount in step 4 is greater than step 1, withhold the amount from step 1

  6. Calculate the amount of CPP contributions you have to remit

    • CPP contributions you have to withhold from your employee (step 5)
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals This is the total amount you have to remit: your employee's share and your share of the CPP contribution
    Calculation example

    Joseph's amount of CPP contribution to deduct is $27.51

    • Employee's contribution is $27.51
    • multiply by 2 (matching employer CPP contribution)
    • equals The total CPP contributions to remit is $55.02: your employee's share and your share of the CPP contribution
Year-end verification or multiple pay periods

Year-end or multiple pay period verification is used to determine if you have deducted properly, under deducted or over deducted CPP contributions for the period.

Steps

  1. Calculate the amount of pensionable earnings for the period of employment

    • Employee’s total salary, wages benefits, and allowances for the total period which will be included in box 14 of their T4 slip
    • minus Employee’s earnings:
      • Received before and including the month they turned 18
      • Received after the month they turned 70
      • Received after the effective date of their completed and signed Form CPT30 to elect to stop contributing to the CPP
      • Received before and including the month where the employee provided you a completed and signed Form CPT30 to start contributing to the CPP
      • The amount the employee received while considered to be disabled under the CPP or QPP
      • From employment, benefits, and payments from which you do not deduct CPP
    • equals This amount is the pensionable earnings for the period of employment

  2. Confirm the amount of pensionable earnings for the period of employment

    Use the amount that applies to your situation to calculate step 3:

    • If the amount in step 1 is less than Maximum annual pensionable earnings  in their employment with you, use this amount
    • If the amount in step 1 is greater than Maximum annual pensionable earnings  in their employment with you, use the maximum pensionable earnings

  3. Determine the amount of required CPP contributions for the period

    • Pensionable earnings for the period of employment (step 2)
    • minus Basic exemption amount  that matches your pay period multiply by Number of pay periods of pensionable earnings
    • equals Employee’s contributory earnings for the period
    • multiply by CPP rate 
    • equals This amount is the employee's required CPP contributions for the period

  4. Determine if you have under or over remitted for the period

    • Employee's required CPP contributions for the period (step 3)
    • minus CPP contributions you deducted from the employee for the period
    • equals This amount should be $0 if you have deducted correctly
    • If the amount is positive you have under deducted for the year
    • If the amount is negative you have over deducted for the year

    To correct a deduction error:

    • If you have not filed the information return, refer to: Make corrections before filing
    • If you already filed the information return, refer to: Make corrections after filing
