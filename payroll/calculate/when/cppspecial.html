Special situations - Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions

You may be looking for if you are a self-employed worker:

  • Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

Depending on the situation, you may need to start, stop or restart withholding CPP contributions. The following situations include information on how proration or other considerations will impact how you calculate CPP contributions:

You can filter by key words to find information specific to your situation.

    Other employee situations

  • Your employee works for more than 1 employer

    The CPP annual maximum pensionable earnings apply to each job your employee holds with different employers (different business numbers).

    If your employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if your employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

    Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. You are not entitled to a refund for your share of CPP contributions.

    Example

    Situation

    • Your employee notifies you that they have reached the maximum CPP contribution for the year. They tell you that they had $1,700 in CPP contributions deducted by a previous employer in the year and that when combined with the CPP you have deducted from their pay, they have reached the maximum.

    Result

    • Do not stop deducting CPP contributions from their pay. You are required to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pay until they reach the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you. Do not factor in CPP contributions made through other employers.
  • Your employee's province of employment is Quebec

    If you have employees whose province of employment is Quebec, regardless of your employee's province or territory of residence, you have to deduct Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) contributions instead of CPP contributions.

    Learn more: Québec Pension Plan Contributions | Revenu Québec

  • Your employee is transferred from an establishment in Quebec to an establishment in another province or territory

    You may have an establishment in Quebec and in another province or territory. If you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory, you must take into account the QPP contributions you deducted from that employee throughout the year when calculating the maximum CPP contributions to deduct.

    The following formula reconciles the amounts contributed to the CPP and QPP to make sure that enough contributions to the CPP are withheld and future benefits are not affected:

    Step 1 - Determine the conversion factor

    • CPP contribution rate 
    • divide by QPP contribution rate
    • equals This is the conversion factor

    Step 2 - Calculate the QPP contribution based on the conversion factor

    • QPP contributions withheld from your employees pay periods
    • multiply by Amount of the conversion factor
    • equals This amount is the CPP equivalent to include in the annual maximum CPP contributions for the year
    Example - Calculation with CPP contributions

    The employer has establishments in both Quebec and Newfoundland. After the first 2 pay periods in March 20X5, the employer transfers their employee Melodie to their Newfoundland establishment. She had $400 in QPP contributions withheld from her 2 pay periods as an employee with a province of employment of Quebec.

    To determine the amount of CPP contributions to withhold, the employer applies the following formula to determine the amount to take into account in making CPP contributions:

    Step 1 - Determine the conversion factor

    • 5.95% is the CPP contribution rate in March 20X5
    • divide by 6.40% is the QPP contribution rate in March 20X5
    • equals 0.9296875 is the conversion factor
    Step 2 - Calculate the QPP contribution based on the conversion factor
    • $400 is the QPP contributions withheld from Melodie's 2 pay periods
    • multiply by 0.9296875 is the amount of the conversion factor
    • equals $371.88 is the CPP equivalent to include in the annual maximum CPP contributions for the year
    Example - Calculation with CPP2 contributions

    The employer has establishments in both Quebec and Newfoundland. The employer transferred their employee Kamal to their Newfoundland establishment after she had made the annual maximum QPP contribution and began to make QPP2 contributions. She had $40 in QPP2 contributions withheld from her additional pensionable earnings as an employee with a province of employment of Quebec.

    To determine the amount of CPP2 contributions to withhold, the employer applies the following formula to determine the amount to take into account in making CPP2 contributions:

    Step 1 - Determine the conversion factor

    • 4% is the CPP2 contribution rate in March 20X5
    • divide by 4% is the QPP2 contribution rate in March 20X5
    • equals 1 is the conversion factor
    Step 2 - Calculate the QPP2 contribution based on the conversion factor
    • $40 is the QPP2 contributions withheld from Kamal's 2 pay periods
    • multiply by 1 is the amount of the conversion factor
    • equals $40 is the CPP2 equivalent to include in the annual maximum CPP2 contributions for the year

    Learn more: Guide T4127, Payroll Deductions Formulas

    How to report on T4 slips

    In addition to deducting CPP/QPP contributions, you will also have to prepare at least two T4 slips:

    • One for your employee’s earnings with a province of employment of Quebec
    • One for their earnings with the other province of employment

    It is important that you calculate and report the proper deductions and insurable/pensionable earnings on each T4 slip.

  • Your employee works outside Canada and you have a business in Canada

    Learn more: Employees who are working outside Canada for a Canadian employer

  • Your employee is a First Nations employee

    The employment income of a First Nations employee which is exempt from income tax is not subject to CPP contributions. If their employment income is not tax exempt, it will be subject to CPP deductions.

    How to apply for CPP coverage

    Election by the employer

    You have the option to extend CPP coverage to your First Nations employees who are exempt from tax by filing out Form CPT124, Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada Under the Canada Pension Plan Whose Income is Exempt Under the Income Tax Act.

    However, you cannot revoke this election and you have to cover all First Nations employees.

    When to start CPP contributions

    If all conditions are met and you signed Form CPT124, you must start deducting CPP contributions on either the date you sign the application or on a later date that you specify on the form. It cannot be retroactive to a date before the date you signed the application.

    Election by the employee

    If you do not choose to cover CPP for your First Nations employees who are exempt from tax, a First Nations employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings by filling out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

    Learn more: First Nations workers and the Canada Pension Plan

  • You (as the employer) are restructuring

    Where you (as the employer) is restructuring, the successor employer may, under certain situations, consider the amounts of CPP deducted, remitted or paid by the former employer for the employment of the employees for the year as if they had been deducted, remitted, or paid by the successor employer.
