Special situations - Employment Insurance (EI) premiums

You may be looking for if you are a self-employed worker:

  • Benefits for self-employed people

Depending on your or your employee’s situation, you will need to review your EI withholding obligations. You may also have to change how you calculate your employee or employer shares of the EI premiums.

You can filter by key words to find information specific to your situation.

    Employee situations

  • Your employee died in the year

    If your employee died in the year, you must deduct EI premiums from amounts paid up to the date of death.

    Do not deduct EI premiums from amounts earned before the death of an employee (such as salary, banked overtime, a bonus, or vacation pay) and not yet paid at the time of death.

  • You hire an employee who is a family member or a related person

    If your employee is related to you (individually or as a corporation) as their employer, they may not be in insurable employment. However, the related employee can be in insurable employment if you would have negotiated a similar contract with a person with whom you deal at arm’s length .

    How to determine if you would have negotiated a similar contract

    All the facts about your working relationship must be reviewed to see if someone else would have been hired under a similar contract of employment.

    Generally, the CRA reviews the following 4 factors when looking at the working relashionship:

    • Remuneration paid
    • Terms and conditions of employment, such as the hours of work
    • Duration of work performed
    • Nature and importance of the work performed by your employee

    Learn more: Not dealing at arm's length for purposes of the Employment Insurance Act (EIA)

  • Your employee works for more than 1 employer

    The EI annual maximum insurable earnings apply to each job the employee holds with different employers (different business numbers).

    If your employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct EI premiums without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if your employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

    Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. As an employer, you are not entitled to a refund for your share of EI premiums.

    Example

    Situation

    • Your employee notifies you that they have reached the maximum EI premiums for the year. They tell you that they had $900 in EI premiums deducted by a previous employer in the year and that when combined with the EI you have deducted from their pay, they have reached the maximum.

    Result

    • Do not stop deducting EI premiums from their pay. You are required to deduct EI premiums from your employee's pay until they reach the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you. Do not factor in EI premiums made through other employers.
  • Your employee's province of employment is Quebec

    If you have employees whose province of employment is Quebec, regardless of your employee's province or territory of residence, you have to deduct a reduced employment insurance (EI) premium using the Quebec EI premium rates and maximums in addition to the Québec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) premiums.

    Learn more: Québec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) Premiums | Revenu Québec

  • Your employee is transferred from an establishment in Quebec to an establishment in another province or territory

    You may have an establishmenta in Quebec and in another province or territory.

    How to report on T4 slips

    In addition to deducting EI/QPIP premiums, you will also have to prepare at least two T4 slips:

    • One for your employee’s earnings with a province of employment of Quebec
    • One for their earnings with the other province of employment

    It is important that you calculate and report the proper deductions and insurable/pensionable earnings on each T4 slip.

    • Employer situations

  • You (as the employer) are restructuring

    Where there was an employer restructuring, the successor employer may consider the EI premiums previously deducted, remitted or paid by the former employer for the employment of the employees for the year as if they had been deducted, remitted, or paid by the successor employer.

    Learn more: Employer restructuring/Succession of employers - CPP/EI explained

  • You (as the employer) have employees outside Canada

    If you have employees working outside (or partly outside) Canada, you must deduct EI premiums if all of the following applies:

    • You, as the employer, reside in Canada or have a place of business in Canada
    • Your employee usually resides in Canada
    • The employment is not insurable in the country where the employment is done
    • The employment is not excluded from insurable employment for any other reason

     Do not deduct EI premiums if the above conditions are not met.

    Learn more: Employment outside Canada.

  • You (as the employer) provides a wage-loss replacement plan for short-term disability (EI premium reduction rate)

    If you provide a wage loss replacement plan for short-term disability to your employees, you can apply for the EI premium reduction rate through Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC).

    Learn more on how and when to apply for a premium reduction through ESDC, including what you have to send with your application:ESDC | EI Premium Reduction Program: For employers

    Opening a separate payroll account

    You have to send a request to the CRA to open a separate payroll account  under your existing business number (BN) for employees participating in a wage-loss replacement plan for short-term disability.

    Once ESDC approved your request for the EI premium reduction rate, you have to inform the CRA and ESDC which account will be assigned as the EI premium reduction rate account.

    Learn more: Open or manage a payroll account

    Calculating EI premiums

    Once approved by ESDC:

    • You will have a reduced EI premium rate that is less than the standard 1.4 times the employee premium (for example, 1.24 times)
    • Your reduced rate will only apply to employees who are covered by the approved plan, including employees serving an eligibility period under the plan of 3 months or less

    Learn more: Calculate EI premiums deductions

    Reporting on T4 slips

    You have to report on separate T4 slips for each payroll account under your BN. For example:

    • RP0001, EI premium reduction rate - For employees covered under an approved plan
    • RP0002, Standard rate of 1.4 times the employee's premiums - For employees who are not covered by the plan

    Where an employee was transferred between both accounts in the same calendar year, you also have to file a separate T4 slip for each account.

    Learn more: How to file

What is a related person

There are several ways employees are considered to be related to the employer.

  • Individuals

    Individuals can be related:

    Persons connected by blood relationship

    The following individuals are related to each other by blood:

    • A parent and a child (or other descendant, such as a grandchild or a great-grandchild)
    • A brother and a sister

    Blood relatives do not include more distant relatives such as aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews or cousins.

    Example
    Description

    Zuly and Silvia are sisters. Zuly is married to Juan and Silvia is married to Yuri. Zuly and Silvia are related because they are sisters. Zuly and Juan are related by marriage. Silvia and Yuri are related by marriage.

    Result

    As a result of their marriages, Yuri is related to Zuly and Juan is related to Silvia, but Juan and Yuri are not related to each other.

    Persons connected by marriage

    Individuals are related to each other if they are legally married to each other. They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their spouse.

    If the marriage is dissolved because of divorce or death of a spouse, the individual ceases to be connected by marriage.

    Depending on the situation, the person may be related by adoption to any children of their spouse, continue to: Adoption.

    Example
    Description

    Jack and Jill were married on June 15, 2016. Later, their marriage broke down and they were legally divorced on October 1, 2019.

    Result

    For the period from the date of marriage until the date of their legal divorce, Jack and Jill were related to each other as well as their respective parents-in-law and brothers/sisters-in-law.

    Persons connected by common-law partnership

    Individuals are related to each other if they are in a common-law partnership with each other. The CRA considers that 2 individuals are in a common-law partnership in one of the following situations:

    • They live together in a conjugal relationship and have done so for a continuous period of at least 12 months
    • They live together in a conjugal relationship and have a child together (this includes through adoption), in which case the 12-month period does not apply

    They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their common-law partner.

    If there is a breakdown in the relationship, they are considered to continue to live in a common-law partnership until they have lived separate and apart for 90 consecutive days.

    Depending on the situation, the person may be related by adoption to any children of their common-law partner, depending on the circumstances. Adoption.

    Example
    Description

    Jack and Jill had a child together. They began living together in a conjugal relationship 1 month before their child was born.

    Result

    They are common-law partners as of the day their child is born. If they did not have a child together, they would not be common-law partners until they lived together for 12 months.

    Persons connected by adoption

    Individuals are related to each other through adoption if one individual is the adopted child of the other.

    In addition to their parents, the child is also related to their siblings, grandparents and great-grandparents.

    Adoption includes legal adoption and de facto adoption.

    The following factors are considered when determining whether a de facto adoption exists:

    • Actual control and custody
    • An exercise of parental care and responsibility on a continuing basis
    • Dependency
    • Proximity to each other
    Example
    Description

    Jack and Jill are related to 2 of their children by blood (Mark and Tom) and one of their children by adoption (Eleanor).

    Result

    Eleanor is related to her siblings (Mark and Tom) and her 4 grandparents (the parents of Jack and Jill).

  • Corporation

    Your employee can also be related to you (as the employer) of your corporation when your employee is related to a person who either controls the corporation or is a member of a related group that controls the corporation.

    Learn more on how to determine if a person is a related person: Income Tax Folio S1-F5-C1, Related Persons and Dealing at Arm's Length
