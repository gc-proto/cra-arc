About the deduction of Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions

Depending on the following situations, you will need to review your CPP withholding obligations. You may also have to change how you calculate your employee or employer shares of the CPP contributions.

Find the topic:

  • Your employee works for more than one employer or you (as the employer) are restructuring
  • Your employee's province of employment is Quebec, including if employees are transferred in another province or territory
  • Your employee works outside Canada and you have a business in Canada
  • You (as the employer) have a business outside Canada
  • Your employee is a First Nations employee

What are CPP pensionable earnings

Pensionable earnings are the employee's remuneration from pensionable office or employment with certain exceptions.

Generally, you must deduct CPP contributions from:

  • Salary, wages or other remuneration
  • Commissions
  • Bonuses
  • Most taxable benefits
  • Honorariums
  • Certain tips and gratuities

To determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions, refer to:

  • Determine if a benefit is taxable
  • Determine the tax treatment of payments other than regular employment income

When to start, stop or restart CPP contributions

  • You have to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pensionable earnings if your employee meets all of the following conditions:

    • Your employee is in pensionable employment during the year
    • Your employee is between 18 and 69 years old, even if the employee is receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension, and has not given you a completed Form CPT30
    • Your employee is not considered to be disabled under the CPP or the QPP

  • You do not deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pensionable earnings if your employee meets one of the following conditions:

    • Your employee is under 18 or 70 years old or older
    • Your employee is considered disabled under the CPP or QPP
    • Your employee is between 65 and 69, is receiving a CPP/QPP retirement pension and has submitted Form CPT30 to stop contributing

Employer CPP contributions

You must also contribute an amount equal to the CPP contributions that you deduct from your employees' remuneration and remit the total of both amounts. Even if the required deductions were not made, you are deemed to have made them and the deductions not being made may result in a PIER.

Learn more: Pensionable and insurable earnings review (PIER)

Special situations

Depending on the situation, you may need to start, stop or restart withholding CPP contributions. The following situations include information on how proration or other considerations will impact how you calculate CPP contributions:

  • Your employee reaches the maximum contribution in the year

    When to stop CPP contributions

    You must stop deducting CPP contributions once your employee reaches the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you, either:

    • The annual maximum CPP contribution (and annual maximum CPP2 contribution, if applicable)
    • Your employee reached their prorated maximum CPP contribution (and annual maximum CPP2 contribution, if applicable)
  • Your employee turns 18 in the year

    When to start CPP contributions

    If your employee turns 18 in the year, you must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after your employee turns 18.

    For example, if your employee turns 18 on March 20, 20X5, you must start deducting CPP contributions from the first pay dated in the month of April 20X5.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months after your employee turns 18.

  • Your employee turns 70 in the year

    When to stop CPP contributions

    If your employee turns 70 in the year, you must continue to deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month when your employee turns 70.

    For example, if your employee turns 70 on March 20, 20X5, you must stop deducting CPP contributions after the last pay dated in the month of March 20X5.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month your employee turns 70.

  • Your employee is at least 65, but under 70, and gives you a completed Form CPT30 to stop CPP contributions

    Your employee can elect to stop contributing to CPP if they meet all of the following conditions:

    • Your employee is at least 65 years of age, but under 70
    • Your employee receives a CPP or QPP retirement pension

    When is the election considered made

    The election is considered to have been made when your employee gives you a copy of the completed and signed Form CPT30.

    When to stop CPP contributions

    If your employee meets all of the above conditions and gives you a signed Form CPT30 Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election with parts A, B and C completed, you must stop deducting CPP contributions on the first pay in the month following the month your employee has given you the signed copy of this form.

    For example, if Form CPT30 is signed on March 20, 20X5 and given to you on the next day, the election is effective as of April 1, 20X5.

    They must also provide you with:

    • Proof of age
    • Proof that you are receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension (for example, a copy of the award letter)

    If Form CPT30 is dated in the past and was either provided to a different employer or your employee providing it to you was delayed due to unforeseen circumstances, the form can be accepted. However, the form must reflect the actual date it was completed, backdating is not permitted.

    Make sure to keep a copy of all completed CPT30 forms with your payroll records.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month before the election becomes effective.

    What if you over deducted CPP contributions

    If you deducted CPP contributions from your employee after the effective date of the election, these contributions are considered a CPP overpayment.

    What if the worker is self-employed

    A self-employed worker cannot use Form CPT30 to elect to stop contributing to the CPP for their self-employed earnings. Instead, they must complete the Schedule 8, Canada Pension Plan Contributions and Overpayment (for all except QC).

    Learn more: Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

    What if your employee's province of employment is Quebec

    If your employee’s province of employment is Quebec, your employee cannot use Form CPT30 to stop making QPP contributions.

    Learn more: Employee Who Is at Least 65 But Under 73 at the End of the Year Who Elects to Stop Contributing to the QPP | Revenu Québec

  • Your employee is at least 65, but under 70, and gives you a completed Form CPT30 to restart CPP contributions

    Your employee can elect to restart contributing to CPP if they meet all of the following conditions:

    • Your employee filed a Form CPT30 with an employer in a prior year
    • Your employee is receiving or will receive pensionable earnings that require CPP contributions

    An employee cannot elect to start contributing again to the CPP in the same calendar year in which he or she made an election to stop contributing to the CPP.

    When is the election considered made

    The election is considered to have been made when your employee gives you a copy of the completed and signed Form CPT30.

    When to restart CPP contributions

    If your employee meets all of the above conditions and gives you a signed Form CPT30 Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election with parts A, B and D completed, you must restart deducting CPP contributions on the first pay in the month following the month your employee has given you the signed copy of this form.

    For example, if Form CPT30 is signed on March 20, 20X5, and they give it to you on the next day, the election is effective as of April 1, 20X5.

    If Form CPT30 is dated in the past and was either provided to a different employer or or your employee providing it to you was delayed due to unforeseen circumstances, the form can be accepted. However, the form must reflect the actual date it was completed, backdating is not permitted.

    Make sure to keep a copy of all completed CPT30 forms with your payroll records.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months after the month in which your employee gives you Form CPT30.

    What if you under deducted CPP contributions

    If you did not deduct CPP contributions from your employee after the effective date of the election, you are responsible for remitting the balance due for both your employee and your share of CPP contributions.

    What if your employee forgot to give you a copy of Form CPT30

    If your employee forgot to provide you a copy of the CPT30 revoking their election, your employee can choose to pay both your employee and your share of CPP contributions by completing Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions and filing it with their personal income tax and benefit return.

    What if the worker is self-employed

    A self-employed worker cannot use Form CPT30 to elect to restart contributing to the CPP for their self-employed earnings. Instead, they must complete the Schedule 8, Canada Pension Plan Contributions and Overpayment (for all except QC).

    Learn more: Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

    What if your employee's province of employment is Quebec

    If your employee’s province of employment is Quebec, your employee cannot use Form CPT30 to restart making QPP contributions.

    Learn more: Employee Who Is at Least 65 But Under 73 at the End of the Year Who Elects to Stop Contributing to the QPP | Revenu Québec

  • Your employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP in the year

    When to stop CPP contributions

    If your employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP, you must continue to deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month when your employee is considered to be disabled according to the letter that Service Canada sent to your employee.

    For example, if your employee is considered disabled according to the letter that Service Canada on March 20, 20X5, you must stop deducting CPP contributions from the last pay dated in the month of March 20X5.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month your employee was considered to be disabled.

  • Your employee is no longer considered to be disabled under the CPP in the year

    When to restart CPP contributions

    If your employee is no longer considered disabled under the CPP, you must restart deducting CPP contributions on the first pay dated in the month after your employee is no longer considered disabled.

    For example, if your employee is no longer considered disabled, you must restart deducting CPP contributions from the first pay dated in the month of April 20X5.

    How to prorate

    When prorating, use the number of months after the month in which your employee was no longer considered to be disabled.

What to do if you are self-employed

If your net self-employment income or pensionable employment income is more than $3,500, you have to contribute to the CPP.

You need to calculate the amount of CPP you have to contribute when you complete your income tax and benefit return (line 22200, line 31000, line 42100, and Schedule 8).

First Nations workers who are self-employed

If you are a self-employed First Nations worker exempt from tax, you can elect to pay CPP on your tax-exempt self-employment income by filing out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing your personal income tax return.

What is the CPP enhancement

From 2003 to 2018, employees were making a contribution of 4.95% on their pensionable earnings up to their annual maximum pensionable earnings (first ceiling), with employers making an equal contribution. These are the base contributions to the CPP.

From 2019 to 2023, the contribution rate for employees was increased gradually from 4.95% to 5.95%. These are the base contributions (4.95%) and the first additional contribution to the CPP (1%).

In 2024, an additional maximum pensionable earnings (second higher ceiling) was introduced. Employees and employers are required to make a second additional CPP contribution (CPP2) on these earnings, beginning at the first earnings ceiling and going up to the second earnings ceiling, at a rate of 4.0%.
