About the deduction of Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions

You may be looking for if you are a self-employed worker:

  • Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

What are CPP pensionable earnings

Pensionable earnings are the employee's remuneration from pensionable office or employment with certain exceptions.

Generally, you must deduct CPP contributions from:

  • Salary, wages or other remuneration
  • Commissions
  • Bonuses
  • Most taxable benefits
  • Honorariums
  • Certain tips and gratuities

To determine if you need to deduct CPP contributions, refer to:

  • Determine if a benefit is taxable
  • Determine the tax treatment of payments other than regular employment income

When to deduct CPP contributions

  • You have to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pensionable earnings if your employee meets one of the following conditions:

    • The employee is in pensionable employment during the year
    • The employee is between 18 and 69 years old, even if receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension
    • The employee is not considered disabled under the CPP or QPP

    Employer CPP contributions>

    You must also contribute an amount equal to the CPP contributions that you deduct from your employees' remuneration and remit the total of both amounts. Even if the required deductions were not made, you are deemed to have made them and the deductions not being made may result in a PIER.

    Learn more: Pensionable and insurable earnings review (PIER)

  • You do not have to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pensionable earnings if your employee meets one of the following conditions:

    • The employee is under 18 or 70 years old or older
    • The employee is considered disabled under CPP or QPP
    • The employee is between 65 and 69 and has submitted Form CPT30 to stop contributing
What is the CPP enhancement

From 2003 to 2018, employees were making a contribution of 4.95% on their pensionable earnings up to their annual maximum pensionable earnings (first ceiling), with employers making an equal contribution. These are the base contributions to the CPP.

From 2019 to 2023, the contribution rate for employees was increased gradually from 4.95% to 5.95%. These are the base contributions (4.95%) and the first additional contribution to the CPP (1%).

In 2024, an additional maximum pensionable earnings (second higher ceiling) was introduced. Employees and employers are required to make a second additional CPP contribution (CPP2) on these earnings, beginning at the first earnings ceiling and going up to the second earnings ceiling, at a rate of 4.0%.

How to calculate in special situations

Depending on the situation, you may need to start, stop or restart deduction CPP contributions. The following situations may also change how you calculate your employee CPP contributions:

Employee situations - Prorating may be required

Situation: Your employee turns 18 in the year

When to start CPP contributions

If your employee turns 18 in the year, you must start deducting CPP from the first pay dated in the month after your employee turns 18.

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months after the employee turns 18.

Situation: Your employee turns 70 in the year

When to stop CPP contributions

If your employee turns 70 in the year, you must continue to deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month when your employee turns 70.

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month the employee turns 70.

Situation: Your employee reaches the maximum contribution in the year

When to stop CPP contributions

If your your employee reaches the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you, you must stop deducting CPP contributions once in one of the following situation:

  • Your employee reached the annual maximum CPP contribution (and CPP2 contribution, if applicable)
  • Your employee reached a prorated maximum in any of the following situation
Situation: Your employee is at least 65, but under 70, and gives you a completed Form CPT30 to stop CPP contributions

Your employee can elect to stop contributing to CPP, if they meet all of the following conditions:

  • Your employee is at least 65 years of age, but under 70
  • Your employee receives CPP or QPP retirement benefits
  • Your employee does not receive CPP or QPP disability benefits (in this situation, they do not have to file an election because they are exempt from contributing)
  • Your employee works in a province or territory other than Quebec

When to stop CPP contributions

If your employee meets all of the above conditions and gives you a signed Form CPT30 Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election with parts A, B and C completed, you must stop deducting CPP contributions on the first pay in the month following the month your employee has given the signed copy of this form. For example, if Form CPT30 is signed on March 20, 2025, the election is effective as of April 1, 2025.

They must also provide you with:

  • A proof of age
  • A proof that you are receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month before the election becomes effective.

What if you over deducted CPP contributions

If you continue to deduct CPP contributions from your employee after the effective date of the election, you are responsible to reimburse your employee for the CPP contributions that were deducted in error.

Learn more: Make corrections before filing

What if the worker is only self-employed

If the income is only from self-employment earnings, the worker cannot use Form CPT30 to make this election.

Learn more: How to stop contributing to the CPP - Self-employed only

What if your employee's province of employment is Quebec

If your employee works in the Province of Quebec, your employee cannot use Form CPT30 to make this election.

Learn more: Employee Who Is at Least 65 But Under 73 at the End of the Year Who Elects to Stop Contributing to the QPP | Revenu Québec

Situation: Your employee is at least 65, but under 70, and gives you a completed Form CPT30 to restart CPP contributions

Your employee can elect to restart contributing to CPP, if they meet all of the following conditions:

  • Your employee is at least 65 years of age, but under 70
  • Your employee receives CPP or QPP retirement benefits
  • Your employee does not receive CPP or QPP disability benefits (in this situation, they do not have to file an election because they are exempt from contributing)
  • Your employee works in a province or territory other than Quebec

When to restart CPP contributions

If your employee meets all of the above conditions and gives you a signed Form CPT30 Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election with parts A, B and D completed, you must restart deducting CPP contributions on the first pay in the month following the month your employee has given the signed copy of this form. For example, if Form CPT30 is signed on March 20, 2025, the election is effective as of April 1, 2025.

They must also provide you with:

  • A proof of age
  • A proof that you are receiving a CPP or QPP retirement pension

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month before the election becomes effective.

What if you over under deducted CPP contributions

If you did not deduct CPP contributions from your employee after the effective date of the election, you are responsible for remitting the balance due for both the employer's and employee's shares.

What if the worker is only self-employed

If the income is only from self-employment earnings, the worker cannot use Form CPT30 to make this election.

Learn more: How to stop contributing to the CPP - Self-employed only

What if your employee's province of employment is Quebec

If your employee works in the Province of Quebec, your employee cannot use Form CPT30 to make this election.

Learn more: Employee Who Is at Least 65 But Under 73 at the End of the Year Who Elects to Stop Contributing to the QPP | Revenu Québec

Situation: Your employee is or was considered to be disabled under the CPP in the year

When to stop CPP contributions if your employee is considered disabled

If your employee is considered to be disabled under the CPP, you must continue to deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month when your employee is considered to be disabled according to the letter that Service Canada sent to the employee.

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month your employee was considered to be disabled.

When to restart CPP contributions if your employee is no longer disabled

If your employee is no longer considered disabled under the CPP, you must restart deducting CPP contributions on the first pay dated in the month after your employee is no longer considered disabled.

Situation: Your employee died in the year

When to stop CPP contributions

If your employee died in the year, you must deduct CPP contributions up to and including the last pay dated in the month in which the employee died. You must also deduct CPP contributions from any amounts and benefits that are earned by or owed to your employee on the date of death.

How to prorate

When prorating, use the number of months up to and including the month of death.

Situation: Your employee is a First Nations employee

The employment income of a First Nations employee which is exempt from income tax is not subject to CPP contributions. If their employment income is not tax exempt, it will be subject to CPP deductions.

How to apply for CPP coverage

Election by the employer

You have the option to extend CPP coverage to your First Nations employees who are exempt from tax by filing out Form CPT124, Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada Under the Canada Pension Plan Whose Income is Exempt Under the Income Tax Act.

However, you cannot revoke this election and you have to cover all First Nations employees.

When to start CPP contributions

If all conditions are met and you signed Form CPT124, you must start deducting CPP contributions on either the date you sign the application or on a later date that you specify. It cannot be retroactive to a date before the date you signed the application.

Election by the employee

If you do not choose to cover CPP for your First Nations employees who are exempt from tax, a First Nations employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings by filling out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

Learn more: First Nations workers and the Canada Pension Plan

Your employee is transferred from an establishment in Quebec to an establishment in another province or territory

You may have an establishment in Quebec and in another province or territory. If you transfer an employee from Quebec to another province or territory, you can take into account the QPP contributions you deducted from that employee throughout the year when calculating the maximum CPP contributions to deduct.

The following formula reconciles the amounts contributed to the CPP and QPP to make sure that enough contributions to the CPP are withheld and future benefits are not affected:

Step 1 - Determine the conversion factor

  • CPP contribution rate 
  • divide by QPP contribution rate
  • equals This is the conversion factor

Step 2 - Calculate the QPP contribution based on the conversion factor

  • QPP contributions withheld from your employees pay periods
  • multiply by Amount of the conversion factor
  • equals This amount is the CPP equivalent to include in the annual maximum CPP contributions for the year

She had $400 in QPP contributions withheld from her 2 pay periods as an employee with a province of employment of Quebec.

  • divide by 6.40% is the QPP contribution rate in March 2025
  • equals 0.9296875 is the conversion factor
Step 2 - Calculate the QPP contribution based on the conversion factor
  • $400 is the QPP contributions withheld from Melodie's 2 pay periods
  • multiply by 0.9296875 is the amount of the conversion factor

How to report on T4 slips

In addition to deducting CPP/QPP contributions, you will also have to prepare two T4 slips.

It is important that you calculate and report the proper deductions and insurable/pensionable earnings on both T4 slips.

Employee situations - Prorating not required

Your employee works for more than 1 employer

The CPP annual maximum pensionable earnings apply to each job the employee holds with different employers (different business numbers).

If an employee leaves one employer during the year to start work with another employer, the new employer also has to deduct CPP contributions without taking into account what the previous employer paid. This is the case even if the employee has contributed the maximum amount during the previous employment.

Any overpayments will be refunded to employees when they file their income tax and benefit returns. As an employer, you are not entitled to a refund for the employer's share of the contributions.

Example

Situation

  • Your employee notifies you that they have reached the maximum CPP contribution for the year. They tell you that they had $1,700 in CPP contributions deducted by a previous employer in the year and that when combined with the CPP you have deducted from their pay, they have reached the maximum.

Result

  • Do not stop deducting CPP contributions from their pay. You are required to deduct CPP contributions from your employee's pay until they reach the maximum contribution for the year in their employment with you. Do not factor in CPP contributions made through other employers.
Your employee' province of employment is Quebec

If you have employees whose province of employment is Quebec, regardless of your employee's province or territory of residence, you have to deduct Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) contributions instead of CPP contributions.

Learn more: Québec Pension Plan Contributions | Revenu Québec

Your employee works outside Canada and you have a business in Canada

If you are a Canadian employer and if you have employees working outside Canada, you must deduct CPP contributions if one of the following applies:

  • Your employee usually reports for work at your place of business in Canada
  • Your employee is a Canadian resident and is paid from your place of business in Canada

However, even if the employment does not meet the above conditions, international social security agreements may require the employment to be pensionable.

You may have the option to extend CPP coverage to your employees or your employee may have the option to file for CPP coverage if they meet the conditions to make the employment pensionable.

How to apply for CPP coverage

Election by the employer

You have the option to extend CPP coverage to your employee working outside Canada by filing out Form CPT8, Application and Undertaking to Cover Employment Outside Canada under the Canada Pension Plan if the employment meets the conditions listed on this form to be pensionable.

Election by the employee

If you do not choose to cover CPP for your employee working outside Canada, your employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings by filling out Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

Learn more: Employment outside Canada

Employer situations

You (as the employer) is restructuring

Where you (as the employer) is restructuring, the successor employer may, under certain situations, consider the amounts of CPP deducted, remitted or paid by the former employer for the employment of the employees for the year as if they had been deducted, remitted, or paid by the successor employer.

Learn more: Employer restructuring/Succession of employers - CPP/EI explained

You (as the employer) have a business outside Canada

If you do not have a place of business in Canada, you have the option to extend CPP coverage to your employees in Canada (resident or non-resident) by filling out Form CPT13, Application for an Employer Resident Outside Canada to Cover Employment in Canada Under the Canada Pension Plan.

What to do if your employee may be subject to a foreign social security plan

Canada has reciprocal social security agreements with certain other countries to ensure that only one plan covers an employee, either the CPP or a foreign social security plan. To find out if a particular country has an agreement with Canada and which CPT application form to use, see What is the purpose of international social security agreements?
