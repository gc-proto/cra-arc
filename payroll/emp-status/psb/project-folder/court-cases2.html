  • Astro Consulting Inc. v. The Queen

    • Result: The worker was carrying on a PSB
    • Court: Tax Court of Canada
    • Industry: Professional, scientific and technical services
    Summary
    • Act: Income Tax Act
    • Reference: 2022 TCC 51
    • File number(s): 2018-1052(IT)G

    The court concluded that Astro Consulting Inc. was carrying on a personal services business, and Mr. Glazier would be considered an employee but for the existence of the corporation.

    Factor-based assessment

    Contract - Intent of relationship
    The structure was corporate, but the court found it to be a PSB.
    Control - Duties (1 of 8)
    Lanmark and Engineering dictated the services.
    Control - Work schedule (2 of 8)
    Work was structured around Lanmark’s operations and needs.
    Control - Degree of autonomy (3 of 8)
    Mr. Glazier was part of Lanmark’s senior management and reported to executives.
    Control - Remuneration (4 of 8)
    Not mentioned in the court case.
    Control - Location of services (5 of 8)
    Services were integrated into Lanmark’s operations.
    Control - Right to exercise punitive action and/or terminate the relationship (6 of 8)
    Mr. Glazier followed the direction of Lanmark and Engineering.
    Control - How the work is done (7 of 8)
    Mr. Glazier performed all services personally.
    Control - Training (8 of 8)
    Not mentioned in the court case.
    Ownership of Tools
    No evidence of independent tools; services were integrated into Lanmark.
    Financial Risk
    Income was based on Lanmark’s profits, not business risk.

  • Mazraani v. Minister of National Revenue

    • Result: The worker was carrying on a PSB
    • Court: Tax Court of Canada
    • Industry: Finance and insurance
    Summary
    • Act: Employment Insurance Act
    • Reference: 2016 TCC 65
    • File number(s): 2013-3484(EI)

    The court concluded that Mr. Mazraani was in a relationship of subordination with Industrial Alliance and thus held insurable employment under the Employment Insurance Act.

    Factor-based assessment

    Contract - Intent of relationship
    Contract stated independent contractor, but court found it did not reflect reality.
    Control - Duties (1 of 8)
    IA assigned clients and dictated sales processes. Expected to meet sales targets and follow up on assigned leads.
    Control - Work schedule (2 of 8)
    While flexible, IA imposed expectations and monitored activity.
    Control - Degree of autonomy (3 of 8)
    Sales managers regularly supervised and reviewed performance.
    Control - Remuneration (4 of 8)
    No real business risk; income tied to IA’s structure and control.
    Control - Location of services (5 of 8)
    Primarily worked from IA’s branch office.
    Control - Right to exercise punitive action and/or terminate the relationship (6 of 8)
    Worker did not have the right to refuse work from the payer.
    Control - How the work is done (7 of 8)
    Extensive training, scripts, and sales techniques were mandated. Services had to be performed personally.
    Control - Training (8 of 8)
    Extensive mandatory training was provided.
    Ownership of tools or equipment
    IA provided office, software, training, and infrastructure. Dress code was enforced.
    Financial information
    Worker bore travel costs. No real business risk.

  • Gomez Consulting Ltd. v. The Queen

    • Result: The worker was carrying on a PSB
    • Court: Tax Court of Canada
    • Industry: Professional, scientific and technical services
    Summary
    • Act: Income Tax Act
    • Reference: 2013 TCC 135
    • File number(s): 2012-1000(IT)

    Overview

    The court concluded that Luis Gomez Almeida would reasonably be regarded as an employee of CRA and CMHC but for the existence of Gomez Consulting Ltd. Therefore, the corporation was carrying on a personal services business and was not entitled to the small business deduction or full expense deductions.

    Contract - Intent of relationship
    Intention was not considered relevant in PSB determinations.
    Control - Duties (1 of 8)
    Clients assigned specific projects and tasks.
    Control - Work schedule (2 of 8)
    Clients authorized hours and overtime; work was on-site.
    Control - Degree of autonomy (3 of 8)
    Clients evaluated progress and required timesheets.
    Control - Remuneration (4 of 8)
    Paid hourly with no operating expenses.
    Control - Location of services (5 of 8)
    Mr. Almeida had 24-hour access but could not sign others in.
    Control - Right to exercise punitive action and/or terminate the relationship (6 of 8)
    Not mentioned in the court case.
    Control - How the work is done (7 of 8)
    Mr. Almeida worked under direction of team leaders on client projects.
    Control - Training (8 of 8)
    Not mentioned in the court case.
    Ownership of tools or equipment
    Clients provided office space, phone, and network access.
    Financial information
    The only risk of loss was potential insolvency of the clients or AQR, which was unlikely.
    No significant operating expenses were incurred.
