Determine the province of employment (POE)

Content has been updated to include the new CRA administrative policy. This change is effective January 1, 2024 and is identified with the tag New CRA administrative policy.

When you pay employment income such as salaries, wages, or commissions, the employee's province or territory of employment (POE) must be determined so that the proper deductions are withheld. This depends on whether your employee “reports for work” at any of your establishments.

The POE is determined by:

  • the type of income
  • the residency status
  • how the employee reports for work

An establishment of the employer is any place or premises in Canada that is owned, leased or rented by an employer where employees report to work or from which employees are paid. For purposes of the POE, this does not need to be a permanent establishment.

Generally, the employer's home office is not considered an establishment of the employer.

POE for employment income: Employee is a resident of Canada

Under the new CRA administrative policy, the employee is considered to be reporting for work at an establishment of the employer if one of the following applies:

  • The employee reports for work physically (there is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place)

  • New CRA administrative policy

    Where a full-time teleworking agreement was entered into, the employee can be reasonably regarded as attached to the establishment of the employer

What is considered a full-time teleworking agreement

Generally, a full-time teleworking agreement between the employer and the employee means any of the following arrangements:

  • Employee required to telework full-time

    Employee is required to telework full-time in a location that is not an establishment of the employer by the agreement or employment contract

  • Employee not required to telework full-time

    Employee is not required to telework full-time in a location that is not an establishment of the employer but chooses to never physically report for work to the establishment of the employer

What is considered "attached to the establishment" of the employer

6 criteria in order of importance

In order of importance, you need to consider the following 6 criteria to determine if an employee is considered to be reasonably regarded as attached to the establishment of the employer. This must be done based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific case.

  1. If it was not for a full-time teleworking agreement, the establishment where the employee would physically come to work in person to carry out the functions related to their employment duties.
  2. If it was not for a full-time teleworking agreement, the establishment where the employee would physically attend meetings, through any type of communication.
  3. If it was not for a full-time teleworking agreement, the establishment where the employee would physically receive work-related material or equipment or associated instructions and assistance.
  4. If it was not for a full-time teleworking agreement, the establishment where the employee would physically come in person to receive instructions from their employer regarding their duties, through any type of communication.
  5. The establishment that is responsible for the employee, as indicated in the contractual agreements between the employer and the employee.
  6. The nature of the duties performed by the employee.

Determine the POE based on the situation

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically or is considered attached) to only 1 establishment of the employer in Canada New CRA administrative policy

The province of employment is where the employee reports for work.

There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically) to more than 1 establishment of the employer in Canada during a pay period

The province of employment is the province in which the establishment of the employer is located where the employee had either:

  • Spent the most time
  • Worked last, if the employee reported to each establishment for the same amount of time

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee works in Canada but does not report for work (never physically and not considered attached) at an establishment of the employer

The province of employment is the province or territory where the employer's establishment is located from which the employee's salary is paid. Generally, this is the location of the employer’s payroll department or payroll records.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee works in Canada but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • Payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada

Do not use:

  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
POE for employment income: Employee is not a resident of Canada for the full year

Where the employee is not a Canadian resident for the whole year, the province of employment will change depending on their specific residency status.

Determine the POE based on the situation

Situation: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter

The province of employment is where employment duties are performed if one of the following situations applies:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

Situation: Employee is a part-year resident

For the part of the year that the employee is either:

  • A resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a resident of Canada
  • Not a resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter
Situation: Employee is a deemed resident or sojourner

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory if one of the following situations applies:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • Payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada

Do not use:

  • TD1 provincial and territorial form

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

POE for non-employment income
for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP

The province of employment is the recipient's province or territory of residence if you pay amounts other than employment income, such as pension income, retiring allowance, or RRSP.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables
