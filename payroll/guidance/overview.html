Overview

For income tax, an individual may have a different employment status. An individual's employment status helps determine, the:

  • Individual's rights
  • Your responsibilities as an employer or a payer

On this page

  • Types of employment status
  • Different employment status for tax purposes
  • What if you made an error in the employment status

Types of employment status

The main types of employment status are:

  • Employee
  • Self-employed and contractor
  • Personal services business
  • Director
  • Office holder

Different employment status for tax purposes

An individual may be an employee in employment law but have a different status under the tax law. As an employer or a payer, you must work out each worker’s status in both employment law and tax law.

Tax law

The CRA determine the employment relationship on the basis of common law and the CPP, EI legislation and the ITA (and the Quebec Civil Code for Quebec employees).

Employment law

Canada Labour Code

The Employment and Social Development Canada are responsible to enforce the Canada Labour Code. The federal labout standards set out the employment conditions for hours of work, payment of wages, leaves, vacation, holidays, and more. These standards apply to employees working in federally regulated businesses.

Learn more: Federal labour standards.

Province or Territory Labour Code

If the employee is not working in a federally regulated industry, the province or territory is responsible to enforce the Labour Code.

Learn more on the employment standards for the province or territory of work:

What if you made an error in the employment status

Individuals and employers may have to pay unpaid tax and penalties, or lose entitlement to benefits, if their employment status is wrong.
