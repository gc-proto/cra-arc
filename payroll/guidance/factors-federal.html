Province or territory other than Quebec

Content has been developed to provide guidance only. The guidance does not replace the law and does not replace a formal request for a ruling. No changes were made to the existing federal legislative requirement.

Use this guidance if the contract was formed (unless the contract states differently):

  • In a province other than Quebec or in a territory
  • Outside Canada and the employment is performed in a province other than Quebec, in a territory, or in another country

Gather the facts

Answer a few questions based on the following factors to help you gather the facts.

The results from each question need to be viewed as a whole in order to help you decide. This also needs to be done on a case-by-case basis.

Factors

  1. Contract arrangement

    The CRA asks the worker and the payer what their intent was when they entered into the working arrangement. Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the employment status they choose must reflect their working relationship.

    Factor 1 - Questions and indicators

    At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

    • Employee-employer
    • Self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

    • Scale or rate of pay
    • Hours of work
    • Location of work
    • Period of employment
    Example

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed on the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $10 an hour.

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

    In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed individual.

    How long has the worker been working for the payer (or is expected to work)?

    • More than 1 month
    • Less than 1 month

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Long period or an indefinite period could be an indication of an employer-employee relationship. The working relationship between the payer and the worker does present a degree of continuity, loyalty, security, subordination, or integration, all of which are generally associated with an employer-employee relationship.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A short term contract could be an indication of self employed. The working relationship between the payer and the worker does not present a degree of continuity, loyalty, security, or integration, all of which are generally associated with an employer-employee relationship.

  2. Control

    Control is the ability, authority, or right of a payer to exercise control over a worker concerning the manner in which the work is done and what work will be done. Consider the degree of control held by the payer or the degree of independence held by the worker. The actual degree of control will vary with the type of work and the skills of the worker.

    Factor 2 - Questions and indicators

    Duties. Does the worker have defined duties?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer decides what jobs the worker will do. The payer controls the worker with respect to both the results of the work and the method used to do the work. Worker is required to keep a detailed record of the duties performed in each day of work, sometimes referred to as an activity report.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A self-employed individual usually works independently. The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities. The worker is not required to keep records of duties and is flexible to make arrangements how the tasks are performed.

    Duties. Can the payer assign or change the worker's duties at their discretion including nature and volume of workload?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The relationship is one of subordination. The payer will often direct, scrutinize, and effectively control many elements of how and when the work is carried out. The worker is not free to engage in other work at their discretion.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A self-employed individual usually works independently. The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities.

    A self-employed person is generally hired to work on a defined task. The only commitment from the worker is on the end result of the agreed task.

    Duties. Can the worker refuse work from the payer without ramifications?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker can accept or refuse work from the payer.

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer decides what jobs the worker will do. The worker doesn’t have the right to refuse work from the payer.

    Work schedule. Does the worker have set days and hours of work?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer has the final word as to when the worker performs his/her service. In other words, the worker requires permission from the payer to make any changes.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker has the final word as to when they perform his/her service. In other words, the worker doesn't require permission from the payer to make any changes.

    Work schedule. If the worker's days and hours of work are flexible, are they subject to the approval of the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    All decisions made by the worker for the days and hours of work are subject to the approval of the payer.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker can decide what days and hours wants to work and they are not subject to the approval of the payer.

    Work schedule. Is the worker required to record their days and hours of work (for example, by email, timesheets, timecards)?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker's day's and hours of work performed are recorded by the payer. The worker is required to keep a record of the starting and finishing time of each day of work.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker maintain books and records for the services provided, revenues generated and expenses, for example purchase receipts, business bank statements, and more.

    Work schedule. Can the worker leave at anytime during the day without the approval of the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker doesn't need the payer's permission to leave.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker must ask for the payer's permission to leave the work during the hours that the payer has established and agreed that must be worked. The payer has the final say as to whether the worker can take a leave from work.

    Work schedule. If the worker is unable to work due to illness or family emergencies, is the worker required to notify the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    If the worker was unavailable due to sickness, family emergencies, etc., the payer must be notified as per the employment standards requirements. The worker must request permission to be absent from work.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is not required to notify the payer if is not able to work due being sick or having personal emergencies.

    Work schedule. If the worker wishes to take time off, is the worker required to ask for the payer's approval?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker must ask for the payer's permission for any leave or absence. The payer had the final say as to whether the worker can take a leave from work.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker doesn't need the payer's permission to take time off.

    Degree of autonomy. Is the worker regularly monitored or supervised (day-to-day activities) by the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The relationship is one of subordination. The payer will often direct, scrutinize, and effectively control many elements of how and when the work is carried out.

    The payer controls the worker with respect to both the results of the work and the method used to do the work.

    Where the schedule is irregular, priority on the worker's time is an indication of control over the worker.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A self-employed individual usually works independently.

    The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities.

    The worker can accept or refuse work from the payer.

    Degree of autonomy. Did the worker work under the supervision of the payer or the staff of the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker works under the direct or indirect supervision of the payer or someone in the payer’s business.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker generally works alone and unsupervised. The worker has no contact with the payer until the job is complete.

    Degree of autonomy. Is the worker's work subject to review by the payer or the supervisor?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The services performed by the worker are subject to the payer’s review as frequently as the payer determines.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker works independently and is ultimately responsible for meeting the client’s expectations.

    Degree of autonomy. Does the payer have the right to direct the worker to redo the work?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker must adhere to the procedures set by the payer in all aspects of their performance.

    For example, the payer has an operational procedures manual and code of conduct expectations in place.

    The payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is responsible for satisfying the client’s needs, and rectifying any problems that may arise.

    Degree of autonomy. If the payer directs the worker to redo the work, would the worker be compensated for the time to redo the work?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Employees are generally paid by the hour rather than by unit of work, so they would be compensated for correcting any mistakes in work.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Self employed people can generally be paid by the job, rather than an hourly or weekly rate.

    Degree of autonomy. Is the worker required to attend meetings with the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker is required to attend meetings at the payers request.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is not required to attend meetings at the payer’s invitation.

    Remuneration. Does the payer determine the worker's rate of pay (negotiated between the worker and the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer dictates the compensation amount. The worker is paid hourly/weekly for a specific rate of pay.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker dictates their compensation amount. The worker negotiates with the payer the amount to be paid for the work performed.

    Remuneration. Is the worker paid regardless of volume of work performed?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker is paid based on the time of work performed (generally an hourly rate) and is entitled to overtime pay.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is typically paid a fixed amount for a specific task or job.

    Remuneration. Does the payer determine the frequency and method of the worker's remuneration?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer dictates when the worker is to be paid (weekly/biweekly/semi-monthly or monthly).

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker dictates when they are to be paid and what the method of payment should be.

    Location of services. Is the worker required to work at the payer's office?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer has the final word as to when and where the worker performs their service.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker decides where they perform their services.

    The worker can perform the service at another location of choosing without the payer’s approval.

    Right to exercise punitive action and or terminate the relationship. Can the payer exercise disciplinary actions against the worker?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer can take corrective action in response to worker's poor performance.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The payer can't issues a verbal or written warning to the worker for poor performance or evaluation.

    Right to exercise punitive action and or terminate the relationship. Did the payer have a right to dismiss the worker without cause and notice?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer can terminate the worker’s employment in accordance with employment laws.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The payer can’t dismiss the worker with cause and notice, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

    How the work is done. Does the payer have first call or priority of the worker's time?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer has first call on the worker’s time.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker sets the priority for their time.

    How the work is done. Does the payer set the worker's deadlines and priorities?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer sets the worker’s priorities and deadlines. The payer decides the priority of the worker’s workload and can change the priorities at their discretion.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker decides which workloads take priority and can set their own priorities on how to complete it.

    How the work is done. Does the worker have the option to choose whatever method they feel appropriate to finish the task?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer determines the method of their choosing that must be used by the worker in completing work. The worker can have input but the payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker determines how best to carry out the workload with minimal interference from the payer.

    Training. Did the payer provide the worker with training?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer provides and pays for any training to the worker.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is responsible to identify and make arrangements for their training that are needed to complete the task.

    Training. Was the worker required to pay for the training?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Training is paid by the payer and there is no requirement for the worker to take training on their own.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker generally pays for their own training.

    Training. Was the worker paid for the time they spent in training?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The arrangement between the worker and the payer is that the payer will pay or reimburse for training that the worker is required to take for completing the task.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Training is the workers responsibility and at their own cost. The payer is not required to pay or reimburse the worker’s training expenses.

    Training. If the training was off site, did the payer provide the worker with transportation, accommodation and or meal allowances?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer is responsible through the contract to pay for the worker’s training and associated costs.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The payer is responsible through the contract to pay for the worker’s training and associated costs.

  3. Ownership of the tools and equipment

    Consider if the worker owns and provides tools and equipment to accomplish the work. Contractual control of, and responsibility for, an asset in a rental or lease situation is also considered under this factor.

    What is considered a significant investment

    What is relevant is the significant investment in the tools and equipment along with the cost of replacement, repair, and insurance. A worker who has made a significant investment is likely to retain a right over the use of these assets, diminishing the payer's control over how the work is carried out. In addition, such a significant investment may place the worker at a risk of a financial loss.

    Tools and equipment can vary widely in terms of value and can include everything from wrenches and hammers, to specialized clothing, appliances, stethoscopes, musical instruments, computers, and vehicles such as trucks and tractors.

    Self-employed individuals often supply the tools and equipment required for a contract. As a result, the ownership of tools and equipment by a worker is more commonly associated with a business relationship.

    However, employees sometimes also have to provide their own tools. The courts have acknowledged that because a worker is required to provide tools of the trade, this does not in itself mean that the worker is a self-employed individual. For example, many skilled tradespeople such as auto mechanics have to supply their own tools, even if they are full-time employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

  5. Financial risk

    Consider the degree of financial risk taken by the worker. Consider if there are any fixed ongoing costs incurred by the worker or any expenses that are not reimbursed.

    Employees will usually not have financial risks

    Usually, employees will not have any financial risk as any expenses will be reimbursed, and they will not have fixed ongoing costs.

    Self-employed individuals, on the other hand, can have financial risk and incur losses because they usually pay fixed monthly costs even if work is not currently being done.

    Employees and self-employed individuals may be reimbursed for business or travel expenses. Therefore, focus on the expenses that are not reimbursed by the payer.

    Factor 5 - Questions and indicators

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

  7. Opportunity for profit (incorporated employee (such as PSBs) or a self-employed through their own corp)

    Consider whether the worker can realize a profit or incur a loss, as this indicates that a worker controls the business aspects of services rendered and that a business relationship likely exists. To have a chance of a profit and a risk of a loss, a worker has to have potential proceeds and expenses, and one could exceed the other.

    Consider this factor from the worker's perspective

    This factor has to be considered from the worker's perspective, not the payer's. It is for the most part an assessment of the degree to which the worker can control their proceeds and expenses.

    Employees normally do not have the chance of a profit and risk of a loss even though their remuneration can vary depending on the terms of their employment contracts. For example, employees working on a commission or piece-rate basis, or employees with a productivity bonus clause in their contract can increase their earnings based on their productivity. This increase in income is not normally viewed as a profit, as it is not the excess of proceeds over expenses.

    Employees may have expenses directly related to their employment, such as automobile expenses, and board and lodging costs. Normally, expenses would not place employees at risk of incurring a loss because it is unlikely that the expenses would be greater than their remuneration.

    Self-employed individuals normally have the chance of profit or risk of loss, because they have the ability to pursue and accept contracts as they see fit. They can negotiate the price (or unilaterally set their prices) for their services and have the right to offer those services to more than one payer. Self-employed individuals will normally incur expenses to carry out the terms and conditions of their contracts, and to manage those expenses to maximize net earnings. Self-employed individuals can increase their proceeds and/or decrease their expenses in an effort to increase profit.

    Employees generally do not share in profits or suffer losses incurred by the payer's business.

    The method of payment may help to decide if the worker has the opportunity to make a profit or incur a loss. In an employer-employee relationship, the worker is normally guaranteed a return for the work done and is usually paid on an hourly, daily, weekly, or similar basis.

    Similarly, some self-employed individuals are paid on an hourly basis. However, when a worker is paid a flat rate for the work done, it generally indicates a business relationship, especially if the worker incurs expenses in doing the work.

Analyze the facts

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on his own account.

You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer and the worker to help you decide if the evidence moves the worker to the employee or self-employed side of the continuum.

  • Worker is an employee
  • Worker is self-employed

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employement.

To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you respond to each question you should be able to decide what weight to assign each category based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, the worker is moved into the independent contractor side of the continuum.

Case-by-case basis

Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move a worker to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine the total working relationship. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

Consider special situations

A deemed employer is one of the following:

  • a third party that generally controls, directly supervises, or pays the remuneration to an insured person in its own name and not on behalf of the actual employer.
  • a person who has rights or privileges on premises or property under a licence, permit, or agreement and who agrees to allow another employer's workers to work on these premises or property.

Analyzing the relationship:

  • who supervises the worker?
  • where do the workers perform their tasks?
  • does the party paying the salary act in its own name?
  • what is the procedure for issuing pay?
  • where do the funds necessary for the payment of the salaries come from?
