Payroll and GST/HST examinations

The purpose of the payroll and GST/HST examinations is to maintain the integrity of the tax system with respect to all of the following:

  • The reporting of employment income and taxable benefits
  • The withholding and remitting of payroll related amounts
  • The proper characterization of workers (employee or self-employed)
  • The GST/HST collecting and filing requirements

They are completed through a combination of taxpayer education and responsible enforcement.

Why are examinations and reviews important

The examinations and reviews promotes employer awareness and understanding of tax laws and obligations as provided in the Income Tax Act, the Excise Tax Act, the Canada Pension Plan, the Employment Insurance Act, and their respective regulations, to increase and enhance voluntary compliance.

Under the Canada Pension Plan and the Employment Insurance Act, the CRA is responsible for determining:

  • Whether or not an individual's employment is pensionable under the Canada Pension Plan and/or insurable under the Employment Insurance Act
  • The amount of pensionable and/or insurable earnings
  • Whether or not Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions and employment insurance (EI) premiums are payable
  • How many hours an insured person has in insurable employment
  • How long an employment lasts, including the dates on which the employment began and ended
  • The amount of CPP contributions and/or EI premiums payable
  • Who is the employer
  • Whether or not employers are considered to be associated for the purposes of the Employment Insurance Act
  • The refund amount

The CRA is also responsible for ensuring that CPP contributions, EI premiums, and income tax are deducted, remitted, and reported as required by legislation.

How the process works

  1. Your account is selected for review

    Timeline: Day 1

    The CRA may contact you (or your authorized representative) to inform you that your payroll account  has been selected for review.

    This review helps confirm that income amounts, deductions, and credits are reported correctly and can be properly supported.

    About authorized reprensentative

    Even if a professional tax preparer completed your payrol information returns (for example, T4 slips and T4 summary) on your behalf, you are responsible for making sure your business meets all their payroll tax obligations. Generally, tax preparers will use the information you give them to prepare your remittance amounts and information returns, but they may not be aware of all your tax obligations. This is why the CRA may contact you directly for the review.

    If the CRA sends a letter to the authorized representative asking for information about one of their clients, the authorized representative must respond by the date mentioned in the letter.

    You want to authorize a representative

    You can use different methods to authorize a representative.

    Learn more: How to give authorization.

    Methods the CRA may contact you

    By phone

    Confirm the call is from the CRA officer

    If the CRA calls you (or your authorized representative), the CRA officer assigned to your case will identify themselves when they contact you by phone. They will provide you with the following:

    • Their name and their phone number
    • Your payroll or GST/HST account  number
    • The purpose of the review

    If you doubt the caller's identity

    Make sure the caller is a CRA employee before providing any information over the phone.

    Don't verify the phone number during the call or rely on the number displayed on your caller ID. End the call and verify the callback number to confirm if a phone number is from the CRA.

    Learn more: Confirm a phone number is from the CRA

    Send you a letter

    If the CRA sends you (or your authorized representative) a letter, the following information will be included:

    • The name of CRA officer assigned to the case and their phone number
    • Your payroll or GST/HST account  number
    • The purpose of the review
    • The timeline for the next step of the review

    Online mail

    You (or your authorized representative) may receive an email notification from the CRA if you signed up to receive email notifications.

    Type of documents you may receive for this review

    Examples of mail you can receive online include:

    • Letter: Advise appointment has been arranged or request phone contact
    • Letter: Confirm appointment for an examination
    • Letter: Advise of failure to deduct
    • Letter: Provides documentation from the results of the examination
    • Letter: Request books and records be submitted through online services for desk examination
    • Letter: Educational reviews (service offering, initial contact or results)
    • Letter: Pensionable and insurable earnings review (PIER)
    • Notice: Notice of assessment (NOA)
    • Notice: Notice of reassessment (NOR)
    • Form: PD7A, Statement of account for current source deductions
    • Form: PD83-1, Payroll examination statement of account
    • Form: GST501, GST/HST examination statement of account
    • Other type of documents, statements or CRA letters

    • CRA sign-in - My Business Account

    • CRA sign-in - Represent a Client

    By email

    For security reasons, the CRA officer cannot contact you directly by email.

    Learn more: Recognize a scam - Why the CRA may contact you.

    Collaborating with the CRA officer assigned to your case

    Once you are satisfied regarding the identity of the CRA officer, the CRA recommends that you answer the questions from the officer to the best of your ability. Collaborating with the officer will lead to a smooth and efficient review.

    Your rights during the review

    The CRA operates on the fundamental belief that you are more likely to comply with the law if you have the information and other services that you need to meet your obligations.

    The CRA wants to make sure you receive all of your entitlements and that you understand and can exercise your rights.

    Learn more: Taxpayer Bill of Rights.

    • Don't continue to next step if:

      • You answered all of the CRA officer's questions
      • The officer confirmed the review is completed and the case is closed
      • The officer confirmed you meet your payroll tax obligations

    • Continue to Step 4: Review completed if all of the following apply:

      • You answered all of the CRA officer's questions
      • The officer confirmed the review is completed and the case is closed
      • The officer confirmed they will send you a letter explaining the changes, including other documents

    • Continue to Step 2: Preparing for the review if one of the following apply:

      • You only received a letter
      • You did not answer all of the CRA officer's questions
      • The officer confirmed an examination of your books and records is required

  2. Preparing for the review

    Target timeline: Within 1 month

    The CRA officer will contact you (or your authorized representative) to begin the review process.

    Make sure that your documents are accurate, complete, and organized to facilitate a smooth review process.

    Methods the CRA officer may contact you

    The CRA may call you (or your authorized representative) or send you a letter. They will provide you with the following:

    • Their name and their phone number
    • Your payroll or GST/HST account  number
    • The specific books and records  you must provide
    • The method the examination will be conducted: In-person or desk review
      In-person review
      • The date, time and address where the CRA officer will visit you (generally at your place of business)
      Desk review
      • The due date to provide your books and records since the CRA officer will not visit you

    Books and records you must provide

    Once you receive the request from the CRA officer for specific books and records, you are legally required to provide your documents for this review.

    Types of books and records

    The following are the types of books and records the CRA may ask for during the review:

    General: Payroll or GST/HST examination

    Accounts receivable and payable (ledgers and journals)

    These records, including bank deposit books, provide:

    • Information regarding your ability to pay
    • A list of assets to assist collections with third party demands
    Bank statements, cancelled cheques, cheque register

    Provides information on your ability to pay (for example: cash flow, balance, amounts of payments and withdrawals). This also provides:

    • Verification for information in the salary and wage journal (for example: dates, amounts, endorsements)
    • Proof of payment where remittances may be missing from the CRA systems
    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, casual workers)
    Cash disbursements and cash receipts journal

    These records (including the petty cash journal) may provide:

    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in the payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, amount paid to casual workers)
    • Information that may lead to a referral to another CRA team (for example: possible referral to Rulings)
    Current financial statements

    The current balance sheet and income statement will provide:

    • An overall view of the profitability, extent of operations, net worth, assets and liabilities
    • Salaries, wages, management salaries and sub-contract costs expensed
    • Owner's equity and shareholders loans
    General ledger and general journal

    This ledger/journal provides information such as:

    • A summary of all of your financial record-keeping
    • Information pertaining to shareholder loan accounts, vehicles, equipment, and inventory
    Corporate documents

    These records provide:

    • Correct name of corporation
    • Type of charter held, for example, federal or provincial
    • The minutes of shareholders meetings
    • The name and addresses of the shareholders and directors
    • The number of shares held by each shareholder (this helps to establish the percentage of the voting shares controlled by each shareholder)

    Specific: Payroll examination

    Salary or wage journal

    This journal (manual or electronic) provides:

    • Gross and net amounts paid to employees, including overtime pay, incentive bonuses, and more
    • Amounts deducted and to be paid for tax CPP and EI
    • Pay period dates and pay dates
    • Amounts which should have been remitted to the Receiver General
    • Dates when remittances are due
    • Information about employees or other workers information relevant to withholding requirements

    When reviewing the salary or wage journal, the CRA reviews the:

    • Province in which the employee reports for work or if they have an arrangement to work remotely
    • Calculation of gross salary including overtime pay, and incentive bonuses
    T4 slips and summaries

    The CRA compares the T4 information return they received with the employer’s records of the T4 information return for any differences. The CRA will request the T4 return if it had not been filed.

    T4A slips and summaries

    The CRA compares the T4A information return they received with the payer’s records of the T4A information return for any differences. The CRA will request the T4A return if it had not been filed.

    Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return

    The CRA reviews the TD1 to verify the calculation of the employee’s income tax deductions and as evidence that:

    • The individuals are actually employees of the business
    • Each employee has a social insurance number

    All provinces and territories have their own personal tax credits return that must also be completed if they are claiming more than the basic personal amounti to withhold correct amount of provincial tax.

    Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian’s Employment Income

    The CRA reviews the TD1-IN to confirm whether the tax exemption under s. 87 of the Indian Act applies to your First Nations employee’s employment income.

    Form TD1X, Statement of Commission Income and Expenses for Payroll Tax Deductions

    Employees who are paid in whole, or in part, by commission can choose to complete this form.

    The CRA reviews the TD1X to make sure that:

    • Rate of tax to be deducted from commission income
    • Employee's prior year income and expenses or the estimated amounts for the current year
    • Employee's estimated annual taxable income
    • Employee's personal information, for example, address and social insurance number
    Form TD3F, Fisher's Election to have Tax Deducted at Source

    The CRA reviews the TD3F to confirm the fisher had elected to have income tax deductions and to make sure that fisherman's personal information is accurate and complete (for example: address and social insurance number).

    Form CPT30, Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election

    The CRA reviews the CPT30 to confirm whether the employee had made a valid election to stop contributing to the Canada Pension Plan or had revoked a valid election as part of determining whether your withholding of CPP deductions was correct.

    Documents relating to taxable benefits

    The CRA reviews what documents you have regarding the taxable benefits you had provided to make sure you are correctly calculating, reporting, and remitting taxes related to taxable benefits for all employees, shareholders, directors, and other persons.

    This review will also confirm all of the following:

    • You correctly classified the benefits as either taxable or non-taxable
    • You withheld the correct amounts of income tax, CPP contributions, and EI premiums deductions and you remitted these amounts to the CRA on any taxable benefits
    • The taxable benefits are correctly included in your employees T4 slips and included in their employment income
    • You correctly claimed input tax credits for the GST/HST paid on taxable benefits you provided to your employees
    Documents to request a CPP/EI ruling

    The CRA officer may need the following documentation to request a CPP/EI ruling to clarify whether you need to deduct and remit CPP contributions and EI premiums for a particular worker or type of employment:

    • Employment contract or agreement: Copy of any contracts or agreements between you and the worker, detailing the terms of employment or the services being provided
    • Worker information: Details about a specific worker, including their job title, duties, and work schedule, to determine the nature of the employment relationship
    • Payment records: Payroll records or other documents that show how the worker is paid, including any deductions for CPP and EI
    • Employment practices: Any documentation on how the worker operates within the business (for example: level of control, tools provided, degree of integration into the business) to help determine the worker's classification
    • Supporting documents: Any additional documentation that clarifies the nature of the work relationship (for example: T4 slips)

    Specific: GST/HST examination

    GST/HST returns for outstanding periods

    The CRA reviews outstanding periods to make sure you are correctly reporting GST/HST, collecting GST/HST and filing GST/HST returns.

    Sales invoices, purchase invoices and summaries for outstanding periods

    These records confirm:

    • If you should have charged the GST/HST on taxable sales or if you should have reported these amounts in your outstanding returns
    • You are not over-claiming ITCs or underreporting the GST/HST collected

    In addition to the above books and records, the CRA may ask for input from your accountant, bookkeeper or employees, about information that relates to the books and records and what was reported in your information returns.

    How long should you keep your payroll and GST/HST records

    Your payroll and GST/HST records must be kept organized. Your records must be made available to the CRA when they ask to see them.

    Learn more: Where to keep your records, for how long and how to request the permission to destroy them early

    To help you prepare, use the: Checklist - Preparing for the review

  3. Examination of your books and records

    Target timeline: Within 1 to 3 months

    The time it takes to complete the examination of your books and records  varies. There may be delays if you cannot provide books and records as requested. There may also be delays caused by the CRA. For example, the officer may need to consult other CRA tax specialists.

    How your T4 and T4A slips and summaries help the CRA officer for this review

    • T4 slips and summaries

      The CRA reviews the T4 information return to:

      • Compare a random sample of the gross salary or wages, taxable allowances and benefits and registered pension plan deductions reported to the amounts shown in the salary and wage journal
      • Reconcile the total wages for the year as recorded in the salary and wage journal with the total wages reported on the summary form
      • Reconcile the total deductions for the year as recorded in the salary and wage journal to the amount of the T4 slip
      • Make sure that bonus payments and amounts paid after the end of the calendar year are reported by the recipient as income in the year they were received
      • Make sure that all taxable benefits and other income are reported

    • T4A slips and summaries

      The CRA reviews the T4A information return to make sure that:

      • Tax deductions from pension and other income which should have been remitted to the Receiver General
      • Gross pension and other income for all of the payees
      • Pension and other income and tax deductions for each payee

    Methods of the examination

    In-person review

    Generally, the CRA officer will examine your books and records at your place of business. The CRA officer may also examine them at an alternate location (for example: your authorized representative’s office or the officer will borrow your documents to complete the review at a CRA office).

    When to provide your documents

    If your books and records are available electronically, the CRA officer may ask you to submit the documents online before or after meeting with you. This usually speeds up the review process and leads to less time spent at your place of business.

    If electronic records are not available, or physical records are required, the CRA officer will either:

    • Review the documents at your place of business or representative’s office.
    • Borrow the documents that are required for further review.
      What if the CRA borrows your books and records

      To borrow your books and records, the CRA officer will:

      • Complete a T2213 receipt identifying the borrowed books, records and documents
      • Provide you with a copy of the receipt

      When the documents are returned, the CRA officer will request that you sign on the receipt acknowledging you received the books and records.

    How to submit your documents online

    You (or your authorized representative) can send the documents to CRA using our secure online service portals.

    • CRA sign-in - My Business Account

    • CRA sign-in - Represent a Client

    Learn more: Submit documents online

    We don't accept documents by email

    For security reasons, you cannot send documents directly by email.

    Learn more: Recognize a scam - Why the CRA may contact you.

    Confirm the visit is from the CRA authorized officer

    The authorized officer will carry a CRA authorization card (RC121A) to prove that they are a representative of the CRA. The CRA authorization card includes the employee's name, photo and badge number. It is the primary method of employee identification.

    If you doubt the legitimacy of a visit from a CRA officer

    Make sure the it is a CRA employee before providing any information.

    If you doubt the legitimacy of a visit from a CRA officer, contact the CRA and ask an agent to confirm the officer's identity.

    Learn more: Contact the CRA - Get help with your business taxes

    Desk review

    The CRA officer will examine your books and records electronically at their CRA office. The CRA officer will not visit you at your place of business during this review.

    When to provide your documents

    Generally, you have 15 days from the date of the letter you received to submit your documents online.

    After 15 days, if the requested books and records are not received, we may examine your books and records at your place of business (in-person review).

    To determine the due date, use the: Date calculator

    What if you need more time to respond or cannot get the documents

    If you are running late or you cannot get the documents requested by the due date, call the CRA officer assigned to your case at the number provided in your letter to let them know you need more time.

    If you no longer have specific books and records, you can discuss with the CRA officer assigned to your case to find alternate methods to confirm the amounts reported on your slips and summaries.


    Confirm the call is from the CRA officer

    If the CRA calls you (or your authorized representative) to discuss your case, the CRA officer assigned to your case will identify themselves when they contact you by phone. They will provide you with the following:

    • Their name and their phone number
    • Your payroll or GST/HST account  number
    • The purpose of the call

  4. Review completed

    Target timeline: Within 1 month

    After the review is completed, you (or your authorized representative) may receive the following:

    • Letter to inform you if you are fully compliant or if any discrepancies were found that need to be corrected (if the CRA officer finds that no changes need to be made to the information returns, the officer will close the case)
    • PD83-1, Payroll examination Statement of Account showing the result of the examination and the balance owing (if applicable)
    • GST501, GST/HST examination statement of account showing the result of the examination and the balance owing (if applicable)
    • NOR showing any changes to your information return (for example, if you under-remitted or significant errors are found), the balance owing, including any penalties or interest applied to overdue payments

    Paying a balance owing

    You have 30 days from the date of the NOR to pay your balance owing.

    To determine the due date, use the: Date calculator

    Learn more on how to make a payment to the CRA: Pay at the counter (teller) at a bank or credit union

    Option to respond to the letter if you disagree

    You can respond to the letter by providing further documentation or request a second review. If issues remain unresolved, you can contact the officer’s team leader to discuss further.

  5. Option to appeal if you disagree

    Timeline: Within 90 days of the NOA or NOR

    If are not satisfied with the answer after talking to the CRA, you have the right to make a formal objection.

    Objections (also appeals specifically for CPP or EI) lead to a formal and impartial review of an assessment, determination, or decision. When any type of objection is filed, the CRA reviews all the information before making its decision.

    When to file an objection

    You have 90 days from the date of the NOA or NOR to file the objection.

    To determine the due date, use the: Date calculator

    • If you want to dispute your income tax NOA or NOD : File an income tax objection
    • If you want to dispute your GST/HST assessment: File a GST/HST objection

Payroll review examination process

What is a payroll examination and GST/HST account review process?

A CRA-led review of an organization’s payroll and GST/HST books and records to ensure accurate remittances and compliance with Canadian payroll laws.

The steps in the payroll review process

  1. Notice of review: CRA sends a notice informing the employer about the payroll review.
  2. Document request: Employer provides payroll records, T4 summaries, and other documents.
  3. In-person vs desk review: CRA reviews the records in-person or desk review.
  4. Compliance check: CRA examines records for compliance with payroll laws.

Common topics reviewed

Employee income: Regular wages, bonuses
Deductions: CPP, EI, income tax
Employee benefits: Taxable benefits (for example: insurance, vehicle allowances)
Remittances: Timely payments to the CRA
GST/HST collected and input tax credits (ITC) claimed on taxable benefits

Review outcome

No Changes required: Payroll records are compliant.
Adjustments Needed: CRA identifies discrepancies and recommends adjustments.
Penalties/interest: CRA may apply penalties or interest for non-compliance.

Responding to the CRA

The employer can submit additional documents or explanations to clarify any discrepancies found in the review.

Final assessment

CRA provides a final report of the review outcome, detailing any changes or payments required.

Best practices for employers

Keep accurate records: Maintain thorough payroll documentation.
Remit on time: Ensure timely and accurate remittances to avoid penalties.
Stay compliant: Regularly review payroll compliance rules and CRA updates.
