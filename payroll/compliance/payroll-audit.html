Employer compliance audits

Employer compliance audits promote employer awareness and understanding of tax laws and obligations, as provided under the Income Tax Act, the Canada Pension Plan, the Employment Insurance Act, and their respective regulations, to increase and enhance voluntary compliance.

The focus of an employer compliance audit is different from a tax audit because it is related to your reporting of employment income and taxable benefits.

Why are audits important

The purpose of employer compliance audits is to maintain the integrity of the tax system by:

  • Making sure you reported employment income correctly
  • Making sure you reported taxable benefits provided to your employees and shareholders correctly.
  • Verifying the employment status of the workers you hired (employee or self-employed)
  • Analyzing executive compensation and verifying that certain transactions between shareholders and corporations are accurately reported
  • Making sure you withheld and remitted payroll related amounts (your employee's CPP contributions, EI premiums and income tax deductions, and your share of the CPP contributions and EI premiums)

Compliance with these obligations is achieved through a combination of taxpayer education and responsible enforcement. Generally, the audit period covers the previous two calendar years.

  1. Your business is being reviewed by an Employer Compliance Auditor

    Timeline: Day 1

    The CRA will contact you (or your authorized representative) to inform you that your business records are being reviewed by an Employer Compliance Auditor.

    Using an authorized representative

    Even if a professional tax preparer completed your payroll returns (for example, T4 slips and T4 summary) on your behalf, you are responsible for making sure your business meets all its payroll tax obligations. Generally, tax preparers will use the information you give them to prepare your remittance amounts and information returns, but they may not be aware of all your tax obligations. This is why the CRA may contact you directly for the review.

    If the CRA sends a letter to the authorized representative asking for information about one of their clients, the authorized representative must respond by the date mentioned in the letter.

    If you choose to authorize a representative

    You can use different methods to authorize a representative.

    Learn more: How to give authorization.

    Why the CRA may audit your business

    The CRA may audit your business based on a risk assessment, including but not limited to the following factors:

    • The likelihood or frequency of errors in tax returns that you file
    • Indications of non-compliance with your tax obligations
    • Information considered from other audits or reviews of your business
    • Information it has on file for your business compared with similar files

    Methods by which the CRA may contact you

    By phone

    Confirm the call is from the CRA auditor

    If the CRA calls you (or your authorized representative), the CRA auditor assigned to your case will identify themselves when they contact you by phone. Before providing any information over the phone:

    1. Tell the caller you would like to first verify their identity
    2. Request and take note of their name, phone number and office location

    If you doubt the legitimacy of a call from a CRA auditor, end the call and contact the CRA from the CRA's list of phone numbers and ask an agent to confirm the auditor’s identity.

    Details of the audit

    The auditor will ask you specific questions related to your account to confirm you are authorized on the account. Once the auditor has confirmed you are the person authorized for the account, they will provide you with the following:

    • The program account  numbers being reviewed
    • The reference number to securely send your documents using our online services
    • The purpose of the audit
    • The period under audit
    • The timeline for the next step of the audit

    By sending a letter

    Details of the audit

    If the CRA sends you (or your authorized representative) a letter, the following information will be included:

    • The name of the CRA auditor assigned to the case and their phone number
    • The program account  numbers being reviewed
    • The reference number to securely send your documents using our online services
    • The purpose of the audit
    • The period under audit
    • The timeline for the next step of the audit

    Online mail

    If you signed up to receive email notifications from the CRA, you (or your authorized representative) may receive an email to let you know that you have mail to view in My Business Account.

    Type of documents you may receive for this audit

    Examples of mail you can receive online include:

    • Letter: Business has been selected for an audit and contact will be made by phone to arrange a meeting
    • Letter: Confirm meeting for an audit
    • Letter: Confirm meeting date or revised meeting date for an audit
    • Letter: Proposed corrections from the audit
    • Letter: Audit is complete and corrections are required (or are not required)
    • Other type of documents, statements or CRA letters

         CRA sign-in     

    By email

    For security reasons, the CRA auditor cannot contact you directly by email.

    Learn more: Recognize a scam - Why the CRA may contact you

    Collaborating with the CRA auditor assigned to your case

    Once you are satisfied regarding the identity of the CRA auditor, the CRA recommends that you answer the questions from the auditor to the best of your ability. Collaborating with the auditor will lead to a smooth and efficient review process.

    Your rights during the process

    The CRA operates on the fundamental belief that you are more likely to comply with the law if you have the information and other services that you need to meet your obligations.

    The CRA wants to make sure you understand and can exercise your rights.

    Learn more: Taxpayer Bill of Rights.

  2. Preparing for the audit

    Timeline: Generally, you have up to 30 days to prepare

    The CRA auditor will contact you (or your authorized representative) to begin the audit process.

    The time provided to prepare will give you the chance to make sure that your documents are accurate, complete, and organized to facilitate a smooth audit process.

    Methods by which the CRA auditor may contact you

    The CRA auditor may call you (or your authorized representative) or send you a letter to provide the following information:

    • The method of the meeting that will be conducted for the audit is either:
      In person

      The CRA auditor will provide the date, time and address when the CRA auditor will visit you.

      Generally, your documents will be requested and audited at your place of business.

      Online or
      Video conference or by Phone

      The CRA auditor will provide the due date to submit your books and records online if there is no in person meeting. The CRA auditor will not visit you but will contact you to discuss the documents received.

      Your electronic books and records will be audited at their CRA office.

    • The specific books and records  you must provide

    Books and records you must provide

    Once you receive the request from the CRA auditor for specific books and records, you are legally required to provide your documents for this audit. The scope of the audit is based on issues identified by the CRA.

    Types of books and records

    The CRA may ask for various types of books and records, including but not limited to the following:

    General: Payroll examination

    Accounts receivable and payable (ledgers and journals)

    These records, including bank deposit books, provide information regarding the cash flow and assets of your business.

    Bank statements, cancelled cheques, cheque register

    These records provide information regarding the cash flow and balances of your business. The records also provide:

    • Verification for information in the salary and/or wage journal (for example: dates, amounts, endorsements)
    • Proof of payment where remittances may be missing from the CRA systems
    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, casual workers)
    Cash disbursements and cash receipts journal

    These records (including the petty cash journal) may provide:

    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in the payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, amount paid to casual workers)
    • Information that may lead to a referral to another CRA area (for example: possible referral to CPP/EI Rulings Division)
    Current financial statements

    The current balance sheet and income statement will provide:

    • An overall view of the profitability, extent of operations, net worth, assets and liabilities
    • Salaries, wages, management salaries and sub-contract costs expensed
    • Owner's equity and shareholder loans
    General ledger and general journal

    This ledger/journal provides information such as:

    • A summary of all of your financial record-keeping
    • Information pertaining to shareholder loan accounts, vehicles, equipment, and inventory
    • Information and payments to subcontractors
    Corporate documents

    These records provide:

    • The correct name of the corporation
    • Type of charter held, for example, federal or provincial
    • The minutes of shareholders meetings
    • The name and addresses of the shareholders and directors
    • The number of shares held by each shareholder (this helps to establish the percentage of the voting shares controlled by each shareholder)

    Specific: Payroll examination

    Salary and/or wage journal

    This journal (manual or electronic) provides:

    • Gross and net amounts paid to employees, including overtime pay, incentive bonuses, taxable benefits, commissions and more
    • Amounts deducted and to be remitted for income tax, CPP and EI
    • Pay period dates and pay dates, including summaries for weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly and monthly pay frequencies
    • Summary of amounts which should have been remitted to the Receiver General, including the total employee and employer portions of CPP and EI
    • Dates when remittances are due
    • Information about employees or other workers information relevant to withholding requirements

    When reviewing the salary and/or wage journal, the CRA examines the:

    • Province in which the employee reports for work or if they have an arrangement to work remotely
    • Calculation of gross salary including overtime pay, and incentive bonuses
    T4 slips and summaries

    The CRA compares the T4 information return they received with the employer’s records with the T4 information return on file for any differences.

    T4A slips and summaries

    The CRA compares the T4A information return they received with the payer’s records with the T4A information return on file for any differences.

    Documents relating to taxable benefits

    The CRA examines all documents to make sure you are correctly calculating, reporting, and remitting taxes related to taxable benefits for all employees, shareholders, directors, and other persons.

    This audit will also confirm all of the following:

    • You correctly classified the benefits as either taxable or non-taxable
    • You withheld the correct amounts of income tax, CPP contributions, and EI premiums deductions and you remitted these amounts to the CRA on any taxable benefits
    • You correctly included taxable benefits on your employees’ T4 slips and in their employment income
    Documents relating to other workers paid

    The CRA officer may need the following documentation to make sure workers are properly categorized (employee vs self-employed) and may request a CPP/EI ruling to confirm their employment status:

    • Employment contract or agreement: Copy of any contracts or agreements between you and the worker, detailing the terms of employment or the services being provided
    • Worker information: Details about a specific worker (social insurance number or business number for anyone who is in your employment or who is subcontracting for your business) and their contact information, including their job title, duties, and work schedule, to determine the nature of the employment relationship
    • Payment records: Payroll records or other documents that show how the worker is paid, including any deductions for CPP and EI
    • Employment practices: Any documentation on how the worker operates within the business (for example: level of control, tools provided, degree of integration into the business) to help determine the worker's classification
    • Supporting documents: Any additional documentation that clarifies the nature of the work relationship (for example: T4 slips)
    Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Return

    The CRA examines the TD1 to verify the calculation of the employee’s income tax deductions and as evidence that:

    • The individual is actually an employee of the business
    • Each employee has a social insurance number

    All provinces and territories have their own personal tax credits return that must also be completed if they are claiming more than the basic personal amount to make sure the correct amount of provincial tax is withheld.

    Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian’s Employment Income

    The CRA examines the TD1-IN to confirm whether the tax exemption under s. 87 of the Indian Act applies to your First Nations employee’s employment income.

    Form TD1X, Statement of Commission Income and Expenses for Payroll Tax Deductions

    Employees who are paid in whole, or in part, by commission can choose to complete this form.

    The CRA examines the TD1X to verify the:

    • Rate of tax to be deducted from commission income
    • Employee's prior year income and expenses or the estimated amounts for the current year
    • Employee's estimated annual taxable income
    • Employee's personal information, for example, address and social insurance number
    Form TD3F, Fisher's Election to have Tax Deducted at Source

    The CRA examines the TD3F to confirm the fisher had elected to have income tax deducted and to make sure that the fisher's personal information is accurate and complete (for example: address and social insurance number).

    Form CPT30, Election to Stop Contributing to the Canada Pension Plan, or Revocation of a Prior Election

    The CRA examines the CPT30 to confirm whether the employee over 65 has made a valid election to stop contributing to the Canada Pension Plan or has revoked a valid election as part of determining whether your withholding of CPP deductions was correct.

    In addition to the above books and records, the CRA may ask for input from your accountant, bookkeeper or employees, about information that relates to the books and records and what was reported in your information returns.

    How long should you keep your payroll and financial records

    Your payroll and financial records must be kept organized. Your records must be made available to the CRA when they ask to see them.

    Learn more: Where to keep your records, for how long and how to request the permission to destroy them early

  3. Audit of your books and records

    Timeline: Generally, your audit takes place within 30 days

    The time it takes to complete the audit of your books and records  varies. There may be delays because of the following:

    • Availability and accuracy of your documents
    • Complexity of your business
    • CRA auditor needs to consult with other areas of the CRA

    The CRA auditor will keep you informed on the status of the audit.

    How your T4/T4A slips and summaries help the CRA auditor for this audit

    • T4 slips and summaries

      The CRA examines the T4 information return to:

      • Compare the gross salary and/or wages, taxable allowances and benefits and registered pension plan deductions reported to the amounts shown in the salary and/or wage journal
      • Reconcile the total wages for the year as recorded in the salary and/or wage journal with the total wages reported on the summary form
      • Reconcile the total deductions for the year as recorded in the salary and/or wage journal to the amount on the T4 slips
      • Make sure that bonus payments and amounts paid after the end of the calendar year are reported by the recipient as income in the year they were received
      • Make sure that all taxable benefits and other income are reported

    • T4A slips and summaries

      The CRA examines the T4A information return to:

      • Make sure that all tax deductions from other income have been reported and remitted to the Receiver General
      • Make sure that all self-employed commissions paid to a self-employed worker have been properly reported
      • Reconcile the total deductions for the year to the amount on the T4A slips

    Methods of the audit

    In person

    Generally, the CRA auditor will examine your books and records at your place of business. The CRA auditor may also examine them at an alternate location (for example: your authorized representative’s office or the auditor will borrow your documents to complete the audit at a CRA office).

    Confirm the visit is from the CRA authorized auditor

    If the CRA auditor visits you (or your authorized representative), the CRA auditor will identify themselves. Before providing any information:

    1. Tell the person you would like to first verify their identity
    2. Request that they show their CRA authorization card (RC121A) to prove that they are a representative of the CRA (this card includes their name, photo and badge number)

    If you doubt the legitimacy of a visit from a CRA auditor, contact the CRA from the CRA's list of phone numbers and ask an agent to confirm the auditor's identity.

    When to provide the required documents

    If your books and records are available electronically, the CRA auditor may ask you to submit the documents online before or after meeting with you. This usually speeds up the audit process and leads to less time spent at your place of business.

    If electronic records are not available, or physical records are required, the CRA auditor will either:

    • Audit the documents at your place of business or representative’s office.
    • Borrow the documents that are required for further audit.
      What if the CRA borrows your books and records

      To borrow your books and records, the CRA auditor will:

      • Complete a T2213 receipt identifying the borrowed books, records and documents
      • Provide you with a copy of the receipt

      When the documents are returned, the CRA auditor will request that you sign the receipt acknowledging you received the books and records.

    What if you cannot get the documents

    If you cannot get the documents by the date requested, call the CRA auditor assigned to your case at the number provided in your letter to request additional time. The CRA auditor assigned to your case will determine if this is possible.

    If you no longer have the requested books and records, the CRA auditor can assist you in finding alternate methods to confirm the amounts reported on your slips and summaries. For example, you could obtain copies from a third party involved in a transaction (a financial institution or a supplier).

    After the deadline, if all the required books and records are not provided to the CRA auditor, they may send you a notice of a requirement to provide information. If you fail to comply with this notice, the CRA can seek a compliance order to enforce compliance.


    How to submit your documents online

    The CRA auditor will provide a reference number to use to securely send your documents using our online services.

    To send your documents using our online services:

    1. Click “Submit documents” in the Correspondence menu and then click “Submit documents” at the bottom of the page
    2. Click “Start” and answer “Yes” to the question “Do you have a case or reference number?”
    3. Select the letters that the reference number starts with in response to the question “What does your case or reference number start with?”
    4. Enter the reference number (numbers only) in the blank field provided and click “Next”. A box will appear for you to attach your files.
    5. Click “+ Attachments” to attach your documents and use the “Browse” button to choose the file(s) you want to attach from your device (if more than one file is being added, each must have a unique name)
    6. Enter a brief description of the file in the blank field “Provide a brief description of your document” and click “Upload file(s)”
    7. If you want to attach more documents, click “+Attachments” again and repeat steps 2-3 and when all your documents are added, click “Next”
    8. Review your submission and if everything is correct and click “Submit”

    Once completed, a confirmation screen will appear which includes a confirmation number and your reference number. You can use your reference number if you need to submit additional documents at a later date.

    Online or Video conference or by Phone

    The CRA auditor will examine your books and records electronically at their CRA office. The CRA auditor will not visit you at your place of business during this audit.

    When to provide the required documents

    The CRA auditor will provide you with a deadline to submit your documents online.


    After the deadline, if the required books and records are not provided to the CRA auditor, they will determine the next steps based on your case:

    • Arrange to audit your books and records at your place of business (in person audit)
    • Make corrections to your account based on the information the CRA auditor has available.

  4. Results of the audit

    Timeline: Generally, the results are given to you the same day if in person or within XX days if the results are sent by mail

    1. Once the CRA auditor has finished reviewing your books and records, they will discuss with you the results of the audit either in person or by video conference or by phone.

      You will receive a letter with the results of the audit. If non-compliance is found, the letter will include a summary of the non-compliance, proposed corrections and the next steps of the audit.

      Most common corrections following an audit

      The following are the most common compliance actions requiring corrections following an employer compliance audit (ECA):

    2. Not reporting payments made to self-employed workers using a T4A slip
    3. Improperly calculating or not reporting the taxable benefit for the employee's personal use of an employer-provided automobile
    4. Not reporting taxable automobile allowances
    5. Not reporting bonuses and commissions paid
    6. Not reporting taxable benefits for parking provided by the employer
    7. Not reporting shareholder benefits
    8. Not reporting the taxable benefit when security options received from a non-CCPC corporation are exercised
    9. Incorrectly treating workers as self-employed when they are actually employees
    Common changes to your T4 or T4A returns after an audit

    The following is a list of the most common changes to your T4 or T4A returns the CRA auditor can make once they finished reviewing your books and records:

    • Amend or add T4 slips

      The CRA auditor may amend your T4 slips to correct the reporting by increasing or reducing the amount reported on the slip.

      They may also add T4 slips when a slip was not originally filed or when the employment status has been determined to be different from how it was reported on the original slip.

      Changes are most commonly made to the following boxes or codes:

      • Total employment income (box 14)
      • Income tax deducted (box 22)
      • CPP contributions (box 16)
      • EI premiums (box 18)
      • Insurable and pensionable earnings (box 24 and box 26)
      • Taxable benefits (codes 30, 32, 33, 34, 36, 38, 40 or 90 )

    • Amend or add T4A slips

      The CRA auditor may amend your T4A slips to correct the reporting by increasing or reducing the amount reported on the slip.

      They may also add T4A slips when a slip was not originally filed or when the employment status has been determined to be different from how it was reported on the original slip.

      Changes are most commonly made to the following boxes or codes:

      • Self-employed commissions (box 020)
      • Income tax deducted (box 022)
      • Other income (code 028)

  5. If you agree to the proposed corrections, you can request that the CRA auditor process the corrections before the end of the 30 day period. The corrections will be processed by the CRA auditor and you will receive a final letter immediately.

    If you disagree with the proposed corrections, you have to respond within 30 days of the letter being received. You may have to provide a written explanation, including any additional information or documents for the CRA auditor to review.

    6. You will receive a final letter to inform you that the audit is complete with the summary of the non-compliance and the proposed corrections along with the following information:

    If corrections are required, the CRA auditor will process all final corrections as proposed because you either agreed to the proposed corrections or you did not respond within the 30 day period. The CRA auditor will also process these proposed corrections where you have provided additional information does not support a change to the audit results and proposed corrections.

    If the additional information you provide is sufficient to require a change in the audit result, the CRA auditor will revise the working papers and proposed corrections.

    The CRA auditor will provide the ECA Statement of Account (if CPP contributions, EI premiums, penalties or interest are assessed) to the legal entity and not to the authorized representative. Copies will only be provided to the authorized representative if you specifically request it.

    If you failed to report income or taxable benefits properly, you will also receive a copy of the following documents with the letter:

    • Additional or amended T4 or T4A slips (you must send copy 3 of all slips to the workers concerned, and keep copy 2 for your records)

    If you failed to deduct CPP contributions and EI premiums properly, you will also receive a copy of the following documents with the letter:

    • CPP and EI Summaries
    • Additional or amended T4 slips (you must send copy 3 of all slips to the workers concerned, and keep copy 2 for your records)

    If a CPP/EI Rulings request was made during the audit to determine the employment status of your workers, a ruling decision letter will be sent both to you and the worker separately.

    If you failed to report taxable benefits that are subject to GST/HST, you will also receive a GST/HST worksheet that calculates the amount required to be charged and taken into consideration. As a result of these corrections, you may need to change your previously filed GST/HST return or report it on your next return and it may be referred to the GST/HST section for further review.

    As a result of this audit, you may subsequently receive a letter or notice of reassessment for your T2 corporation income tax return.

    Paying a total amount owing

    As the employer compliance audit found you had provided taxable benefits requiring CPP contributions and EI premiums to be withheld that you did not remit by their due date, the total amount owing on the statement is past due and payable immediately.

    You are required to provide the payment in full for the balance owing to the CRA auditor once you receive your statements of account.

    If you are not able to pay the total amount owing, you may set up a payment arrangement with the CRA.

    Learn more: Debt collection at the CRA.
