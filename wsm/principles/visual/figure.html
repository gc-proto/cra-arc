Law of figure and ground

On this page

  • Overview
  • Examples
  • Take aways

Overview

Definition: We perceive objects as either figures (distinct elements of focus) or ground (background, foreground or landscape on which the figures rest).

In practice: A background colour is perceived as separate from the foreground text. It presents the user with more than one thing to note on the page. A background colour may add visual interest or a focal point to the page. It can also overload the user, so don't over-use it.

Examples

Correct use

  • The next example uses the law of figure and ground correctly, as the table header is given a different background colour than the table data.

Incorrect use

  • In the next example every single content element has a background colour which unnecessarily increases the cognitive load on the user.
  • In the left menu of the following page, both the first level of content and the third level have a white background, while the second level has a gray background. There is no logic to colour choices, and it is difficult to determine which is which, and what is the hierarchy.
  • The table in the example below applies colour to all the data cells which brings all numbers into focus. This approach could interfere with how a user may want to read this table, where only one or two data points might be of interest to them.
