Who can elect

You must meet the following criteria to file a valid election under section 216 of the Income Tax Act:

  1. You were a non-resident of Canada for all or part of 2024
    2. Are you a non-resident for tax purposes?

    Generally, you are a non-resident of Canada for tax purposes if you permanently live outside Canada and do not have any residential ties with Canada.

    For more information, see:

    • Income Tax Folio S5-F1-C1Determining an Individual’s Residence Status
    • Income Tax Folio S6-F1-C1, Residence of a Trust or Estate.
  2. You or a partnership which you were a member [rephease and add 'not a business']
  3. While you were a non-resident, one of the following applies to you:
    • Received rental income from real or immovable property in Canada
    • Received timber royalties on a timber resource property or timber limit in Canada
    • Disposed of rental property (Canadian real or immovable property, timber resource property, or timber limit) that you had previously claimed capital cost allowance (CCA) for on a Section 216 return and, as a result of the disposition, you have a recapture of that CCA
  4. You report all of your Canadian rental income, timber royalties and expenses together in one Section 216 return
    More than one Canadian rental property

    If you have rental income from more than one rental property in Canada and make an election under section 216, you must report all of your Canadian rental income and expenses together in one Section 216 return.

  5. File your Section 216 return by the due date [add link]
    6. What happens if you are late?

    If you do not send the CRA your Section 216 return by the due date, your election will be invalid. If the payer did not withhold the correct amount of non-resident tax from your rental income, the CRA will issue a non-resident tax assessment to you.

    If the CRA approved your Form NR6 for the year and you do not file your Section 216 return by the due date, you will be subject to non-resident tax on your gross rental income. If the correct amount of this tax was not withheld at source, the CRA will issue a non-resident tax assessment to your agent.

Disposing of your rental property

If you have disposed of or are planning to dispose of your Canadian rental property, go to Disposing of or acquiring certain Canadian property or see Information Circular IC72-17R6, Procedures Concerning the Disposition of Taxable Canadian Property by Non-Residents of Canada – Section 116, for the special rules that apply.

Disposing of your rental property

If you have disposed of or are planning to dispose of your Canadian rental property, go to Disposing of or acquiring certain Canadian property or see Information Circular IC72-17R6, Procedures Concerning the Disposition of Taxable Canadian Property by Non-Residents of Canada – Section 116, for the special rules that apply.

Note

If you owned or held a rental property in Canada for less than 365 consecutive days before it's disposition, it is generally considered to be a flipped property. The resulting profit on the disposition is taxable as business income and not as a capital gain, unless the property was already considered inventory if the disposition occurred due to, or in anticipation of, certain life events. For more information about flipped property and the life-event exceptions, see Guide T4037, Capital Gains.

Other Canadian-source income

If you have other Canadian-source income for 2024, you may have to send the CRA a separate Canadian return to report it.

If you received Canadian-source employment or business income or you realized a taxable capital gain from disposing of taxable Canadian property in 2024, see Guide T4058, Non-Residents and Income Tax.

As a non-resident of Canada, you can choose to file a separate Canadian tax return to report your rental income from real, immovable property and timber royalties in Canada by electing under .

If you were a non-resident of Canada for all or part of 2023 and, while a non-resident, one of the following situations applied to you:

  • You or a partnership which you were a member of received rental income from real or immovable property in Canada
  • You or a partnership which you were a member of received timber royalties on a timber resource property or timber limit in Canada
  • You or a partnership which you were a member of  disposed of rental property (Canadian real or immovable property, timber resource property, or timber limit) that you had previously claimed capital cost allowance (CCA) for on a Section 216 return and, as a result of the disposition, you have a recapture of that CCA

Eligible source income

You can only report the following Canadian-source income for the calendar year:

  • Rental income from all of your Canadian rental properties
  • Timber royalties

Amounts not to be included

Do not include the following on your section 216 return: 

  • Interest income
