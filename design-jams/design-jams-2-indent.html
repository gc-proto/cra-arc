Design Jams with Canada Revenue Agency

The Canada Revenue Agency holds design jams to help improve the services it offers.

Services and information

What are design jams

Design jams are workshops that bring together participants from various backgrounds, including applicants and taxpayers, to develop ideas that improve services.

Each design jam includes participants who have personal experience with the service challenge or have knowledge on the topic, from both inside and outside the CRA.

The design jams focus on specific service challenges that were selected from proposals submitted by our employees across the Agency, as well as the Taxpayers' Ombudsman.

What happens during a design jam

During the design jams, participants discuss challenges and answer questions such as:

  • What do clients need?
  • What difficulties do they face?
  • How can we make their experience better?

Participants then build mock-ups of their ideas and test them. The goal following a design jam is to use the insights gained from testing to improve services to see how they work for the people who use them.

The Design Jam Phases

Define

Use existing insights from research to define the main issue and create a "How might we" statement that clearly outlines the problem needed to be explored.

"How might we" statements help to identify and understand complex service challenges that are missing a solution.

Understand

Conduct interviews with experience and knowledgeable people to better understand their perspective and experience with the challenge.

Analyze the results to identify key insights and pain points.

Explore

In a one-day workshop, review the research and findings. Work together to explore potential solutions to the challenge.

Develop and refine the ideas proposed and create prototypes of your ideas.

Explore

Test the ideas developed in the workshop with people that would use the product or service, and adjust or improve as needed.

Based on the outcome of the testing, refine the ideas and retest until they test well. If they test well, implement the ideas for people to use.

Previous Design Jams

Simplifying the CRA Candidate Profile

Service Challenge

The Candidate Profile is the system that job candidates outside the Agency and CRA employees use when they want to apply for a position with us. We held a design jam to gain insight into how to make the tool easier to use.

Insights gained from the design jam
  • The Candidate Profile system is very generic. Individuals who use it to apply for jobs feel the experience isn’t personal.
  • When individuals start using the system, it is not clear what all the steps will be or how the process works. It is not clear why certain information is required.
  • There are acronyms and terminology in the system that are complicated.
  • There seems to be a lot of unnecessary steps and those steps have to be repeated each time an individual applies for a position.
Proposals developed during the design jam

During the workshop, participants came up with “quick fixes”, such as changing the wording in the system so that it is easier to understand and describing each step at the beginning so that candidates know what to expect. They also came up with longer-term solutions that can be worked on gradually, such as redesigning the log-in process to make it more simple.

Next steps

Explore implementation of design jam outcomes aligned with the work of the CRA’s Staffing Redesign Project

Design Jam 2

Service Challenge

The Disability Tax Credit (DTC) is a non-refundable tax credit that helps eligible persons with disabilities or their supporting persons reduce the amount of income tax they may have to pay. We held a design jam to better understand the DTC application process for applicants and medical practitioners and explored how it could be improved.

Insights gained from the design jam
  • It is not always clear who should be telling the applicants’ stories during the application process (individuals or medical practitioners), and how they should tell the stories. There needs to be a way to allow individuals to share details of their lives without putting pressure on medical practitioners.
  • All the different players in this process have a common goal but are trying to achieve it in different ways. Those involved need a better understanding of all of their roles so they can work together.
  • This design jam should only be one part of a series of efforts to improve the DTC.
Proposals developed during the design jam

During the Design Jam, participants came up with several ideas to help address the insights, such as:

  • creating an app that could act as a “one-stop” shop for accessing information and being assessed
  • creating a video-based web site that would deliver clearer information about eligibility for the credit, and
  • redesigning the main application form to be more collaborative and easy to use.

The CRA will explore the feasibility of these suggestions.

Next steps

Ideas from the design jam to be further explored are the redesign of the paper T2201 DTC certificate application form and the creation of a digital online application.

Enhancing Scientific Research & Experimental Development (SR&ED) claim process

Service Challenge

The Scientific Research & Experimental Development (SR&ED) Program encourages Canadian businesses of all sizes, and in all industries, to conduct research and development. This may lead to scientific and technological advancements, which would contribute to Canada’s innovation economy.

The SR&ED design jam explored how the claim process could be made easier for both applicants (e.g. businesses) and tax professionals.

Insights gained from the design jam

During the design jam, participants had the following insights:

  • Applicants sometimes feel like they are being audited, instead of participating in a tax incentive program. Sometimes companies are nervous to have CRA employees in their building because they are trying to follow rules that they don’t fully understand.
  • Technology is changing rapidly and the definitions about what is "innovative" change quickly.
  • The application process takes a lot of time and sometimes companies wonder if the benefits outweigh the costs of participating.
Proposals developed during the design jam

Participants proposed a variety of solutions, including:

  • developing an Online SR&ED Community Portal
  • introducing a new way of gathering information for SR&ED projects
  • updated custom questions for different industry sectors, and
  • a new tool that would provide applicants with immediate feedback on the status of their projects.
Next steps

Feedback received through the Design Jam process have provided the CRA with a deeper understanding of the client’s experience in making a SR&ED claim. The insights expressed from the claimant and tax preparer communities will also inform the future direction of the SR&ED tax incentive program.

Moving forward, the CRA has committed to developing solutions based on the results of the Design Jam that will further improve client experience with the program, while communicating our progress to program stakeholders at the earliest possible opportunities.

Upcoming Design Jams

Helping individuals with modest income and self-employment earnings file taxes

Service Challenge

The Community Volunteer Income Tax Program (CVITP) is a collaboration between the CRA and community organizations. These organizations across Canada host free tax preparation clinics and arrange for volunteers to prepare income tax and benefit returns for eligible individuals.

To be eligible, individuals must have a modest income and a simple tax situation. Under current guidelines, individuals with self-employment earnings are not eligible, even if they meet the other criteria.

Although the guidelines make these clients ineligible, these types of clients are seeking support and the organizations and volunteers are eager to help them. This design jam will look into how we can support individuals with modest income and self-employment earnings to file their taxes successfully.

Improving the Canada Child Benefit (CCB) Review Process

Service Challenge

The Canada Child Benefit (CCB) is available to eligible Canadians who have a child under 18 years of age. Eligibility is based on family net income, as reported in the previous year’s tax return, and other factors. Sometimes individuals have to go through a review process, sometimes long after they have already received the benefit. We have heard, through different sources, that some Canadians find this review process difficult.

This design jam will look into the barriers that Canadians experience when they are going through the review, and will explore ways to improve the process.
